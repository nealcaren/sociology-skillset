# Phase 3: Revision & Quality Check

You are reviewing and refining the draft to meet publication standards. This phase catches formulaic writing, ensures analytical rigor, and verifies that the text reads as argument rather than transcript.

## Why This Phase Matters

First drafts often display evidence without arguing with it. Common problems include mechanical quote-analysis-quote patterns, hedging empirical claims, and letting quotes carry analytic weight. This phase transforms competent drafts into compelling arguments.

## Revision Priorities

Work through these checks in order. Earlier issues compound later ones.

---

### 1. Argument Structure

**Check the Roadmap**:
- [ ] Does the opening paragraph summarize the entire argument before showing evidence?
- [ ] Are all mechanisms/processes named upfront?
- [ ] Does the reader know where the section is going?

**Check Each Subsection**:
- [ ] Does it advance one clear analytic claim?
- [ ] Is that claim stated in the first 2-4 sentences (before any quotes)?
- [ ] Does the heading name a mechanism or process (not a topic)?
- [ ] Does the close connect forward to the next section?

**Red flags**:
- Opening with "We interviewed X people..." or "This section presents..."
- Subsections that list themes rather than advance arguments
- Headings like "Findings," "Race," "Gender," or "What Participants Said"

---

### 2. Evidence Strategy and Depth Variation

**Across the full findings section, verify**:
- [ ] Not every subsection uses the same evidence strategy (avoid monotonous pacing)
- [ ] At least one subsection goes deep on an individual case
- [ ] At least one subsection demonstrates breadth across multiple voices
- [ ] Quote lengths vary across subsections (not all block quotes, not all embedded)

**For each subsection, verify the chosen strategy is executed well**:

If **anchor-echo**: One deep vignette (2-3 paragraphs), social location established, extended quote, then 2-3 echo quotes showing prevalence.

If **convergence**: 3-5 participants quoted briefly, each adding an angle, with analytic bridges between voices and a synthesis at the end.

If **extended case**: One participant sustained across most of the subsection, multiple quotes interleaved with narration, theoretical interpretation at the close.

If **polyphonic**: Many brief voices cycled through efficiently, cumulative weight building toward an interpretive conclusion.

**Red flags**:
- All subsections use identical structure (repetitive pacing)
- All quotes treated equally (no depth variation across the findings)
- Prevalence never established for major claims
- Participant introduced only by name, not situation

---

### 3. Quote Integration

**Check every quote**:
- [ ] Is it introduced with analytic framing (not just "X said")?
- [ ] Is it followed by explicit interpretation?
- [ ] Does the reader know what to listen for before it appears?
- [ ] Are consecutive quotes separated by analytical sentences?

**Red flags**:
- Quotes stacked back-to-back
- Neutral attributions: "Maria said...", "According to Steve..."
- Quotes that "speak for themselves" (no interpretation follows)
- Quotes introducing analytic novelty (the claim appears after, not before)

**The hamburger test**: If every quote follows the pattern [brief context] → [quote] → [analysis sentence], the prose is too formulaic. Look for:
- Embedded phrases woven into author sentences
- Anticipatory framing where the claim precedes the quote
- Varied quote lengths matched to purpose
- Pivot phrases elevated to conceptual anchors

---

### 4. Voice and Confidence

**Authorial voice should dominate**:
- [ ] Does the author's voice outweigh participant voice overall?
- [ ] Are causal claims made in sociological language (not respondent language)?
- [ ] Are mechanisms named by the author, not discovered in quotes?

**Check for inappropriate hedging**:
- [ ] Empirical patterns stated confidently ("This pattern emerged...") not tentatively ("This might suggest...")
- [ ] Theoretical scope hedged appropriately, but not empirical observations
- [ ] Confidence comes from patterning, not sample size apologetics

**Red flags**:
- "This may indicate..."
- "It could be interpreted as..."
- "Participants seemed to feel..."
- Over-reliance on participant voice for analytical claims

---

### 5. Variation Handling

**Check sequencing**:
- [ ] Is baseline pattern established before variation introduced?
- [ ] Is variation signaled explicitly ("However...", "In contrast...")?
- [ ] Is the axis of difference named (race, class, context)?
- [ ] Is variation explained (why it occurs), not just noted?

**Red flags**:
- Opening with difference ("Some said X while others said Y")
- Variation as exception dump (list of outliers)
- No explanation for why variation occurs

---

### 6. Scope and Prevalence

**Check that breadth is established for each major claim** through at least one of:
- [ ] Prevalence language ("most," "nearly all," "across both sites")
- [ ] Counts ("twelve of our 24 respondents," "N = 19")
- [ ] Group-based framing ("middle-class parents uniformly...", "working-class families, by contrast...")
- [ ] Implicit breadth through convergence or polyphonic strategies (many voices demonstrate scope)

**Red flags**:
- A major claim rests on a single quote with no indication of breadth
- Vague quantifiers used throughout ("some," "many," "few") — pick a strategy
- Numbers implying statistical representativeness
- Prevalence never addressed for the central argument

---

### 7. Methods Section (if included)

- [ ] Population explicitly defined (not vague)
- [ ] Sampling strategy named and justified
- [ ] Sample size justified through legitimate strategy
- [ ] Interview details specified (format, duration, topics)
- [ ] Analysis approach described
- [ ] Positionality addressed (when appropriate)

---

## Common Problems and Solutions

| Problem | Symptom | Solution |
|---------|---------|----------|
| **Formulaic structure** | Every subsection has identical deep-case-then-prevalence pattern | Vary evidence strategy across subsections: anchor-echo, convergence, extended case, polyphonic |
| **Quotes speaking for themselves** | Quote followed by transition, not interpretation | Add 1-2 sentences naming what the quote shows |
| **No depth variation** | All cases treated equally briefly | Pick one exemplary case in at least one subsection and go deep (2-3 paragraphs) |
| **Stacked quotes** | Quote immediately follows quote | Insert analytical sentences between |
| **Weak attribution** | "Maria said..." | "Maria, a [demographics] in [situation], explained..." |
| **Descriptive headings** | "Race," "Findings," "Results" | Name the mechanism: "Racialized Pathways to..." |
| **Starting with variation** | "Some said X, others said Y" | Establish baseline first, then stratify |
| **Hedged empirics** | "This might suggest..." | "This pattern shows..." (hedge theory, not data) |
| **Topic headings** | "What Workers Said About Pay" | "Wage Theft as Normalized Exploitation" |
| **No roadmap** | Jumps straight into first subsection | Add 1-2 paragraph overview naming all mechanisms |
| **No deviant case treatment** | Outliers buried in hedges or ignored | Consider a dedicated subsection when exceptions are analytically revealing |

---

## Prohibited Moves Checklist

Verify the draft does **not**:

- [ ] Start any subsection with a quote
- [ ] List themes without argument
- [ ] Use quotes without interpretation
- [ ] Stack quotes back-to-back
- [ ] Hedge empirical patterns
- [ ] Use purely descriptive subheadings
- [ ] Let quotes introduce analytic novelty
- [ ] Treat all quotes with equal depth
- [ ] Start with variation before baseline
- [ ] Use neutral attribution when analytic framing is possible

---

## Quality Indicators

The final draft should demonstrate:

- **Argument over display**: Each subsection advances a claim, not just shows data
- **Analytical confidence**: Patterns stated assertively, mechanisms named clearly
- **Narrative craft**: Varied evidence strategies across subsections, varied quote integration, smooth transitions
- **Grounded abstraction**: Sociological concepts tied to concrete evidence
- **Strategic depth**: Evidence depth varies by purpose — not every subsection uses the same approach

---

## Output

Provide:
- Revised draft with tracked changes or highlighted revisions, OR
- A revision memo listing specific changes needed, organized by section

Include a **quality memo** with:
1. Strengths of the current draft
2. Key revisions made or recommended
3. Remaining issues requiring author attention
4. Assessment against the five quality indicators above

## When You're Done

Return a summary to the orchestrator with:
1. Key revisions made
2. Remaining issues or questions for the user
3. Overall assessment of draft quality
4. Suggested next steps
