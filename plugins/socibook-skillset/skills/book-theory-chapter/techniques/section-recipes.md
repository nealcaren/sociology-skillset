# Section-by-Section Drafting Recipes for Theory Chapters

Detailed instructions for drafting each section of a theory chapter in political sociology and American politics monographs. Each recipe specifies what the section accomplishes, its internal paragraph structure, word count targets, the paragraph functions it deploys, and real examples from the corpus.

Use this file alongside `paragraph-functions.md` for paragraph-level guidance. This file tells you how the paragraphs combine into sections, and how the sections combine into a complete theory chapter.

---

## Overview: What a Theory Chapter Does

A theory chapter is the book's engine room. It builds the original conceptual framework that the empirical chapters will apply, test, or illustrate. Unlike an introduction (which sells the project) or an empirical chapter (which delivers evidence), the theory chapter must accomplish three things:

1. **Clear the ground** -- show what existing approaches get right and where they fall short.
2. **Build the framework** -- define concepts, specify mechanisms, state predictions.
3. **Create portable tools** -- give the reader hypotheses, typologies, or vocabulary they can carry through the rest of the book.

Target length: **6,000-12,000 words** (corpus range: Patashnik Ch. 2 at ~8,000 words; Bolton & Thrower Ch. 2 at ~11,000 words; Lichterman Ch. 1 at ~12,000 words; Lowande Ch. 2 at ~10,000 words; Tesler Chs. 1-2 combined at ~14,000 words; Smith & King Ch. 2 at ~13,000 words).

Theory chapters are typically 8-12% of total book length. They are shorter than the longest empirical chapters but denser in conceptual content per paragraph.

## Section 1: Opening Frame

**Target length:** 500-1,200 words | **Paragraphs:** 2-5

### What This Section Accomplishes

The opening frame establishes what the chapter will build and why the reader needs it. It connects backward to the puzzle established in the introduction and forward to the framework the chapter will construct. It gives the reader a reason to endure the abstraction that follows.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

- EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE (in Deductive-Formal and Realist-Strategic clusters)
- CONCEPT_DEFINE (in Historical-Institutionalist cluster)
- LIT_POSITION (in Interpretive-Conceptual cluster)

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Anecdote Opening (Deductive-Formal)

Bolton & Thrower (2022, Ch. 2) open with the Facebook hearing anecdote:

> "Amid public controversies surrounding Facebook's role in propagating misinformation from Russian intelligence during the 2016 election, US senators convened a 2018 joint hearing of the Judiciary and the Commerce, Science, and Transportation Committees to probe the company's data policies and practices. While Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg certainly received his fair share of ridicule after the hearing, reports did not cast senators in a particularly flattering light either."

The anecdote does triple duty: it illustrates low legislative capacity, it is recognizable and vivid, and it creates a question (if senators cannot understand Facebook, how can they constrain executive power?). The chapter then transitions to the framework in the second paragraph.

#### Conceptual Opening (Historical-Institutionalist)

Patashnik (2008, Ch. 2) opens by defining the chapter's task:

> "The next five chapters provide a detailed examination of reform experiences in key arenas of American national government. To lay the theoretical foundation for this analysis, this chapter discusses the nature of general-interest reform as a political project."

This is the most efficient opening in the corpus -- two sentences that tell the reader exactly what the chapter will do and why it precedes the case chapters. There is no anecdote, no scene-setting, no throat-clearing.

#### Epigraph + Anecdote Opening (Behavioral-Spillover)

Tesler (2016, Ch. 2) opens with a quoted epigraph from Al Sharpton about the New York Post chimpanzee cartoon, followed by a narrative of the incident:

> "On February 16, 2009, the police were called to the home of a Connecticut woman whose pet chimpanzee, Travis, viciously mauled her friend."

The anecdote serves as a condensed version of the book's mechanism: a non-racial event (a chimpanzee attack) becomes racially charged because of Obama's presidency. The chapter then moves from anecdote to theory.

#### Problem-First Opening (Realist-Strategic)

Lowande (2024, Ch. 2) opens with the Thanksgiving turkey pardon -- a deliberately absurd example that defamiliarizes the presidency:

> "Every year around the last Thursday in November, the president gathers supporters, staff, and press to pardon a Thanksgiving turkey. ... Most people do not know these turkeys are bred to be slaughtered. They have terminal health conditions because of their enormous size. All die within months of being pardoned."

The turkey pardon is the book's argument in miniature: a symbolic act that appears consequential but changes nothing. The chapter then asks: "We like to think presidents care most about what the government they lead actually accomplishes. ... But that is not all they are."

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Opening type | Length | Notes |
|---------|-------------|--------|-------|
| Deductive-Formal | Anecdote illustrating the IV | 500-800 words | Transitions quickly to formal framework |
| Interpretive-Conceptual | Opening question or literature critique | 800-1,200 words | May begin with a direct challenge to the dominant approach |
| Historical-Institutionalist | Task statement + concept definition | 400-600 words | Shortest opening; the chapter gets to work immediately |
| Behavioral-Spillover | Epigraph + narrative anecdote | 600-1,000 words | The anecdote *is* the mechanism in compressed form |
| Realist-Strategic | Defamiliarizing example | 600-1,000 words | Uses absurdity or irony to unsettle the reader's assumptions |

## Section 2: Literature Engagement

**Target length:** 1,500-4,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 5-12

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section reviews existing scholarship not as a comprehensive survey but as an argument. Its purpose is to show that the existing frameworks, however valuable, cannot fully account for the phenomenon the book studies. Each paragraph engages a body of work, concedes its contributions, and identifies what it leaves unexplained. The section ends by naming the specific gap the book's framework fills.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

- LIT_POSITION (dominant)
- LIT_GAP (typically 1-2 paragraphs, usually at the end of the section)
- CONCEPT_DEFINE (when existing concepts are being repurposed or rejected)

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Compressed Engagement (Deductive-Formal)

Bolton & Thrower (2022, Ch. 2) handle the literature in approximately 1,500 words. They name the principal-agent framework, acknowledge its contributions, and then specify where it falls short:

> "We are hardly the first to highlight how legislatures use various tools to curb executive powers. Since the introduction of the new economics of organization literature into the discipline of political science, scholars have often conceived of legislative-executive interactions in the form of a principal-agent problem."

The engagement is functional: it establishes the conversation the book joins without belaboring it. The gap paragraph is equally compressed:

> "Previous studies mainly contend legislatures only engage in constraining activities when they do not share the policy goals of the executive. Consequently, we have little indication as to whether or why legislatures curb executive power when they ostensibly share these goals, notably during unified government."

#### Extended Critique (Interpretive-Conceptual)

Lichterman (2021, Ch. 1) devotes approximately 4,000 words -- nearly a third of the chapter -- to critiquing the "entrepreneurial actor" model. The engagement is organized around two subproblems:

> "Entrepreneurial claims making: the missing 'how'" and "Entrepreneurial relationship building: again, the missing 'how.'"

Each subproblem gets its own subsection. The critique proceeds by examining what the entrepreneurial model would predict, confronting those predictions with the ethnographic puzzles from the introduction, and showing where the model's predictions fail. The gap is not a missing fact but a missing question:

> "The problem is that we need room to imagine different ways of relating to intentions and goals, deciding what counts as a goal, and what counts as winning."

#### Positioning Against Multiple Streams (Historical-Institutionalist)

Patashnik (2008, Ch. 2) positions against seven prior studies in a single long paragraph, naming each and specifying the exact point of departure:

> "In providing a detailed examination of how the rules, incentives, and norms embedded in reforms channel and constrain political dynamics over extended periods of time, my analysis departs from Maltzman and Shipan's important quantitative study of legal durability."

He then differentiates from Campbell and Mettler (positive feedback), Moe and Skowronek (administrative structure), Berry, Burden, and Howell (enacting coalitions), Hacker (policy drift), and Baumgartner and Jones (punctuated equilibrium). Each differentiation is one or two sentences.

#### Building on Prior Mechanisms (Behavioral-Spillover)

Tesler (2016, Ch. 2) uses the literature engagement section to *construct* his hypothesis from prior findings rather than to critique them:

> "Prior research suggests that this racialization of issues with no manifest racial content results from mass communications that heighten the association -- either consciously or unconsciously -- between African Americans and political evaluations."

He then inventories five prior findings (evangelical support for the Iraq War, gendered health care opinions, race-of-source experiments, Catholic attitudes and JFK, Jesse Jackson's 1984 campaign) that collectively establish the mechanism his hypothesis extends. The gap is that no one has applied this mechanism to an entire presidency:

> "If, as these studies suggest, the salient social background characteristics of elite sources have the potential to activate considerations in the realms of race, religion, and gender, then President Obama's most visible political positions should have been especially ripe for racialization."

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Length | Tone | Organization |
|---------|--------|------|-------------|
| Deductive-Formal | 1,500-2,000 | Functional, brisk | Single subsection; compressed citations |
| Interpretive-Conceptual | 3,000-4,000 | Critical, argumentative | Multiple subsections organized by sub-problem |
| Historical-Institutionalist | 1,500-2,500 | Positioning, differentiation-heavy | One dense paragraph listing departures |
| Behavioral-Spillover | 2,000-3,000 | Constructive; builds hypothesis from prior findings | Literature serves as building blocks, not targets |
| Realist-Strategic | 1,500-2,500 | Appreciative but redirecting | Acknowledges good work, then adds neglected dimension |

## Section 3: Core Framework Development

**Target length:** 2,500-5,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 8-15

### What This Section Accomplishes

This is the central section of the theory chapter. It builds the original framework by defining key concepts, specifying causal mechanisms, and stating the logic that connects the independent and dependent variables. Everything before this section clears the ground; everything after crystallizes the results. This section does the building.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

- CONCEPT_DEFINE (primary concepts, if not already defined)
- MECHANISM_SPECIFY (dominant)
- SCOPE_CONDITION
- EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE (interspersed to make mechanisms concrete)

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Worked Example: Patashnik (2008, Ch. 2)

Patashnik builds the framework through three sequential mechanisms, each getting its own subsection:

**Political Structures** (~800 words): Four types of institutional shifts that promote reform durability -- strengthening governing capacities, razing policy subsystems, raising transaction costs, and shifting venues. Each is defined in one or two sentences, then illustrated briefly.

**Creative Destruction** (~600 words): The Schumpeterian mechanism applied to policy reform. Market forces, not political actors, may deliver the final verdict on reform sustainability.

**Policy Feedback** (~1,200 words): The longest subsection. Four types of interest-group feedback are specified: creating constituencies, increasing/decreasing cohesion, altering cognitive mindsets, and tarnishing public images. Each type is a paragraph.

The section culminates in the 2x2 matrix (TYPOLOGY_PRESENT) that combines group identity stability with investment levels to predict four post-reform trajectories.

This demonstrates the core pattern of the Historical-Institutionalist cluster: *inventory mechanisms, then synthesize them into a typology*.

#### Worked Example: Bolton & Thrower (2022, Ch. 2)

Bolton & Thrower build the framework through a different logic: first define two domains of capacity, then trace how each operates through ex ante and ex post mechanisms:

> "With respect to the former, these legislatures can reduce statutory discretion -- that is, the leeway afforded to the executive branch in implementing the law. ... They can likewise impose ex post constraints on the executive branch for using discretion in ways that run counter to legislative preferences."

They then add a motivational argument: legislators constrain executives even during unified government because of reelection incentives and institutional pride:

> "Reelection-seeking members of Congress crave activities to publicize their value to constituents. Executive branch oversight may offer them the opportunity to garner favorable publicity in their districts or states, even if they mostly share the executive's policy goals."

This is the section's key move: the argument that capacity matters even when ideology aligns. It generates the conditional hypotheses.

This demonstrates the core pattern of the Deductive-Formal cluster: *define concepts precisely, trace mechanisms, then derive conditional predictions*.

#### Worked Example: Lowande (2024, Ch. 2)

Lowande builds the framework by distinguishing two dimensions of executive action:

> "Presidents' key decisions are to allocate effort and resources on two dimensions: a symbolic dimension, which is defined as the degree to which an action clarifies the position of the president, and a substantive one, which is the degree to which an action alters the doings of government."

He then works through the costs and benefits of each dimension:

> "The symbolic content of an action mostly impacts the president's popularity. ... The payoff for substance is different. Having a real impact on the status quo demands more than announcement."

This is followed by the typology of symbolic actions (think pieces, non sequiturs, basket bets), each illustrated with concrete presidential examples.

This demonstrates the core pattern of the Realist-Strategic cluster: *redefine the variable, decompose it into dimensions, then show what each dimension implies*.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Length | Structure | Central device |
|---------|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Deductive-Formal | 2,500-3,500 | Concept definitions --> mechanisms --> motivation | Conditional logic (when X and Y, then Z) |
| Interpretive-Conceptual | 3,500-5,000 | New vocabulary developed through extended examples | Conceptual contrast (what the new framework sees that the old one cannot) |
| Historical-Institutionalist | 3,000-4,000 | Sequential mechanism inventory --> typology | 2x2 matrix or equivalent classification |
| Behavioral-Spillover | 2,500-3,000 | Single mechanism stated, then bounded by conditions | Named hypothesis + scope conditions |
| Realist-Strategic | 3,000-4,000 | Two-dimensional decomposition --> typology --> cost-benefit analysis | Named types of the phenomenon |

## Section 4: Scaffolding Device

**Target length:** 800-2,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 3-6

### What This Section Accomplishes

The scaffolding device is the chapter's most portable product: a hypothesis list, a typology, a matrix, a table, or a named set of conditions. It is what the reader will remember, cite, and carry through the empirical chapters. Not all theory chapters have a visually distinct scaffolding section (in some clusters, the device is embedded in the framework development section), but all successful theory chapters produce at least one portable tool.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

- HYPOTHESIS_STATE (in Deductive-Formal, Behavioral-Spillover, and Realist-Strategic)
- TYPOLOGY_PRESENT (in Historical-Institutionalist and Realist-Strategic)
- SCOPE_CONDITION (in Behavioral-Spillover)
- FRAMEWORK_CRYSTALLIZE (often the final paragraph of this section)

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Numbered Hypotheses (Deductive-Formal)

Bolton & Thrower (2022, Ch. 2) present four hypotheses, each with precise conditional logic. Hypotheses 1-2 address legislative behavior; Hypotheses 3-4 address executive behavior. The parallel structure (two about constraint, two about unilateralism) mirrors the chapter's two-part argument:

> "Hypothesis 1: When legislatures have high levels of both policymaking and resource capacities, they place more overall constraints on the executive branch relative to when either is lacking."

> "Hypothesis 4: When legislatures have high levels of both policymaking and resource capacities, executives issue fewer unilateral actions during interbranch ideological conflict, but more unilateral actions during interbranch ideological conflict when either type of capacity is low."

The hypotheses are each set apart as block text, making them visually scannable. A reader can skim the chapter and capture the theory from the hypotheses alone.

#### 2x2 Matrix (Historical-Institutionalist)

Patashnik (2008, Ch. 2) presents Table 2-2 ("Varieties of Interest-Group Feedback") and Figure 2-1 (the post-reform dynamics matrix). The figure cross-tabulates group investment levels with group identity stability to generate four outcomes:

| | Stable identities | Fluid identities |
|---|---|---|
| **Modest investments** | Reversal ("The Empire Strikes Back") | Erosion ("Death by a thousand cuts") |
| **Extensive investments** | Entrenchment ("Dug in") | Reconfiguration ("Whole new ball game") |

The nicknames are the scaffolding's secret weapon. "Empire Strikes Back" is more memorable than "reversal of reform under conditions of stable group identities and modest group investments."

#### Named Conditions (Behavioral-Spillover)

Tesler (2016, Ch. 2) produces a numbered list of four scope conditions rather than formal hypotheses:

> "(1) issues on which Barack Obama takes a visible position that is reinforced over time, (2) issues on which Barack Obama's position is perceived as benefiting blacks, (3) political evaluations in which the pre-Obama effects of racial attitudes were low relative to the impact of party and ideology, and (4) political evaluations that were not already crystallized prior to Obama's presidency."

Each condition is then tested in specific empirical chapters, creating a one-to-one mapping between the theory chapter and the book's empirical structure.

#### Named Types (Realist-Strategic)

Lowande (2024, Ch. 2) produces three named types of symbolic executive action: think pieces, non sequiturs, and basket bets. Each name is evocative and self-explanatory. The typology is accompanied by italicized hypotheses linking each conditioning variable to the symbolic/substantive ratio:

> "*As the president's discretion decreases, their actions should become less substantive and more symbolic.*"

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Scaffolding type | Visual form | Portability |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|-------------|
| Deductive-Formal | Numbered hypotheses | Block text or numbered list | Each hypothesis maps to an empirical chapter |
| Interpretive-Conceptual | New vocabulary set | Prose definitions, sometimes a summary table | The vocabulary is the framework |
| Historical-Institutionalist | Matrix or typology | Figure or table with memorable cell labels | Readers cite the matrix by name |
| Behavioral-Spillover | Named hypothesis + numbered scope conditions | Prose with numbered list | The conditions generate the chapter structure |
| Realist-Strategic | Named types + directional hypotheses | Italicized propositions interspersed with typology | The type names become terms of art |

## Section 5: Empirical Preview

**Target length:** 300-800 words | **Paragraphs:** 1-3

### What This Section Accomplishes

The empirical preview connects the abstract framework to the concrete evidence that follows. It tells the reader what to expect from the empirical chapters without delivering the findings. It creates appetite and provides a navigational map.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

- EMPIRICAL_PREVIEW (dominant)

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Chapter-by-Chapter Preview

Bolton & Thrower (2022, Ch. 2) map their hypotheses onto specific chapters:

> "In the chapters to follow, we empirically evaluate the theory presented here. We test the hypotheses related to legislative constraints by examining statutory discretion and oversight. We then consider executive power by reviewing both presidential and gubernatorial executive orders in separate chapters. All throughout, we provide distinct measures of resource and policymaking capacities to test the interactive relationships posited in this theory."

Three sentences. Each names the dependent variable (discretion, oversight, executive orders) and the empirical strategy (federal + state variation). The preview is efficient because the hypotheses did the heavy lifting.

#### Variable-List Preview

Tesler (2016, Ch. 2) previews the empirical strategy by listing the specific measurement approach:

> "The statistical models used in the analyses that follow help separate the effects of racial attitudes from the impact of such factors as partisanship and ideology. These regression models, which are standard procedures in political science analyses, estimate what the relationship between racial attitudes would be if racial liberals were not more likely to be liberal Democrats."

The preview doubles as a methods justification: the reader is told not just what is coming but how the evidence will be evaluated.

#### Case-Naming Preview

Patashnik (2008, Ch. 2) names the cases and the chapter structure:

> "The next five chapters provide a detailed examination of reform experiences in key arenas of American national government."

This is the shortest preview in the corpus -- one sentence -- because the chapter's typology already tells the reader what to expect. The cases will populate the matrix.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Preview length | Content |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| Deductive-Formal | 300-500 words | Hypothesis-to-chapter mapping |
| Interpretive-Conceptual | 400-800 words | Methods discussion + case introduction |
| Historical-Institutionalist | 100-300 words | Brief case listing (typology does the work) |
| Behavioral-Spillover | 400-800 words | Measurement strategy + data sources |
| Realist-Strategic | 300-600 words | Chapter-by-chapter roadmap |

## Section 6: Closing Crystallization

**Target length:** 300-600 words | **Paragraphs:** 1-3

### What This Section Accomplishes

The closing crystallization is the chapter's send-off. It restates the framework in its most compressed form, reminds the reader what portable tool they now possess, and turns the page toward the empirical chapters. It is not a summary (which would rehash the chapter) but a distillation (which compresses it).

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

- FRAMEWORK_CRYSTALLIZE (dominant)
- EMPIRICAL_PREVIEW (if not already present in a separate section)

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Single-Sentence Crystallization

Lowande (2024, Ch. 2) closes with extreme compression:

> "Some executive actions will fail to move policy, while creating the impression that it does."

This sentence, which could serve as the book's subtitle, is the theory chapter's final word. Everything the reader needs to carry forward is here.

#### Conditional-Logic Crystallization

Bolton & Thrower (2022, Ch. 2) close by restating the interactive theory and turning toward the empirical chapters:

> "Overall, when legislatures lack sufficient capacity, executive actors are empowered to act in ways that may make legislative majorities worse off. Once their capacity increases, legislators can constrain executive activities through ex ante and ex post means, both of which are necessary for inducing executive restraint in unilateralism. Correspondingly, executives' power to bypass legislatures should be weakened overall and even more so when facing high-capacity ideologically-opposed majorities."

The crystallization preserves the conditional structure. It is not "capacity matters" (too vague) but "when capacity is low, executives are empowered; when capacity is high, they are constrained, especially when preferences diverge."

#### Dynamic-Process Crystallization

Patashnik (2008, Ch. 2) crystallizes the framework as a process rather than a proposition:

> "The main lesson is that reform outcomes are never completely settled. Policy reform must be seen as a dynamic process in which forces seeking to protect a reform may be opposed by forces seeking to undo it."

The word "dynamic" is the crystallization's core -- it tells the reader to expect *processes*, not equilibria, in the empirical chapters.

#### Vocabulary-Summary Crystallization

Smith & King (2024, Ch. 2) close by restating their modified framework with its key distinctions:

> "Our modified institutional orders framework, then, embraces more fully than ever the conception of human societies as interinstitutional systems with subsystems of institutional orders, while insisting that all institutional orders contain at least some state institutions; that those state institutions most often should not be conceived as unitary actors comprising a single, distinct institutional order; and that those policy alliances that do not control any state institutions are nonetheless often important subjects of institutionalist analyses."

The crystallization is dense by design -- it is a compressed restatement of three claims that the reader needs to hold simultaneously.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Crystallization style | Length |
|---------|----------------------|--------|
| Deductive-Formal | Conditional if-then restatement | 300-500 words |
| Interpretive-Conceptual | Vocabulary summary with key distinctions | 400-600 words |
| Historical-Institutionalist | Dynamic process statement | 200-400 words |
| Behavioral-Spillover | Hypothesis restatement with scope conditions | 300-500 words |
| Realist-Strategic | Single-sentence compression | 200-400 words |

## Complete Chapter Recipes by Cluster

### Recipe 1: Deductive-Formal (Bolton & Thrower model)

**Total target:** 8,000-11,000 words

| Section | Word Count | Key Functions | Notes |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| 1. Opening Frame | 600 | EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE | Open with a vivid anecdote (Facebook hearing) that makes the DV tangible |
| 2. Literature Engagement | 1,500 | LIT_POSITION, LIT_GAP | Brief, functional; establish principal-agent framework and its limits |
| 3. Core Framework | 3,500 | CONCEPT_DEFINE x2, MECHANISM_SPECIFY x4, SCOPE_CONDITION x2 | Define two domains of capacity; trace ex ante and ex post mechanisms; add motivational argument for unified government |
| 4. Scaffolding Device | 1,200 | HYPOTHESIS_STATE x4 | Four numbered hypotheses; each specifies both positive and null conditions |
| 5. Empirical Preview | 400 | EMPIRICAL_PREVIEW | Map hypotheses to specific empirical chapters |
| 6. Closing Crystallization | 400 | FRAMEWORK_CRYSTALLIZE | Restate the interactive theory in conditional form |

**Subsection count:** 6-8 named subsections

**Distinguishing feature:** The hypotheses are the chapter's culmination. Everything before them is setup; everything after is transition. The chapter reads like a funnel narrowing from concepts to predictions.

### Recipe 2: Interpretive-Conceptual (Lichterman model)

**Total target:** 10,000-13,000 words

| Section | Word Count | Key Functions | Notes |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| 1. Opening Frame | 1,000 | LIT_POSITION (opening challenge to dominant approach) | Begin by naming the problem with existing scholarship |
| 2. Literature Engagement | 4,000 | LIT_POSITION x6-8, LIT_GAP x2 | Extended critique organized by sub-problem ("the missing 'how' of claims making," "the missing 'how' of relationship building") |
| 3. Core Framework | 4,500 | CONCEPT_DEFINE x3-4, EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE x4-5, MECHANISM_SPECIFY x2 | Build new vocabulary through contrast with old; ground concepts in ethnographic vignettes |
| 4. Scaffolding Device | 800 | CONCEPT_DEFINE (summary), TYPOLOGY_PRESENT (optional) | The vocabulary *is* the scaffolding; may include a summary table of concepts |
| 5. Empirical Preview | 600 | EMPIRICAL_PREVIEW | Describe the ethnographic or comparative strategy |
| 6. Closing Crystallization | 500 | FRAMEWORK_CRYSTALLIZE | Restate the new vocabulary as an integrated framework |

**Subsection count:** 8-12 named subsections (often organized around sub-critiques)

**Distinguishing feature:** The literature engagement section is the longest section in the chapter, sometimes longer than the framework development. The chapter's contribution is as much about what it clears away as what it builds. No formal hypotheses; the portable tool is the new conceptual vocabulary.

### Recipe 3: Historical-Institutionalist (Patashnik model)

**Total target:** 7,000-9,000 words

| Section | Word Count | Key Functions | Notes |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| 1. Opening Frame | 400 | CONCEPT_DEFINE | Define "general-interest reform" and the chapter's task in 2-3 paragraphs |
| 2. Literature Engagement | 1,500 | LIT_POSITION x3-4, LIT_GAP | Position against multiple prior frameworks in rapid succession |
| 3. Core Framework: Adoption | 2,000 | MECHANISM_SPECIFY x3, SCOPE_CONDITION x3 | Three adoption strategies (information, procedural, compensation) and their post-enactment limitations |
| 4. Core Framework: Sustainability | 2,500 | MECHANISM_SPECIFY x3, TYPOLOGY_PRESENT | Three sustainability factors (political structures, creative destruction, policy feedback) culminating in 2x2 matrix |
| 5. Empirical Preview | 200 | EMPIRICAL_PREVIEW | One-sentence transition to the case chapters |
| 6. Closing Crystallization | 300 | FRAMEWORK_CRYSTALLIZE | Restate reform as a "dynamic process" |

**Subsection count:** 10-14 named subsections (one per mechanism)

**Distinguishing feature:** The chapter is organized as an inventory of mechanisms. Each mechanism gets its own subsection, its own SCOPE_CONDITION paragraph, and sometimes its own EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE paragraph. The typology at the end synthesizes all mechanisms into a single visual framework.

### Recipe 4: Behavioral-Spillover (Tesler model)

**Total target:** 8,000-11,000 words (may be split across two chapters)

| Section | Word Count | Key Functions | Notes |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| 1. Opening Frame | 800 | EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE | Open with an anecdote that *is* the mechanism in compressed form (chimpanzee cartoon) |
| 2. Literature Engagement | 2,500 | LIT_POSITION x4-5, MECHANISM_SPECIFY x2 | Constructive review: prior findings become building blocks for the hypothesis |
| 3. Core Framework | 1,500 | HYPOTHESIS_STATE, SCOPE_CONDITION x4 | State the named hypothesis; then systematically bound it with numbered conditions |
| 4. Scaffolding Device | 1,500 | SCOPE_CONDITION x4 (expanded), LIT_GAP (anger and polarization extension) | Each condition gets its own subsection with supporting citations |
| 5. Empirical Preview | 800 | EMPIRICAL_PREVIEW | Describe the measurement strategy and data sources (surveys, experiments) |
| 6. Closing Crystallization | 400 | FRAMEWORK_CRYSTALLIZE | Restate the hypothesis and its four conditions |

**Subsection count:** 6-8 named subsections (one per scope condition)

**Distinguishing feature:** The chapter has a single named hypothesis, not a list. The theoretical work is in the scope conditions, which tell the reader when the effect should be strong, weak, or absent. Each scope condition maps to a specific empirical test in later chapters.

### Recipe 5: Realist-Strategic (Lowande model)

**Total target:** 9,000-11,000 words

| Section | Word Count | Key Functions | Notes |
|---------|-----------|---------------|-------|
| 1. Opening Frame | 800 | EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE | Open with a defamiliarizing example (Thanksgiving turkey) that reveals the neglected dimension |
| 2. Literature Engagement | 2,000 | LIT_POSITION x3-4, LIT_GAP | Appreciate existing work on policymaker presidency, then identify the symbolic gap |
| 3. Core Framework: Dimensions | 1,500 | CONCEPT_DEFINE x2 | Define the two dimensions (symbolic vs. substantive); distinguish from prior uses of "symbolic" |
| 4. Core Framework: Typology | 2,000 | TYPOLOGY_PRESENT, EXAMPLE_ILLUSTRATE x3 | Name the three types (think pieces, non sequiturs, basket bets); illustrate each with a presidential case |
| 5. Core Framework: Costs & Benefits | 1,500 | MECHANISM_SPECIFY x2, SCOPE_CONDITION x3, HYPOTHESIS_STATE x3 | Trace costs of substantive vs. symbolic; derive predictions for discretion, opposition, and election timing |
| 6. Empirical Preview | 400 | EMPIRICAL_PREVIEW | Map predictions to chapters |
| 7. Closing Crystallization | 300 | FRAMEWORK_CRYSTALLIZE | One-sentence distillation of the false-front argument |
