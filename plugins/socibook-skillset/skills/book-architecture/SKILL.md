# Book Architecture

You help researchers design **the overall architecture of an academic monograph** -- the high-level structural decisions that determine how many chapters the book has, what function each chapter serves, how chapters are sequenced, and whether the book uses formal parts. Given a book project memo, prospectus, or equivalent description of the argument, evidence, and methods, you guide users through selecting an architecture type, setting quantitative targets, and mapping the full chapter sequence.

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when the researcher has:
- A defined book argument (the core thesis is known, even if still being refined)
- A sense of the evidentiary base (data type, case structure, methods)
- Enough material to know roughly what the book's empirical content will be
- Need to decide the book's overall structure before drafting individual chapters

This skill assumes the researcher knows what the book argues and what evidence it marshals. The task is designing the container -- the architecture that will hold the argument together across 200-400 pages.

## Overview: What Book Architecture Does

Architecture is not decoration. It is the book's argument made visible in the table of contents. The structural decisions an author makes -- how many chapters, what type each chapter is, what order they appear in, whether formal parts group them -- communicate the book's logic before the reader encounters a single sentence of prose.

A well-chosen architecture does three things:
- **Embodies the argument's logic** -- the chapter sequence IS the argument's structure, not a container imposed on it
- **Manages cognitive load** -- the reader can hold the book's trajectory in mind at any point
- **Signals scholarly identity** -- the architecture tells the reader what kind of book this is (comparative, thematic, historical, ethnographic) within seconds of scanning the table of contents

Target total word count: **85,000--130,000 words** (corpus mean ~108,000; median ~105,500; IQR ~86,000--116,000). Min in corpus: 53,000 (Fisher). Max: 178,000 (Smith & King).

## Quantitative Benchmarks

These benchmarks are drawn from analysis of 22 monographs in political sociology and American politics. They define the empirical center of the genre.

| Dimension | Min | Max | Mean | Median | Typical Range |
|-----------|-----|-----|------|--------|---------------|
| Total word count | 53,000 | 178,000 | ~108,000 | ~105,500 | 85,000--130,000 |
| Body chapters (excl. intro/conclusion) | 5 | 10 | 7.0 | 7 | 6--8 |
| Introduction length | 6,000 | 9,000 | ~7,500 | 8,000 | 7,000--9,000 |
| Conclusion length | 4,000 | 8,500 | ~5,700 | 5,500 | 5,000--6,000 |
| Empirical chapter length (case-study books) | -- | -- | -- | -- | 12,000--18,000 |
| Empirical chapter length (quantitative books) | -- | -- | -- | -- | 8,000--15,000 |

The 8,000-word introduction appears in 12 of 22 books in the corpus -- it is the modal length.

Seven body chapters is the central tendency: the mean is 7.0, the median is 7, and the mode is tied at 6 and 8. Manuscripts with fewer than 5 body chapters risk feeling thin; those with more than 10 risk losing the reader.

## The Seven Universal Decisions

Every book architecture, regardless of type, requires the author to make these seven decisions. They are non-negotiable. Deferring any one produces a manuscript that cannot be outlined coherently.

| # | Decision | What It Determines | Diagnostic Question |
|---|----------|--------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Architecture type** | The fundamental logic of chapter sequencing | Does the table of contents embody the argument's structure? |
| 2 | **Chapter count** | The number of body chapters (excluding intro/conclusion) | Is the chapter count justified by the number of analytical units (cases, themes, periods), not by convenience? |
| 3 | **Theory placement** | Where the theoretical framework is developed | Does the reader encounter the framework before, during, or after the evidence -- and is this deliberate? |
| 4 | **Chapter function assignment** | What type each chapter is (theory, context, empirical, synthesis) | Can the author name each chapter's function in one word? |
| 5 | **Chapter ordering** | The sequence of empirical chapters | Does the sequence create argumentative momentum rather than merely listing cases or themes? |
| 6 | **Parts usage** | Whether formal parts group chapters | Do the parts signal a real analytical distinction, or are they cosmetic? |
| 7 | **Word count allocation** | How the total word budget distributes across chapters | Is the longest chapter the one that does the most analytical work? |

## The Six Architecture Types

### Type A: Quant-then-Case

**Defining logic:** A cross-case quantitative analysis establishes the general pattern; qualitative case studies then reveal the mechanisms that produce it. The quantitative chapter comes first to anchor the pattern; case chapters follow to explain the "how" and "why."

**Corpus count:** 1 (Kadivar)

**Signature features:**
- A large-N or cross-case quantitative chapter immediately after the introduction or theory chapter
- Case study chapters that explicitly reference the quantitative findings as their starting point
- Cases selected to vary on the outcome identified in the quantitative analysis
- The quantitative chapter establishes that a pattern exists; the case chapters explain why

**Structural template:**
1. Introduction
2. Theory chapter (optional; can be embedded in introduction)
3. Quantitative / cross-case chapter
4. Case study 1 (canonical confirmation)
5. Case study 2 (paired comparison or extension)
6. Case study 3 (anomalous or deviant case)
7. Conclusion

**When to use:** The book has both large-N observable implications and small-N process tracing. The quantitative evidence is strong enough to establish a general relationship, but insufficient to identify mechanisms. The cases are selected to complement the quantitative analysis, not to stand alone.

**Exemplar:** Kadivar (6 body chapters): quantitative chapter establishes the general pattern, then three case studies -- paired, single, and anomalous -- trace the mechanisms.

---

### Type B: Parallel Cases

**Defining logic:** One or more theory chapters establish the framework, then discrete, bounded case studies appear in sequence, each getting its own chapter. A synthesis chapter (optional) draws comparative conclusions.

**Corpus count:** 6 (Patashnik 2008, Mucciaroni, Brown-Saracino, Patashnik 2023, Smith & King, Dearborn as hybrid)

**Signature features:**
- Clear separation between theory chapters and empirical chapters
- Each case (a policy domain, a city, a movement, a historical episode) gets its own chapter
- Cases share a recognizable parallel structure (similar subsection types, similar analytical categories)
- The sequence of cases creates argumentative momentum (e.g., success to failure, simple to complex)
- Optional synthesis chapter before the conclusion draws explicit cross-case comparisons

**Structural template:**
1. Introduction
2. Theory chapter(s) -- 1 or 2
3. Case 1 (clearest confirmation or canonical case)
4. Case 2 (extension or contrasting outcome)
5. Case 3 (further variation)
6. [Case 4--6, if needed]
7. Synthesis chapter (optional)
8. Conclusion

**When to use:** The book examines a singular causal mechanism producing differential outcomes across 3--6 discrete, bounded cases. The theory-testing ambition is explicit. Each case is large enough to warrant its own chapter. The comparative design is the book's primary contribution.

**Exemplars:**
- Patashnik 2008 (8 body chapters): 2 theory chapters, then 6 case chapters ordered from success to failure
- Mucciaroni (7 body chapters + conclusion): 3 theory chapters, then 4 domain cases
- Smith & King (10 body chapters, 4 parts): 2 theory chapters, then two groups of 3 case chapters each (one per alliance), then synthesis

---

### Type C: Thematic Progression

**Defining logic:** The concept or argument is built incrementally across chapters, with evidence presented thematically rather than case-by-case. No single chapter is "the tax reform chapter" or "the Brazil chapter" -- instead, each chapter develops one dimension of the argument using whatever evidence best illuminates that dimension.

**Corpus count:** 7 (Bolton & Thrower, Fisher, Grumbach, Lowande, Mettler, SoRelle, Tesler)

**Signature features:**
- Chapters named for analytical concepts or themes, not for cases or time periods
- Evidence from multiple cases, datasets, or domains may appear in a single chapter
- The argument builds cumulatively -- each chapter adds a layer that depends on the previous
- Quantitative or mixed-methods evidence is common
- The reader cannot skip chapters without losing the thread

**Structural template:**
1. Introduction
2. Theory / framework chapter (common but not universal)
3. Empirical chapter: dimension 1 of the argument
4. Empirical chapter: dimension 2, building on dimension 1
5. Empirical chapter: dimension 3, building on dimensions 1--2
6. [Additional empirical chapters as needed]
7. Empirical chapter: implications, extensions, or contemporary application
8. Conclusion

**When to use:** The book develops a concept that needs incremental building. The evidence is quantitative or mixed-methods. Domain variation is examined analytically (across themes) rather than case-by-case. No single case is the unit of analysis -- the phenomenon is.

**Exemplars:**
- Grumbach (7 body chapters, 3 parts): historical context chapter, then quantitative analyses building incrementally across institutional domains
- SoRelle, Mettler, Tesler: thematic chapters each developing one facet of a larger argument about institutional or political processes

---

### Type D: Historical Arc

**Defining logic:** Chapters are organized chronologically or by historical period. The argument is that temporal change is the primary explanatory variable -- the phenomenon evolved, and understanding why requires tracing that evolution.

**Corpus count:** 4 (Dearborn, Krimmel, Pettinicchio, Rodriguez-Muniz)

**Signature features:**
- Chapter titles or subtitles reference time periods, decades, or eras
- The sequence tracks a process of change, not a comparison of static cases
- Earlier chapters provide necessary context for later chapters (strong path dependence in the argument)
- The conclusion often connects the historical arc to the present moment

**Structural template:**
1. Introduction
2. Theory / framework chapter (optional)
3. Origins / early period chapter
4. Development / middle period chapter(s)
5. Transformation / critical juncture chapter(s)
6. Contemporary period / aftermath chapter
7. Conclusion

**When to use:** Temporal change is the primary explanatory variable. The book traces the evolution of a phenomenon over decades or longer. The argument depends on showing how earlier conditions produced later outcomes. The cases are not discrete units but phases of a single process.

**Exemplars:**
- Krimmel: chapters track the historical development of a partisan phenomenon across distinct periods
- Pettinicchio: traces the evolution of disability policy through historical eras
- Rodriguez-Muniz: follows the development of census politics over time

---

### Type E: Ethnographic

**Defining logic:** Theory and methods are established first (often in dedicated chapters), then thematic empirical chapters organized by analytical concept present field-derived evidence. The architecture reflects the logic of ethnographic inquiry: immersion first, then analytical distillation.

**Corpus count:** 5 (Abrams, Lichterman, Ternullo, Vogler; plus elements in Brown-Saracino)

**Signature features:**
- Dedicated theory chapter and often a dedicated methods/setting chapter before empirical chapters
- Empirical chapters named for analytical concepts, not for field sites or informants
- Heavy use of interview quotations and field scenes as primary evidence
- The author is present as observer and interpreter
- Chapter sequence reflects the analytical framework's conceptual categories, not chronology

**Structural template:**
1. Introduction
2. Theory chapter
3. Methods / setting chapter (describes the field site, research design, positionality)
4. Empirical chapter: analytical concept 1
5. Empirical chapter: analytical concept 2
6. Empirical chapter: analytical concept 3
7. [Additional empirical chapters as needed]
8. Conclusion

**When to use:** The book is built from place-based or field-derived data. The argument concerns meaning-making, practice, or organizational culture. The evidence is primarily ethnographic (interviews, participant observation, field notes). The contribution is interpretive.

**Exemplars:**
- Lichterman (9 body chapters + intro/conclusion): theory chapter, then methods/setting chapter, then 5 thematic empirical chapters
- Ternullo (7 body chapters + intro/conclusion, 3 parts): parts organized as Past / Present / Future, with ethnographic evidence throughout
- Vogler, Abrams: theory and methods precede thematic empirical chapters

---

### Type F: Multi-case Thematic

**Defining logic:** Multiple cases are woven through thematic chapters rather than appearing sequentially. No case gets its own chapter; instead, each thematic chapter draws on multiple cases to develop one facet of the argument. The comparison is additive, not contrastive.

**Corpus count:** 1 (Han et al.)

**Signature features:**
- Chapters named for themes or analytical dimensions, not for cases
- Multiple cases appear within each chapter, compared on the chapter's theme
- No single case is decisive on its own -- the argument accumulates across cases
- Differs from Thematic Progression (Type C) in that the cases are discrete and named, but woven together rather than sequenced

**Structural template:**
1. Introduction
2. Theory / framework chapter
3. Thematic chapter 1 (multiple cases compared on dimension 1)
4. Thematic chapter 2 (same cases compared on dimension 2)
5. Thematic chapter 3 (same cases compared on dimension 3)
6. [Additional thematic chapters as needed]
7. Conclusion

**When to use:** No case is decisive on its own. The comparison is additive -- each case contributes partial evidence that cumulates across themes. The book's contribution is the thematic framework, not any single case's story.

**Exemplar:** Han et al. (6 body chapters): multiple cases woven through thematic chapters, with comparison building across dimensions rather than proceeding case by case.

## Architecture Decision Tree

Use this tree to select the right architecture type. Work through each question in order.

### Question 1: What is the book's unit of analysis?

- **Discrete, bounded cases** (countries, cities, policy domains, movements) that each warrant a full chapter --> likely **B (Parallel Cases)**
- **A phenomenon examined thematically**, with evidence drawn from multiple sources per chapter --> likely **C (Thematic Progression)**
- **Historical periods or eras** that track a process of change --> likely **D (Historical Arc)**
- **A field site or community** studied ethnographically --> likely **E (Ethnographic)**
- **Multiple discrete cases, but none decisive on its own** --> likely **F (Multi-case Thematic)**
- **A general pattern (large-N) plus a few cases (small-N)** --> likely **A (Quant-then-Case)**

### Question 2: What is the primary evidence type?

- **Quantitative or large-N** --> favors **A** or **C**
- **Comparative-qualitative with bounded cases** --> favors **B**
- **Mixed methods, building a concept incrementally** --> favors **C**
- **Historical-archival, tracing change over time** --> favors **D**
- **Ethnographic (interviews, observation, field notes)** --> favors **E**
- **Multiple cases compared on shared dimensions** --> favors **B** or **F**

### Question 3: What is the book's theoretical ambition?

- **Testing a singular causal mechanism across cases** --> favors **B (Parallel Cases)**
- **Building or elaborating a new concept** --> favors **C (Thematic Progression)**
- **Tracing the evolution of a phenomenon** --> favors **D (Historical Arc)**
- **Interpreting meaning-making, practice, or culture** --> favors **E (Ethnographic)**
- **Establishing a general pattern and explaining mechanisms** --> favors **A (Quant-then-Case)**
- **Developing a thematic comparative framework** --> favors **F (Multi-case Thematic)**

### Question 4: How many discrete cases does the book have?

- **3--6 bounded cases, each large enough for a chapter** --> strongly favors **B**
- **More than 6 cases, none large enough for a chapter** --> favors **F** or **C**
- **1 extended case examined in depth** --> favors **E** or **D**
- **Large-N plus 2--4 process-tracing cases** --> favors **A**

### Confirming selection:

If the answers point to different types, prioritize **Question 1** (unit of analysis). A book built from discrete bounded cases will feel forced into a thematic architecture; a book built from thematic evidence will feel artificial chopped into case chapters. Name the primary architecture type and note any hybrid elements before proceeding to structural planning.

## Technique: Theory Chapter Placement

Approximately 12 of 22 books in the corpus have standalone theory chapters. The remaining embed theoretical development in the introduction or distribute it across empirical chapters. This decision is consequential.

### Option 1: Standalone Theory Chapter

**When it works best:**
- The theory is genuinely complex, with multiple interlocking concepts
- The reader needs a "checklist" of theoretical variables before the empirical chapters make sense
- The book is theory-testing: the framework must be fully specified before evidence is examined

**In the corpus:** Patashnik 2008 uses 2 theory chapters before any empirical material. Mucciaroni uses 3 theory chapters -- the heaviest scaffolding in the corpus.

**Risk:** If the theory chapter is too long or abstract, the reader may disengage before reaching the evidence. A standalone theory chapter works only when the payoff is clear.

### Option 2: Embedded in Introduction

**When it works best:**
- The theoretical contribution is simpler or when the theory IS the comparative structure
- The book has 5--6 body chapters and cannot afford to spend one on theory alone
- The argument can be stated compactly and the introduction can carry the theoretical load

**In the corpus:** Several Thematic Progression and Historical Arc books embed theory in the introduction, reserving all body chapters for empirical work.

### Option 3: Two Theory Chapters (Framework + Mechanism)

**When it works best:**
- The book distinguishes between a broad analytical framework and specific causal mechanisms
- The first theory chapter positions the book in the literature; the second specifies the causal argument

**In the corpus:** Patashnik 2008 separates framework from mechanism across two chapters. Smith & King devotes two chapters to theoretical architecture before empirical material.

### Option 4: Distributed Across Empirical Chapters

**When it works best:**
- The theory emerges from the evidence (grounded theory logic)
- Each empirical chapter introduces the theoretical concepts it needs
- The book resists a priori framework specification

**Risk:** If the theoretical contribution is not synthesized somewhere (introduction, conclusion, or a late-appearing theory chapter), the reader may not see the forest for the trees.

## Technique: Case Ordering (for Parallel Cases and Quant-then-Case)

The corpus strongly favors ordering by theoretical clarity over chronology or alphabetical order. Three ordering principles appear:

### Principle 1: By Theoretical Clarity (most common)

Place the clearest confirmation of the theory first, the clearest disconfirmation second, complicating cases in the middle, and the most complex or anomalous case last.

**Logic:** The reader learns the framework through its cleanest application, then sees how it handles variation.

**In the corpus:** Patashnik 2008 orders from success to failure. Kadivar places the paired confirmation case first, then the single confirmation, then the anomalous case (Tunisia) last. Brown-Saracino places the clearest case (Ithaca) first and the most complex dual-culture case (Greenfield) last.

### Principle 2: By Outcome Gradient

Order cases from success to mixed to failure (or vice versa), creating a gradient that reveals the theory's explanatory range.

**Logic:** The gradient creates narrative momentum and shows the theory's scope conditions.

**In the corpus:** Mucciaroni moves from total success (sodomy law repeal) through partial success (adoption) to failure (military). Patashnik 2008 follows the same logic across six cases.

### Principle 3: Chronological

Order cases by when they occurred, when this is meaningful for the argument.

**Logic:** Works when the cases are not independent -- when earlier cases influence later ones, or when the phenomenon changes over time.

**When to use:** Only when temporal sequence is analytically meaningful, not merely convenient. The corpus favors theoretical clarity over chronology when the two conflict.

## Technique: Parts vs. No Parts

Eight of 22 books (36%) use formal parts; 14 do not. This is not a default decision.

### When Parts Work

- **Clear binary or tripartite analytical distinction:** The book has a natural two- or three-part structure that reflects a real analytical division (e.g., Smith & King's two-alliance structure; Ternullo's Past / Present / Future).
- **Navigational scaffolding for long books:** Books with parts average ~120,000 words and 7.9 body chapters; books without parts average ~100,000 words and 6.8 chapters. Parts help readers navigate longer manuscripts.

### When Parts Hurt

- **They signal cases belong to distinct families:** Parts can work against a tight comparative argument by implying the cases in Part II and Part III are fundamentally different kinds of things, rather than variations on a single theme.
- **They add structural overhead without analytical payoff:** If the part titles are generic ("Empirical Evidence," "Analysis"), they are not doing analytical work.

### Decision rule:

Use parts when (a) the book has a genuine binary or tripartite analytical structure that readers need to see in the table of contents AND (b) the book is long enough (typically 8+ body chapters or 110,000+ words) that navigational scaffolding above the chapter level is needed. Otherwise, omit them.

## Technique: Chapter Count and Word Count Allocation

### Setting the Chapter Count

The chapter count should be determined by the number of analytical units the book contains, not by word count or convention.

- **Parallel Cases (Type B):** One chapter per case, plus 1--2 theory chapters, plus optional synthesis. If the book has 4 cases and 1 theory chapter, the body has 5--6 chapters.
- **Thematic Progression (Type C):** One chapter per analytical dimension. If the argument has 5 dimensions, the body has 5--6 chapters (plus optional context or framework chapter).
- **Historical Arc (Type D):** One chapter per period. If the history divides into 4 periods, the body has 4--5 chapters (plus optional context chapter).
- **Ethnographic (Type E):** One theory chapter + one methods chapter + one chapter per analytical concept. If the framework has 4 concepts, the body has 6--7 chapters.

### Allocating Word Count

A 105,000-word book (the corpus median) with 7 body chapters might allocate:

| Component | Words | Share |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Introduction | 8,000 | 7.6% |
| Theory chapter | 10,000--12,000 | 9.5--11.4% |
| 5 empirical chapters @ 13,000 | 65,000 | 61.9% |
| Synthesis chapter (optional) | 8,000--10,000 | 7.6--9.5% |
| Conclusion | 5,500 | 5.2% |
| **Total** | **~105,000** | **100%** |

Empirical chapters should receive 60--70% of total word count. If the introduction, theory chapter, and conclusion together consume more than 25% of the manuscript, the scaffolding is crowding out the evidence.

## Technique: Chapter Function Assignment

Every chapter in the manuscript should be assigned one of the following functions. A chapter that tries to serve two functions (e.g., theory AND first case) typically does neither well.

### Chapter Function Taxonomy (from 22 books)

| Function | Frequency | Description |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Introduction | 22/22 | Frames the book's argument, stakes, evidence, and structure |
| Theory / Framework | ~12 standalone | Develops the book's theoretical contribution in detail |
| Historical Background / Context | ~8 standalone | Provides the historical or institutional context needed before empirical chapters |
| Methods / Case Selection | ~3 standalone | Describes research design, case selection rationale, data sources; often embedded in introduction |
| Empirical: Quantitative / Cross-case | varies | Presents large-N or cross-case analysis |
| Empirical: Case Study | varies | Presents a single bounded case within a comparative framework |
| Empirical: Thematic | varies | Develops one analytical dimension using evidence from multiple sources |
| Comparative Synthesis | ~4 books | Draws explicit cross-case or cross-theme comparisons after empirical chapters |
| Conclusion | 22/22 | Synthesizes findings, addresses implications, stakes, and limitations |

**Key observation:** Methods chapters are rare as standalone units (3/22). Most books embed case selection and methods in the introduction or theory chapter. A standalone methods chapter is warranted only when the research design is complex enough to require extended justification (e.g., ethnographic books with multi-site designs or mixed-methods books with elaborate sampling procedures).

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. The architecture that does not match the argument

If the book argues that a single mechanism produces different outcomes across cases, but the chapters are organized thematically rather than case-by-case, the architecture contradicts the argument. The table of contents should make the argument's logic visible. A comparative argument needs a comparative architecture (Type B). A concept-building argument needs a thematic architecture (Type C).

### 2. Too many theory chapters before any evidence

Mucciaroni's 3 theory chapters before any empirical material is the maximum in the corpus -- and it is an outlier. Most books use 1 theory chapter. If the reader must wade through 30,000 words of theory before seeing any evidence, the scaffolding has become the building. Two theory chapters is defensible for complex frameworks; three is rare and risky.

### 3. The chapter that serves two functions

A chapter that tries to be both the theory chapter and the first case study typically does neither well. The theory is underdeveloped because the case demands attention; the case is underdeveloped because the theory demands space. Assign one function per chapter.

### 4. Case ordering by convenience rather than argument

Alphabetical or chronological ordering of cases is almost never the strongest choice. The corpus strongly favors ordering by theoretical clarity -- clearest case first, most complex last. If the case order does not create argumentative momentum, reconsider the sequence.

### 5. Parts without analytical justification

Using parts because the manuscript "feels long" is not sufficient. Parts must signal a real analytical distinction. If the part titles could be replaced with "Part I" and "Part II" without loss of information, they are not doing work. Either give them analytically meaningful titles or remove them.

### 6. The 12-chapter manuscript

The maximum in the corpus is 10 body chapters (Smith & King), and that book is also the longest at 178,000 words. A manuscript with 12+ body chapters is almost certainly either (a) including chapters that should be combined, (b) treating sub-cases as full cases, or (c) writing a handbook rather than a monograph. If the chapter count exceeds 10, audit each chapter's function and consider combining.

### 7. The 60,000-word manuscript with 8 chapters

If the word count is modest but the chapter count is high, each chapter will be too thin to develop its argument adequately. An 8,000-word empirical chapter is at the floor of what the corpus supports for quantitative chapters and well below the minimum for case study chapters. Either reduce chapter count or increase total word count.

### 8. Embedding the conclusion's work in the last empirical chapter

The last empirical chapter should deliver its own findings. The conclusion chapter is where synthesis, implications, and broader stakes belong. If the last empirical chapter is doing the conclusion's work, the conclusion will be redundant and the last case underdeveloped.

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Architecture Type Selection

**Goal:** Determine which of the six architecture types fits the book's argument, evidence, and methods.

**Steps:**
1. Read the book project memo, prospectus, or equivalent
2. Identify: What is the core argument? What is the unit of analysis? What is the primary evidence type?
3. Walk through the decision tree (Questions 1--4) with the user
4. Select a primary architecture type
5. Note any hybrid elements (e.g., a Parallel Cases book with one thematic chapter)

**Pause:** Confirm architecture type before proceeding.

---

### Phase 2: Structural Planning

**Goal:** Map the full chapter sequence with functions, word count targets, and ordering rationale.

**Steps:**
1. Determine the chapter count based on the number of analytical units
2. Assign a function to each chapter (introduction, theory, empirical, synthesis, conclusion)
3. Decide theory placement (standalone, embedded, two chapters, or distributed)
4. Decide case or theme ordering (by theoretical clarity, outcome gradient, or chronology) and justify
5. Decide whether to use formal parts (apply the decision rule)
6. Allocate word counts to each chapter, checking that empirical chapters receive 60--70% of total
7. Map the full sequence in a chapter-by-chapter outline:
   - Chapter number and title
   - Function
   - Target word count
   - One-sentence description of what the chapter argues
   - Position rationale (why this chapter comes here in the sequence)

**Pause:** Review the structural plan with the user before individual chapter drafting begins.

---

### Phase 3: Calibration

**Goal:** Test the structural plan against corpus benchmarks and architectural logic.

**Calibration checks:**

| Test | Pass Criteria |
|------|---------------|
| **Total word count** | 85,000--130,000 (or justified deviation) |
| **Chapter count** | 5--10 body chapters |
| **Introduction length** | 7,000--9,000 words |
| **Conclusion length** | 5,000--6,500 words |
| **Empirical chapter length** | 12,000--18,000 (case study) or 8,000--15,000 (quantitative) |
| **Theory-to-evidence ratio** | Scaffolding (intro + theory + conclusion) is less than 25% of total |
| **Architecture-argument alignment** | The chapter sequence embodies the argument's logic |
| **Case ordering rationale** | The sequence creates argumentative momentum |
| **Parts justification** | Parts signal real analytical distinction, or are absent |

**Structural logic tests:**

| Test | Question | Fix if Failing |
|------|----------|----------------|
| **Table of contents test** | Can a reader infer the book's argument from the table of contents alone? | Revise chapter titles and sequence until the argument is visible |
| **Function uniqueness test** | Does each chapter serve exactly one function? | Split or combine chapters that serve multiple functions |
| **Ordering test** | Does the case/theme sequence create momentum? | Reorder by theoretical clarity or outcome gradient |
| **Load balance test** | Is the longest chapter the one doing the most analytical work? | Redistribute material if scaffolding chapters are longer than empirical chapters |
| **Parts test** | Do the part titles convey analytical content? | Remove parts or give them substantive titles |
