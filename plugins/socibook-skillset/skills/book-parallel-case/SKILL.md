# Parallel Case Study Chapter

You help researchers write **parallel case study chapters for academic monographs** -- the empirical chapters where a single case (a movement, a policy domain, a city, a country) receives its own chapter within a systematic comparative framework. Given the book's theoretical framework, the case's position in the comparative design, and the available evidence, you guide users through selecting a chapter cluster, structuring the chapter, and drafting publication-ready prose.

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when the researcher has:
- A defined comparative framework (the book's theory is established, typically in an introduction or theory chapter)
- A specific case to present (one unit in a set of parallel cases)
- Evidence assembled for that case (interviews, archives, field notes, secondary sources)
- Knowledge of where this chapter sits in the book's sequence (first case, middle case, last case, anomalous case)

This skill assumes the comparative framework exists. The task is presenting one case within that framework -- grounding the theory in specific evidence, delivering a verdict, and positioning the case relative to the others.

## Overview: What a Parallel Case Chapter Does

A parallel case chapter is not a standalone essay. It is one unit in a comparative architecture. Its purpose is to show what happens when the book's theoretical framework meets one specific case. The chapter must simultaneously:

- **Ground the abstract in the concrete** -- translate theoretical variables into observable processes within this case
- **Present evidence systematically** -- organize the case's empirical material so it speaks to the framework's analytical categories
- **Deliver a verdict** -- state what this case shows about the theory (confirms, complicates, extends, or refutes)
- **Maintain parallel structure** -- use a subsection architecture recognizably related to the other case chapters, so readers can compare across chapters
- **Signal its position** -- announce whether this is the canonical case, the surprise, the failure, or the anomaly

Target length: **9,000--14,000 words** (corpus range: 8,500--17,000). The most complex case (anomalous, dual-culture, or multi-sub-case) tends to be longest.

## The Six Universal Moves

Every parallel case chapter, regardless of cluster, executes these six moves. They are non-negotiable. Their order and emphasis vary, but omitting any one produces a structurally incomplete chapter.

| # | Move | Function | Diagnostic Question |
|---|------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Claim announcement** | State what this chapter will argue or show | Can the reader identify the chapter's central claim by the end of the second paragraph? |
| 2 | **Theory connection** | Link this case to the book's comparative framework | Does the reader know which theoretical variables are being tested and what the theory predicts for this case? |
| 3 | **Evidence in bounded sections** | Present evidence organized in labeled subsections that mirror the analytical categories | Are the subsections doing argumentative work, not just organizing material by topic? |
| 4 | **Explicit verdict** | State what the case demonstrates | Can the reader quote the finding in 1--2 sentences? |
| 5 | **Calibration** | Acknowledge what the evidence does not show, or address rival explanations | Has the author qualified the finding without undermining it? |
| 6 | **Bridge** | Connect to the next chapter or to the book's synthesis | Does the reader know where the argument goes from here? |

No parallel case chapter in the corpus opens with a literature review. No parallel case chapter opens with methodology. The case comes first.

## The Four Chapter Clusters

### Cluster 1: The Analytical Case

**Defining logic:** Open with the outcome stated, then explain it through theory-driven subsections. The reader knows the verdict before the evidence is presented; the chapter's task is to show *why* the outcome occurred.

**Exemplar:** Patashnik (2008), Chapters 3--8

**Signature features:**
- Outcome stated in the first two paragraphs ("the TRA did not successfully reconfigure the political economy of tax policymaking")
- Theory-connection paragraph immediately follows, mapping the case onto the framework's variables
- Subsections organized by analytical category (pre-reform situation, adoption politics, erosion mechanisms, political dynamics), not by chronology alone
- Evidence is documentary and archival: legislative records, statistical tables, expert quotations, secondary scholarship
- Verdict delivered in the opening paragraph of a labeled CONCLUSIONS section
- No original interviews; no first-person presence

**Structural sequence:**
1. Outcome + theory connection (500--800 words)
2. Pre-reform / background section (1,000--2,000 words)
3. Adoption / creation narrative (1,500--2,500 words)
4. Aftermath / erosion / sustainability analysis, in 3--5 thematic subsections (3,000--5,000 words)
5. Conclusions: verdict + calibration (500--1,000 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 9,000--13,000 |
| Subsections | 8--12 |
| Quote density | 18--30 (all secondary-source quotations) |
| Opening type | Outcome stated or puzzle named |
| Verdict style | Enumerated mechanism-by-mechanism |
| Bridge | Implicit (no explicit transition to next chapter) |

**When to use:** The book's cases are policy domains, institutional reforms, or historical episodes. Evidence is primarily documentary and archival. The framework specifies testable variables. The author's voice is analytical, not experiential.

---

### Cluster 2: The Ethnographic Case

**Defining logic:** Open with the author arriving at the field site. Let the case unfold through scenes, interview voices, and the researcher's evolving understanding. The verdict emerges from accumulated ethnographic observation.

**Exemplar:** Brown-Saracino (2018), Chapters 1--4

**Signature features:**
- First-person arrival narrative as the opening move ("In 2006 I moved to Ithaca, New York, to teach at Cornell University")
- Field scenes anchor each analytical concept: the pie contest, the dance party, the Farmers Market
- Interview quotes as primary evidence (~30--60 per chapter), identified by first name or descriptor
- Subsections organized thematically around the case's identity culture, with ethnographic scenes as section openers
- Theory connection comes in an early subsection (not the opening paragraph), where the author names the case's position in the comparative framework
- Verdict in a labeled Conclusion subsection, stating what the city "demonstrates," "reveals," or "confirms"
- Explicit bridge to the next city chapter, often framed as a challenge to the current case's universality

**Structural sequence:**
1. First-person arrival and initial observations (500--1,000 words)
2. Quantitative/demographic anchoring (200--500 words)
3. Research puzzle arising from the observations (200--500 words)
4. Theory-connection subsection linking city ecology to the framework (500--1,000 words)
5. 3--5 thematic subsections, each anchored in a scene or extended interview (6,000--12,000 words)
6. Conclusion: verdict + reflective generalization (500--1,500 words)
7. Bridge to next chapter (1--3 sentences)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 11,000--17,000 |
| Subsections | 6--9 |
| Quote density | 30--60 interview quotes + 6--10 field scenes |
| Opening type | First-person arrival or expectation-violation |
| Verdict style | Reflective generalization ("this city demonstrates that...") |
| Bridge | Explicit forward-looking sentence |

**When to use:** The book's cases are field sites (cities, organizations, communities). Evidence is primarily ethnographic: interviews, participant observation, field notes. The author is present as observer. The contribution is interpretive.

---

### Cluster 3: The Biographical-Vignette Case

**Defining logic:** Open with the stories of specific individuals affected by the policy or institution under study. Use their experiences to anchor a chronological narrative that tests the book's hypotheses.

**Exemplar:** Mucciaroni (2008), Chapters 4--6

**Signature features:**
- Opening with two paired biographical vignettes -- real people whose lives illustrate the issue's stakes (McConnell and Baker applying for a marriage license; Lawrence and Garner arrested for sodomy)
- Chronological narrative organized around institutional tracks (legislative, then judicial)
- Evidence is documentary: court opinions, legislative records, statistical tables, secondary scholarship
- Zero original interview quotations; the human stories come from legal records and news accounts
- Theory connection appears at the transition from background to analysis ("Following the kind of analysis presented in chapter 1...")
- Verdict in the Conclusion subsection's *opening* paragraph, explicitly mapping findings onto the book's numbered hypotheses
- Chapters across the book use the same paired-vignette opening template

**Structural sequence:**
1. Two paired biographical vignettes illustrating the issue (500--800 words)
2. "As these stories illustrate..." pivot to the analytical stakes (100--200 words)
3. Historical development of the issue (1,500--2,500 words)
4. Institutional analysis organized by the book's theoretical variables (4,000--7,000 words)
5. Comparative cases within the chapter (cross-national or cross-group), if applicable (1,000--3,000 words)
6. Conclusion: explicit hypothesis-by-hypothesis verdict (500--1,500 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 12,000--15,000 |
| Subsections | 7--11 |
| Quote density | 15--25 (all from legal records, testimony, secondary sources) |
| Opening type | Paired biographical vignettes |
| Verdict style | Hypothesis-by-hypothesis enumeration |
| Bridge | Implicit or carried by the next chapter's opening |

**When to use:** The book's cases are policy issues, legal domains, or institutional arenas. The author wants to ground the analysis in human impact. Evidence is documentary but the opening creates narrative engagement. The framework has numbered or testable hypotheses.

---

### Cluster 4: The Mechanism-Tracing Case

**Defining logic:** Apply a rigid analytical template across all cases. Each case chapter uses the same subsection headings, derived directly from the theoretical framework. The chapter's task is to trace the mechanisms the theory posits, showing how they operated (or failed to operate) in this specific case.

**Exemplar:** Kadivar (2022), Chapters 3--5

**Signature features:**
- Opening with a case-selection rationale: why this case was chosen and what outcome variation it represents
- Rigid repeated template: identical subsection headings across all case chapters (Campaign / Transition / Leadership Change / Civil Society / Political Outcome in Kadivar)
- Evidence is secondary historical narrative: organizational histories, electoral data, scholarly reconstructions
- Relatively low quote density; the author's analytical voice dominates
- Theory connection is methodological: explains that the subsection template *is* the theory's analytical categories
- Verdict delivered through explicit mechanism enumeration ("seven numbered mechanisms linking long mobilization to democratic durability")
- Anomalous cases explicitly name themselves as such and state the theoretical extension

**Structural sequence:**
1. Case-selection rationale and outcome preview (300--600 words)
2. Repeated analytical subsections (same headings as other case chapters): 3--5 subsections per case (5,000--7,000 words)
3. Mechanisms synthesis: numbered enumeration of identified mechanisms (500--1,000 words)
4. Conclusion: verdict + theoretical implications (500--1,000 words)
5. Bridge to next chapter (explicit when methodologically distinct chapters follow)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 8,500--9,500 |
| Subsections | 5--7 (derived from theoretical template) |
| Quote density | 5--35 (varies by case; highest in anomalous/deviant cases) |
| Opening type | Case-selection rationale or explicit puzzle statement |
| Verdict style | Mechanism enumeration |
| Bridge | Explicit when the next chapter shifts methodology |

**When to use:** The book compares cases cross-nationally or across historical episodes. The framework specifies mechanisms that should be observable in each case. Consistency of analysis across cases is more important than narrative engagement within any single case. Evidence is primarily secondary-historical.

## How Chapter Position Affects Structure

Position in the book's sequence is not decorative. It determines the chapter's argumentative function and shapes its opening move, evidence structure, and closing move.

### First Case Chapter

- Establishes the empirical baseline or demonstrates the theory's primary positive/negative case
- Opens with broad framing or expectation-setting; does NOT open with a complicating anomaly
- Closing delivers an explicit verdict plus a forward bridge suggesting the pattern needs further testing
- Tends to be the clearest, most straightforward demonstration
- **Patashnik:** Tax Reform (Ch. 3) as canonical failure case
- **Brown-Saracino:** Ithaca (Ch. 1) as the "integration" pole
- **Mucciaroni:** Sodomy Laws (Ch. 4) as total success case

### Middle Case Chapter

- Accumulates evidence for a pattern already established, or extends the theory to a new domain
- Opens by building on prior findings, often with an "as we have seen" bridge or an expectation statement
- Evidence is marshaled to confirm and extend, not to challenge
- Closing is forward-pointing, establishing what the next chapter will show
- **Patashnik:** Agriculture (Ch. 4) as mixed case; Procurement (Ch. 6) as partial case
- **Mucciaroni:** Adoption (Ch. 5) as partial success
- **Brown-Saracino:** San Luis Obispo (Ch. 2) as the surprise counter-case; Portland (Ch. 3) as the hybrid

### Last Case Chapter (before conclusion)

- Reserved for the book's most complex, revealing, or theoretically generative case
- Often the anomalous case, the strongest positive case, or the multi-case synthesis
- Opens by acknowledging prior findings and announcing what this chapter adds
- Closing delivers the grand synthesis verdict or the theoretical extension that opens to the conclusion
- **Patashnik:** Acid Rain (Ch. 8) as strongest positive demonstration
- **Brown-Saracino:** Greenfield (Ch. 4) as most complex (two coexisting identity cultures)
- **Kadivar:** Tunisia (Ch. 5) as anomalous case

### Anomalous Case Chapter

- Can appear anywhere after canonical cases are established, but is most effective last
- Opens with an explicit puzzle announcement: "This case does not fit the pattern"
- The chapter's function is to extend the theory (identifying additional mechanisms, alternate pathways, or scope conditions)
- Closing is almost always a theoretical extension or qualification
- **Kadivar Ch. 5:** "Exploring the case of Tunisia thus presents an opportunity to investigate alternative pathways"
- **Brown-Saracino Ch. 4:** "In Greenfield, however, something different is found: there is more than one identity culture"

### Negative Case Chapter

- Almost always positioned late, after affirmative cases establish the pattern
- Opens by signaling methodological logic: examine where the expected outcome did not occur
- Closing delivers the necessity claim: without the key factor, the outcome does not materialize
- **Mucciaroni Ch. 6:** Military as "the reverse image of the sodomy repeal and adoption cases"
- **Kadivar Ch. 4:** Egypt as failure of short mobilization

## Cluster Decision Tree

### Question 1: What is the primary evidence type?

- **Documentary and archival** (legislative records, court decisions, secondary scholarship) --> likely **Analytical Case** or **Biographical-Vignette Case**
- **Ethnographic** (interviews, participant observation, field notes) --> likely **Ethnographic Case**
- **Secondary historical** (organizational histories, scholarly reconstructions) --> likely **Mechanism-Tracing Case**
- **Mixed** (interviews + some documentary) --> consider **Ethnographic Case** with documentary subsections

### Question 2: Does the book's framework specify testable hypotheses or mechanisms?

- **Numbered hypotheses** --> favors **Biographical-Vignette Case** (explicit hypothesis testing in verdict)
- **Named mechanisms** --> favors **Mechanism-Tracing Case** (mechanism enumeration in verdict)
- **Ecological/contextual variables** --> favors **Ethnographic Case** (reflective generalization in verdict)
- **Institutional variables** --> favors **Analytical Case** (institutional analysis in subsections)

### Question 3: Is the author present in the evidence?

- **First-person fieldwork** --> must use **Ethnographic Case**
- **No authorial presence** --> use **Analytical Case**, **Biographical-Vignette Case**, or **Mechanism-Tracing Case**

### Question 4: How rigid is the cross-chapter template?

- **Identical subsection headings across all cases** --> **Mechanism-Tracing Case**
- **Parallel but varying subsections** --> **Analytical Case** or **Biographical-Vignette Case**
- **Thematic subsections that respond to the case's own texture** --> **Ethnographic Case**

### Confirming selection:

If answers point to different clusters, prioritize **evidence type** (Question 1). A book built from interviews cannot use the Analytical Case template; a book built from archives cannot use the Ethnographic Case opening. Name the primary cluster and any borrowed moves before drafting.

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Cluster Selection and Structural Planning

**Goal:** Determine the chapter's cluster, position, and subsection architecture before any drafting begins.

**Steps:**
1. Read the book's theoretical framework and the case evidence
2. Determine the case's position: first, middle, last, anomalous, or negative case
3. Walk through the decision tree to select a cluster
4. Identify any moves borrowed from adjacent clusters (e.g., an Ethnographic Case that borrows the hypothesis-by-hypothesis verdict from the Biographical-Vignette cluster)
5. Map the subsection structure with approximate word counts per section
6. Determine:
   - Opening type (outcome statement, arrival scene, biographical vignette, case-selection rationale)
   - Theory-connection placement (paragraph 2, early subsection, transition paragraph)
   - Verdict style (mechanism enumeration, reflective generalization, hypothesis testing)
   - Bridge type (explicit sentence, implicit through next chapter's opening, none)

**Pause:** Confirm cluster selection and structural plan before drafting.

---

### Phase 2: Drafting

**Goal:** Draft each section following the selected cluster's sequence, applying word count targets.

**Prose calibration:** Invoke `prose-craft` in **book mode**. Use descriptive mode for case narratives and evidence presentation; evaluative mode for cross-case comparison and analytical claims.

**Steps:**
1. Read `techniques/section-recipes.md` for the recipe matching your cluster and position. Read `techniques/paragraph-functions.md` for paragraph-level moves.
2. Draft the opening. The first two paragraphs must accomplish the claim announcement. Use the opening type prescribed by your cluster.
3. Draft the theory-connection passage. It must name the theoretical variables and state what the theory predicts for this case.
4. Draft each evidence subsection, using the subsection architecture from the structural plan. Each subsection should have an analytical purpose, not just a topic.
5. Draft the verdict. It must be quotable in 1--2 sentences and explicitly connect to the book's framework.
6. Draft the calibration: what the evidence does not show, or what rival explanations exist.
7. Draft the bridge (if prescribed by cluster and position).
8. Verify all six universal moves are present:
   - [ ] Claim announced in first two paragraphs
   - [ ] Theory connected to the framework
   - [ ] Evidence in bounded, analytically purposeful subsections
   - [ ] Verdict explicitly stated
   - [ ] Calibration present
   - [ ] Bridge present (if applicable)

**Pause:** Review the full draft against the structural plan.

---

### Phase 3: Calibration and Revision

**Goal:** Test the draft against cluster benchmarks and the six universal moves. Revise as needed.

**Calibration checks:**

| Test | Pass Criteria |
|------|---------------|
| **Total word count** | Within cluster range |
| **Subsection count** | Within cluster range |
| **Quote density** | Within cluster range; check that quotes serve analytical purposes, not mere illustration |
| **Opening type** | Matches cluster prescription |
| **Verdict placement** | In labeled conclusion section; quotable in 1--2 sentences |
| **Theory connection** | Present and explicit; reader can identify which theoretical variables are being tested |
| **Position signals** | Chapter announces its position (first, middle, last, anomalous) within the first page |

**Universal move tests:**

| Test | Question | Fix if Failing |
|------|----------|---------------|
| **Claim test** | Can the reader state the chapter's argument by paragraph 2? | Move the claim earlier; add "This chapter argues that..." or equivalent |
| **Theory test** | Does the reader know what the framework predicts for this case? | Add a theory-connection paragraph with explicit variable mapping |
| **Evidence test** | Do the subsections have analytical purposes, not just topics? | Rename subsections to signal arguments; add analytical framing at subsection openings |
| **Verdict test** | Can a reader quote the finding in 1--2 sentences? | Rewrite the verdict paragraph with a signal phrase and explicit claim |
| **Calibration test** | Has the chapter acknowledged limits or alternatives? | Add a qualification paragraph near the verdict |
| **Bridge test** | Does the reader know where the argument goes next? | Add a forward-pointing sentence at the chapter's end |

**Revision priorities** (in order):
1. Verdict clarity -- if the verdict is not quotable, the chapter fails its comparative function
2. Theory connection -- if the reader cannot see how this case relates to the framework, it is a standalone essay, not a parallel case chapter
3. Opening engagement -- the first page must establish the case and its stakes
4. Evidence organization -- subsections must do argumentative work
5. Calibration -- acknowledging limits signals rigor
6. Word count -- trim or expand to match cluster benchmarks

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. The case chapter that forgets it is part of a series

If the chapter could stand alone as an independent essay with no reference to the comparative framework, it has failed its parallel function. Every case chapter must explicitly connect to the theory and to the other cases in the set.

### 2. The template that feels robotic

Mechanism-Tracing chapters use rigid templates, but even Kadivar varies his evidence density and narrative emphasis across cases. If every subsection reads identically across chapters, the template has become a cage. Allow the case's own texture to show through within the template.

### 3. The ethnographic chapter that never delivers a verdict

Brown-Saracino's chapters are deeply immersive, but every one ends with an explicit statement of what the city demonstrates. If an ethnographic chapter accumulates scenes without ever stating a finding, it is a field report, not an analytical chapter.

### 4. The verdict that does not map onto the framework

If the verdict uses language disconnected from the book's theoretical variables, the reader cannot integrate the finding into the comparative argument. Mucciaroni maps every verdict onto his three hypotheses. Patashnik maps every verdict onto his three mechanisms (institutional reconfiguration, creative destruction, policy feedback). The verdict must use the framework's own language.

### 5. The opening that buries the case

If the chapter opens with three pages of historical background before the reader knows which case they are reading about, the opening has failed. Every cluster prescribes an opening that identifies the case and its stakes within the first two paragraphs.

### 6. The anomalous case that is not flagged as anomalous

Kadivar opens the Tunisia chapter by explicitly naming it as an anomaly: "Exploring the case of Tunisia thus presents an opportunity to investigate alternative pathways." If the anomalous case is presented without signaling its exceptional status, the reader will be confused about why the evidence contradicts the theory.

### 7. Overly long background sections

If the pre-reform situation, historical development, or background section exceeds 25% of the chapter, it has become the chapter. Background serves the analysis; it is not the analysis itself.
