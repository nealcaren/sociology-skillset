# Section-by-Section Drafting Recipes for Parallel Case Study Chapters

Detailed instructions for drafting each section of a parallel case study chapter. Each recipe specifies what the section accomplishes, its internal paragraph structure, and real examples from four books showing how published authors execute each move.

Use this file alongside `paragraph-functions.md` (for paragraph-level guidance). This file tells you how the paragraphs combine into sections, and how the sections combine into a complete chapter.

---

## Section 1: The Opening

**Target length:** 500--1,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 2--5

### What This Section Accomplishes

The opening identifies the case, announces the chapter's claim, and signals the case's position in the comparative architecture. By the end of the opening section, the reader must know: (1) which case they are reading about, (2) what the chapter will argue, and (3) how this case relates to the others in the book.

### Recipe by Cluster

#### Analytical Case Opening (Patashnik model)

**Structure:** Outcome statement --> theory-connection --> puzzle or preview of the chapter's arc.

**Paragraph 1:** Name the case and state the outcome. Be direct. The reader does not need historical context before knowing what happened.

Patashnik (Tax Reform) opens with the outcome in sentence one:

> Major policy reforms can be unraveled without ever being repealed. A leading example has been the gradual erosion of the Tax Reform Act of 1986 (TRA), which has been justly described as 'one of the most extraordinary economic policy initiatives of the 20th century.'

The chapter continues by documenting the erosion -- Bush raising capital gains preferences, Clinton raising rates, Bush II creating new breaks -- before delivering the verdict: "the 1986 reform has been eroded by subsequent changes."

Patashnik (Airline Deregulation) opens by naming the case and its pre-reform context, then arrives at the puzzle:

> The Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 is a leading case of general-interest reform. ... Airline deregulation has proved highly sustainable. This is a genuine puzzle for three reasons.

The three reasons are enumerated: faded political conditions, turbulent transition, accusations of monopoly. Then:

> The central claim of this chapter is that airline deregulation has endured because policymaking has gradually but unmistakably been reconfigured, making it increasingly costly and unattractive to go back.

**Paragraph 2:** Connect to theory. Map the case onto the framework's variables. Patashnik's second paragraph for Tax Reform states:

> The TRA's unraveling was predictable because the reform was built upon weak political foundations. While some hoped the reform's passage would initiate a new era of tax policymaking, the TRA failed to change political incentives. It did not recast institutions to constrain future rent-seekers. ... The TRA also did not generate positive policy feedbacks.

Every variable from the framework (institutional reconfiguration, creative destruction, policy feedback) is named and assessed in this single paragraph.

**Total opening:** 500--800 words, 2--3 paragraphs.

---

#### Ethnographic Case Opening (Brown-Saracino model)

**Structure:** First-person arrival --> sensory accumulation --> research puzzle --> (theory connection comes later, in a dedicated subsection).

**Paragraph 1:** Arrive at the field site. Use first person. Pile up specific, sensory details that convey the place's character.

Brown-Saracino (Ithaca):

> In 2006 I moved to Ithaca, New York, to teach at Cornell University. Before moving, I knew what many northeasterners might know of Ithaca: that it was famous for its steep ravines and gorges; that it was home to Cayuga Lake, Cornell University, and Ithaca College; that visitors flocked to the Moosewood, a natural foods restaurant and cooperative; and that it was remote.

**Paragraphs 2--3:** Continue the discovery. Shift from general place description to the phenomenon the chapter will analyze.

> Not as obvious, but just as important, was another discovery: the unmistakable presence of a large population of lesbian, bisexual, and/or queer (LBQ) individuals in and around Ithaca. I watched women holding hands with abandon. Playgrounds often contain two-mom families alongside heterosexual couples.

**Paragraph 4:** Pivot to the research question. Move from observation to inquiry.

> However, what neither the data nor feel-good stories like the Advocate's could tell me is why Ithaca possesses a disproportionately high number of lesbian couples. These sources communicate even less about what it feels like to be LBQ in Ithaca.

**For later city chapters,** the opening must signal the case's position. San Luis Obispo opens with explicit expectation violation:

> I assumed that my time in San Luis Obispo would affirm the lessons I had learned in Ithaca. ... But I was very wrong.

Greenfield opens by naming the book's pattern and then announcing the complication:

> This book has already sketched how LBQ residents respond to the ecology of the city in which they live. ... In Greenfield, however, something different is found: there is more than one identity culture.

**Total opening:** 800--1,000 words, 4--5 paragraphs.

---

#### Biographical-Vignette Case Opening (Mucciaroni model)

**Structure:** Vignette 1 --> Vignette 2 --> "As these stories illustrate..." pivot.

**Paragraphs 1--2:** Tell two real stories of individuals affected by the policy under study. Each story should illustrate a different aspect of the issue.

Mucciaroni (Sodomy Laws):

> On May 18, 1970, James McConnell and Jack Baker applied for a marriage license in Minneapolis. After the two men announced publicly that they were gay, the board of regents of the University of Minnesota voted unanimously to withdraw its offer of a librarian's job to McConnell.

Then the second vignette:

> On the night of September 17, 1998, someone called the Harris County, Texas, police to report that 'a black male was going crazy' and 'was armed with a gun' in an apartment located in a lower-middle-class neighborhood of Houston. ... They found John Lawrence and Tyrone Garner having anal sex.

Mucciaroni (Military):

> Michelle Douglas joined the Canadian military in 1986 and graduated at the top of her class in basic training. ... She admitted that she was gay and had engaged in same-sex conduct but refused to identify other homosexuals in the military.

Then:

> Alastair Gamble studied German and Latin in high school and at Emory University in Atlanta. ... During a surprise inspection in the middle of the night in April 2002, the military caught Gamble sleeping with his boyfriend.

**Paragraph 3:** Pivot from stories to stakes. Name the policy issue and its significance.

> As these stories illustrate, sodomy laws had profound impacts on the lives of some gay men. Their elimination could have a similarly profound impact on the gay rights movement.

For the military chapter:

> Virtually alone among developed democracies, the United States bans gays and lesbians from serving openly in the military. The failure to end the ban in the 1990s was a major political defeat for the gay rights movement and its allies.

**Key technique:** The first vignette shows harm; the second shows either a contrasting outcome or the legal challenge that eventually changed things. The pairing creates a before/after or success/failure structure that embodies the chapter's analytical puzzle.

**Total opening:** 600--800 words, 3 paragraphs.

---

#### Mechanism-Tracing Case Opening (Kadivar model)

**Structure:** Case-selection rationale --> outcome variation --> methodological statement.

**Paragraph 1:** Name the cases and their outcome variation.

Kadivar (Ch. 3, three cases):

> South Africa, Poland, and Pakistan ushered in democratic transitions around the same time. The South African regime started its negotiations with Nelson Mandela and his party in 1990. In Poland, the Communist regime convened the historic roundtables with the opposition in 1989. The Pakistani generals negotiated the terms of transition with Benazir Bhutto, the leader of the opposition, in 1988.

Kadivar (Ch. 5, anomalous case):

> The democratic transition in Tunisia occurred after a short period of protest, but in contrast to Egypt, the transition was completed. ... Exploring the case of Tunisia thus presents an opportunity to investigate alternative pathways for democratic consolidation than those presented in previous chapters.

**Paragraph 2:** State the purpose and the selection logic.

> In this chapter, I explore the mechanisms by which unarmed campaigns may affect the survival of young democracies. I chose these three case studies because they exhibit variations in the duration of nonviolent mobilization and the survival of democracy.

**Paragraph 3:** List the cases with their outcome classification.

> - South Africa (1994--present) with a lengthy mobilization and successful democratic consolidation
> - Poland (1989--present) with a long period of mobilization and successful democratic consolidation
> - Pakistan (1988--99) with no mobilization and democratic failure

**Total opening:** 400--600 words, 3--4 paragraphs.

---

### Common Opening Mistakes

- **Opening with background before naming the case.** Three paragraphs of historical context before the reader knows which policy, city, or country is being discussed. Name the case in sentence one or two.
- **Opening with theory.** "As the theory from Chapter 2 predicts..." -- the theory connection belongs in paragraph 2 or later, not paragraph 1. Start with the world, not the framework.
- **Failing to signal position.** If this is the second, third, or fourth case chapter and the opening does not acknowledge the prior cases, the reader loses the comparative thread.

## Section 2: The Theory Connection

**Target length:** 200--500 words | **Paragraphs:** 1--2

### What This Section Accomplishes

The theory connection tells the reader how this case maps onto the book's comparative framework. It names the theoretical variables and states what the theory predicts (or fails to predict) for this case. In some clusters, this section is a standalone paragraph; in others, it opens a dedicated subsection.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### The Immediate Variable-Mapping (Patashnik)

Patashnik places the theory connection in the chapter's second paragraph, mapping every variable from the framework:

> The TRA's unraveling was predictable because the reform was built upon weak political foundations. ... It did not recast institutions to constrain future rent-seekers. ... The TRA also did not generate positive policy feedbacks. ... In sum, the TRA represented a stunning victory of general interests over narrow ones ... but the reform failed to durably reconfigure the political dynamic.

Three variables (institutional reconfiguration, creative destruction, policy feedback) are named and assessed. The reader knows exactly which dials are being tested.

#### The Ecological-Comparison Frame (Brown-Saracino)

Brown-Saracino's theory connection comes in an early subsection, not the opening. It works through cross-case comparison:

> How can two college towns offer such vast differences in identity and ties? If feelings of acceptance, an abundance of LBQ residents, and talk of the city as a beacon of acceptance produce Ithaca's post-identity politics and ambient community, subtle differences in city ecology propel identity politics and lesbian community in San Luis Obispo.

The theoretical variable (city ecology) is named through the contrast between two cases.

#### The Self-Referential Frame (Mucciaroni)

Mucciaroni signals the theory connection with explicit self-reference to prior chapters:

> Following the kind of analysis presented in the last two chapters, we focus our attention on the preferences of key policymakers and the institutional venue in which they addressed the military ban issue.

The phrase "following the kind of analysis" tells the reader that this chapter applies the same analytical template.

#### The Methodological-Template Frame (Kadivar)

Kadivar's theory connection explains that the chapter's subsection structure *is* the theory:

> I organize the narratives here according to five major components drawn from the theoretical framework: prodemocracy mobilization and its organizational infrastructure, democratic transition, the effect of the mobilization on leadership change, the effect of mobilization on civil society, and the role of these factors in the survival or failure of the new democracy.

#### The Anomaly-Resolution Frame (Kadivar, Tunisia)

For anomalous cases, the theory connection acknowledges the mismatch and explains how the case nonetheless aligns with the book's deeper logic:

> In previous chapters, I argued that long episodes of prodemocracy mobilization create organizational capacities beneficial for democratic consolidation. The UGTT, however, existed before the advent of the 2011 revolution. ... The fact that the UGTT, a legacy organization from the authoritarian era, played a crucial role in Tunisia's emerging democracy highlights the relationship between the political institutions of the ancien regime and their impact on the postauthoritarian civil society.

### Common Mistakes

- **Omitting the theory connection entirely.** A case chapter without an explicit connection to the framework is a standalone essay. Even Brown-Saracino, whose chapters are deeply ethnographic, states the connection within the first few pages.
- **Using generic language.** "The theory predicts..." without naming which specific variables or mechanisms are at work. Name them.
- **Placing the theory connection too late.** If the reader has read 3,000 words of evidence before learning how it connects to the framework, they have been processing the evidence without a guide.

## Section 3: The Evidence Body

**Target length:** 6,000--12,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 25--50 | **Proportion:** 60--70% of the chapter

### What This Section Accomplishes

The evidence body is where the case is presented. It occupies the vast majority of the chapter. Its organization -- the subsection structure, the sequence of evidence types, the placement of key analytical moves -- determines whether the chapter works as a unit in the comparative architecture.

### Organizing Principles by Cluster

#### Analytical Case: Thematic Subsections with Chronological Sub-Threads

Patashnik organizes the Tax Reform chapter into 11 subsections, each addressing a distinct analytical dimension: pre-reform situation, adoption politics, erosion pattern, new tax breaks, public skepticism, economic effects, political dynamics, tax shelters, terms of debate, conclusions. Within each subsection, evidence unfolds chronologically.

The Airline Deregulation chapter uses a two-part structure: (1) historical narrative of adoption and aftermath, then (2) a series of "stress tests" -- political challenges to the reform that failed. This stress-test structure is distinctive to success cases; failure cases document the erosion directly.

**Recipe:**
1. Background/pre-reform subsection: what the world looked like before the reform (1,000--2,000 words)
2. Adoption narrative: how the reform was enacted (1,500--2,500 words)
3. 3--5 analytical subsections, each testing one dimension of the framework (800--1,500 words each)
4. For success cases: a "stress test" section examining specific reversion threats (1,000--2,000 words)
5. For failure cases: a "what did not change" section documenting the absence of expected mechanisms (1,000--2,000 words)

Transition between subsections using analytical pivots:

> Two decades after the 1986 Act, the federal tax code still bears vestiges of reform. ... But if the 1986 Act has not been reversed, its accomplishments nonetheless have been eroded.

> Rent-seeking behavior has not been eliminated from the airline industry -- witness the industry's lobbying effort following the 9/11 attacks -- but the old iron triangle relationship between the industry, lawmakers, and regulators has not reconstituted itself. Why not?

---

#### Ethnographic Case: Thematic Subsections Anchored by Scenes

Brown-Saracino organizes each city chapter around thematic subsections that explore dimensions of the identity culture: what migrants find, how they practice integration or identity politics, what costs and benefits they perceive, and the limits of the dominant pattern.

Within each subsection, evidence moves in a consistent pattern:
1. **Field scene** -- the author observes a specific event (Farmers Market, dance party, chips-n-chat)
2. **Interview cluster** -- 3--5 quotes from named informants illustrating the same theme
3. **Analytical interpretation** -- the author's synthesis connecting observations to the framework

**Recipe:**
1. "What they find" subsection: the migrants' initial encounter with the city (1,000--2,000 words)
2. 2--3 thematic subsections on the dominant identity culture, each anchored by a scene (2,000--3,000 words each)
3. A complicating/limits subsection that tests the chapter's main finding (1,000--2,000 words)
4. Conclusion subsection: verdict + bridge (500--1,500 words)

The pivotal analytical moment in each chapter is a sustained ethnographic scene that functions as a natural experiment. In San Luis Obispo, the downtown dance party reveals hidden cliques; in Ithaca, the pie contest demonstrates integration in action. These scenes are not illustrations -- they are evidence.

Transitions between subsections use conceptual pivots:

> The manner of relating with neighbors -- both heterosexual and LBQ -- that these dimensions of city ecology cultivate helps to encourage a second surprise, one that we'll see throughout the book: a transformation of ways of thinking, talking, and feeling about the self.

> The rest of the chapter considers the paradoxical position many in Ithaca find themselves to be in, as they navigate the benefits and burdens of integration and acceptance.

---

#### Biographical-Vignette Case: Theory-Driven Categories with Chronological Sub-Threads

Mucciaroni organizes each case chapter around the book's institutional variables rather than chronology. The Sodomy Laws chapter moves: historical development --> federalism and legislative track --> stakeholder role (ALI) --> judicial track --> explaining the Court's reversal --> conclusion. The Military chapter moves: historical background --> institutional explanation --> military as stakeholder --> Congress --> international comparison --> U.S. judicial behavior --> conclusion.

The distinctive feature is the internal hypothesis-testing structure. The Military chapter includes a full subsection ("Explaining the Court's Reversal in Lawrence") that tests three explanations -- ideological replacement, generational turnover, political adjustment -- using the same evidence to adjudicate among them.

**Recipe:**
1. Historical development of the issue (1,500--2,500 words)
2. 3--4 subsections organized by theoretical variable, each presenting evidence for or against the framework's predictions (2,000--3,000 words each)
3. Comparative cases (cross-national or cross-group) as an additional test (1,000--3,000 words)
4. Conclusion: hypothesis-by-hypothesis verdict (500--1,500 words)

Transitions between subsections use predictive language:

> If our argument about the key role of legislative power and preferences is correct, we should find a very different institutional context in nations that have dropped their bans on gays in their militaries.

> Why did the Supreme Court declare state sodomy laws unconstitutional after it concluded just the opposite seventeen years earlier?

---

#### Mechanism-Tracing Case: Rigid Template Repeated Across Cases

Kadivar uses the same five subsection headings for each case: Campaign, Transition, Leadership Change, Civil Society, Political Outcome. This template is derived directly from the theoretical framework and announced in the chapter introduction.

**Recipe:**
1. For each case within the chapter, apply the template:
   - Campaign: organizational development of the movement (800--1,200 words)
   - Transition: how mobilization shaped negotiations (600--1,000 words)
   - Leadership Change: whether movement leaders entered government (400--600 words)
   - Civil Society: post-transition life of movement organizations (400--800 words)
   - Political Outcome: brief verdict on democratic durability (200--400 words)
2. Mechanisms synthesis: numbered enumeration of identified mechanisms (500--1,000 words)
3. Conclusion: verdict + theoretical implications (500--1,000 words)

Transitions between cases use parallel syntax:

> Poland also presents a case of sustained mobilization and democratic survival, although the effects of its long campaign differ in some ways from South Africa's.

> Pakistan also experienced a democratic transition in the late twentieth century, but in contrast with South Africa or Poland, the transition occurred without a strong prodemocracy mobilization and failed to consolidate.

---

### Evidence Density Patterns

The corpus reveals a consistent pattern: authors deploy their most varied and strongest evidence for their most important theoretical demonstrations. Patashnik's strongest positive case (Acid Rain) has EPA data, market statistics, compliance records, and regulatory documents. Kadivar's anomalous case (Tunisia) has survey regression results, translated Arabic-language press, and organizational data. Brown-Saracino's most complex case (Greenfield) has the highest quote density (~50--60) and the most extended individual profiles.

| Case Position | Evidence Density |
|---------------|-----------------|
| First / canonical | Moderate -- establishing the pattern |
| Middle / confirming | Moderate -- extending the pattern |
| Last / complex / anomalous | Highest -- the most varied evidence, the most elaborate subsection structure |

### Common Mistakes

- **Organizing purely by chronology.** Chronology is a useful tool within subsections, but a chapter organized as a pure timeline (1960--1970--1980--1990) has no analytical structure. Use thematic subsections with chronological threads inside them.
- **Subsections without analytical purposes.** A subsection titled "The 1990s" tells the reader nothing about the argument. "The Failure of Legislative Repeal" tells the reader what the section argues.
- **Evidence without interpretation.** Every evidence paragraph must contain an interpretive sentence connecting the data to the framework. Data dumps -- paragraphs of facts without analysis -- slow the reader and obscure the argument.
- **Uneven subsection lengths.** If one subsection is 3,000 words and another is 300, the proportions signal that the short subsection is underdeveloped. Aim for rough parity (800--2,000 words per subsection).

## Section 4: The Verdict

**Target length:** 500--1,500 words | **Paragraphs:** 2--5

### What This Section Accomplishes

The verdict states what this case demonstrates about the book's theoretical framework. It is the most important paragraph in the chapter -- the sentence a reader will quote to summarize the finding. The verdict must be explicit, must use the framework's language, and must be quotable.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### The Mechanism-Enumeration Verdict (Patashnik)

Patashnik's verdict paragraphs enumerate the specific mechanisms that explain the outcome. For the failure case:

> While the TRA was a stunning piece of reform legislation, it did not successfully reconfigure the political economy of tax policymaking. Its failures as breakthrough policy are both multiple and mutually reinforcing. The TRA did nothing to disempower tax lobbyists or slow the proliferation of new groups seeking privileged tax treatment. The reform did not raise the political transaction costs to the government of creating or expanding tax loopholes. ... Positive policy feedback -- self-reinforcing shifts in ideas, group identities, and political dynamics -- has been notable for its absence.

For the success case:

> The reform's sustainability has been as impressive as its original enactment. The emissions trading market has lasted not because it has 'frozen in place' environmental outcomes, or even pollution control technologies, but rather because it has given firms the flexibility to find the cheapest emissions control methods in exchange for the achievement of overall pollution reduction goals. That political bargain has stuck ... because the Acid Rain Program has worked well on the ground, because it has been embedded in well-designed governance structures, and because it has generated a new constellation of interests that have developed stakes in the program.

The "not because ... but because" structure is distinctive: it rules out simpler explanations before stating the correct one.

#### The Hypothesis-Testing Verdict (Mucciaroni)

Mucciaroni's verdicts explicitly map findings onto the book's numbered hypotheses:

> Our examination of the repeal of sodomy laws supports the hypotheses presented in chapter 1 for the most part. ... Three reasons account for this outcome: (1) reformers took advantage of variable conditions in the states that were often conducive to the spread of repeal from 1960 to 2000; (2) the presence of influential stakeholders in the legal profession who favored repeal; and (3) eventual judicial cooperation in the repeal movement.

For the failure case, the verdict uses explicit comparative language:

> The military case is in crucial respects the reverse image of the sodomy repeal and adoption cases. Despite public support for lifting the ban, it remains in place. Opponents rebuffed the only serious effort to get the ban removed and have kept the issue off the agenda ever since.

#### The Reflective Verdict (Brown-Saracino)

Brown-Saracino's verdicts state what the city "demonstrates," "reveals," or "reminds":

> Being LBQ in San Luis Obispo clearly means something vastly different than it does in Ithaca. The enormous contrast between the residents in these two cities reminds that there is no neat chronological progression out of the closet or away from gay and lesbian identities. ... At least in San Luis Obispo, even amid a string of legal and cultural victories, and with unprecedented mainstream acceptance of alternate lifestyles, identity politics -- predicated on notions of a common 'lesbian' identity -- is alive and well.

For the anomalous case (Greenfield), the verdict rules out an alternative before stating the finding:

> We learn, for instance, that chain migration -- the movement to a city of one woman, informed by the movement of another before her and so on -- does not neatly account for the emergence of newcomers' post-identity-politics orientation. ... Greenfield's story is not one of a neat chain migration of migrants with inside knowledge of local identities. As in the other sites, newcomers report arriving in Greenfield with one approach to their identity, only to find that it takes new shape as they build a life in a new city.

#### The Mechanism-Extension Verdict (Kadivar, Tunisia)

The anomalous case verdict extends the theory rather than simply confirming it:

> The important role of the UGTT highlights an alternate pathway for democratization that is not marked by a lengthy unarmed mobilization. In Tunisia, a short mobilization liberated a co-opted civil organization from the tight control of the former regime and enabled that organization to play a pivotal role in the transition period. In the absence of organizations built from lengthy waves of mobilization, civil society organizations surviving from the authoritarian period, also known as legacy organizations, could improve the likelihood of successful democratization.

### Verdict Structure by Position

| Position | Verdict Logic | Example |
|----------|--------------|---------|
| First case | "This case establishes that..." | Patashnik (Tax Reform): canonical failure mapped onto all three variables |
| Middle case | "Like [prior case], this case confirms / extends..." | Mucciaroni (Adoption): "all three institutional characteristics expected to dampen resistance come together" |
| Negative case | "This case is the reverse of..." | Mucciaroni (Military): "reverse image of the sodomy repeal and adoption cases" |
| Last / strongest | "This case represents the strongest form of..." | Patashnik (Acid Rain): "a case of successful policy reconfiguration -- the strongest form of reform sustainability" |
| Anomalous case | "This case highlights an alternate pathway..." | Kadivar (Tunisia): "alternate pathway ... legacy organizations" |

### Common Mistakes

- **Verdict that does not use the framework's language.** If the book's framework specifies three mechanisms and the verdict discusses something else, the reader cannot integrate the finding into the comparative argument.
- **Verdict distributed across five paragraphs.** The finding must be concentrated enough to quote. Even if elaboration follows, there must be one paragraph that captures the core finding.
- **Verdict that is merely descriptive.** "This case shows that tax reform was eroded" is a description. Patashnik's "the reform failed to durably reconfigure the political dynamic" is an analytical verdict that names *why* the erosion occurred, using the framework's concept of reconfiguration.

## Section 5: The Bridge

**Target length:** 50--300 words | **Paragraphs:** 1--3

### What This Section Accomplishes

The bridge connects this chapter to what comes next -- either the next case chapter or the book's conclusion. It signals what question remains open and previews how the next chapter will address it.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### The Explicit Forward-Looking Bridge (Brown-Saracino)

Brown-Saracino closes her first city chapter by challenging its own findings' universality:

> The following chapters, though, give pause to any notion of the universality of this shift for LBQ residents and others, as well as about the precise relation between such shifts and orientations to and practices of community. Despite the sense articulated by many Ithacans that they are caught in a moment of broad and universal transformation, Ithaca is one way, but far from the only way.

Her last city chapter bridges to the synthesis:

> When local conditions change, identities and ties change, too. Sexual identities are not enshrined in time or place; they are responsive social identities that take form via context-specific interaction. It is to the question of precisely how city features shape these interactions, and, ultimately, self-understanding, that the next and final chapter turns.

#### The Explicit Sequential Bridge (Kadivar)

Kadivar names the next chapters and their cases:

> But how does a democratic transition unfold following short bursts of mass mobilization? In the next two chapters, I examine the failed and successful transitions in Egypt and Tunisia, respectively, to tackle this question.

#### The Implicit Bridge (Patashnik)

Patashnik uses no explicit bridge sentences. Each chapter ends with its verdict and a closing quotation:

> 'Tax experts now regard the 1986 act as a promise failed.'

> Deregulation has endured ... because it has changed the menu of policy solutions.

The comparative framework, established in the book's introduction, provides the implicit connection. Readers track the cross-case comparison themselves.

#### The Next-Chapter-Opening Bridge (Mucciaroni)

Mucciaroni's case chapters end without a bridge, but the next chapter's opening paragraph performs the function:

> The three preceding chapters demonstrated the critical role of institutions in the legalization of homosexual conduct and gay adoption and in the failure to lift the ban on gays in the military. ... What about the policy responses to gay rights advocates' demands for same-sex marriage, civil unions, employment nondiscrimination, and hate crimes legislation?

### Bridge Conventions by Position

| Position | Bridge Type | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| First case | Challenge to universality | Brown-Saracino: "Ithaca is one way, but far from the only way" |
| Middle case | Forward-pointing question or next-case preview | Kadivar: "In the next two chapters, I examine..." |
| Last case (before conclusion) | Bridge to synthesis or theoretical extension | Brown-Saracino: "It is to the question of precisely how..." |
| Last case (final in book) | Close on the verdict; no bridge needed | Patashnik (Acid Rain): ends with the verdict paragraph |

### Common Mistakes

- **Introducing new evidence in the bridge.** The bridge synthesizes and points forward; it does not introduce new findings.
- **Formulaic bridges.** "The next chapter will examine..." repeated across every chapter becomes invisible. Vary the bridge structure: challenge to universality, open question, theoretical preview.
- **No bridge at all for first or middle cases.** Last cases can end on the verdict, but first and middle cases that end abruptly leave the reader without guidance about the comparative argument's trajectory.

## Position-Specific Recipes

### Recipe for the First Case Chapter

The first case establishes the baseline. Everything that follows will be read against it.

**Opening:** Broad framing or discovery-based. Do NOT open with a complicating anomaly. The first case should be the clearest demonstration of the theory's logic.

**Evidence:** Present the most straightforward application of the framework. Use this chapter to teach the reader how to read the subsequent chapters. If the framework has three variables, show all three clearly.

**Verdict:** State what the case demonstrates, with clear mapping onto the framework.

**Bridge:** Signal that this case is instructive but not universal. Brown-Saracino: "Ithaca is one way, but far from the only way."

**Benchmarks:** Word count at the lower-middle range for the cluster. Quote density moderate. Subsection count moderate.

---

### Recipe for a Middle Case Chapter

Middle cases accumulate evidence. Their function is to extend, replicate, or complicate the pattern.

**Opening:** Build on what came before. Signal the case's position: "Like [prior case], this case..." or "Unlike [prior case], this case surprises by..."

Brown-Saracino (San Luis Obispo):
> I assumed that my time in San Luis Obispo would affirm the lessons I had learned in Ithaca. ... But I was very wrong.

**Evidence:** Use the same analytical template but highlight what is different. Middle cases are where the framework's flexibility (or rigidity) is tested.

**Verdict:** State the finding in comparative terms. Mucciaroni (Adoption): "all three institutional characteristics expected to dampen resistance come together."

**Bridge:** Forward-pointing. What does this case leave unresolved? What must the next case address?

**Benchmarks:** Similar to first case in length and density.

---

### Recipe for the Last / Most Complex Case Chapter

The last case is the book's most theoretically generative. It earns the most pages, the most varied evidence, and the most elaborate analysis.

**Opening:** Acknowledge what has been established and announce what this chapter adds. Brown-Saracino (Greenfield): "This book has already sketched how LBQ residents respond to the ecology of the city in which they live. ... In Greenfield, however, something different is found."

**Evidence:** Deploy the most varied evidence here. Brown-Saracino's Greenfield chapter has ~50--60 quotes and the most extended individual profiles. Patashnik's Acid Rain chapter has EPA data, market statistics, compliance records, and regulatory documents. This is where the author shows mastery.

**Verdict:** State the finding with the broadest theoretical implications. Patashnik (Acid Rain): "the strongest form of reform sustainability." Brown-Saracino (Greenfield): three lessons "not available from the other three cities."

**Bridge:** Bridge to the conclusion or synthesis chapter. This is the last time the evidence speaks; make it count.

**Benchmarks:** Longest chapter in the set. Highest evidence density. Most subsections.

---

### Recipe for the Anomalous Case Chapter

The anomalous case extends or qualifies the theory.

**Opening:** Name the anomaly explicitly. Kadivar: "Exploring the case of Tunisia thus presents an opportunity to investigate alternative pathways." Brown-Saracino: "In Greenfield, however, something different is found: there is more than one identity culture."

**Theory connection:** Acknowledge that the case does not fit the main prediction, then explain how it aligns with the theory's deeper logic. Kadivar: "Even though Tunisia may seem an anomalous case, a closer look reveals an alignment with the general argument proposed in this book."

**Evidence:** Include a "rival explanations" subsection that tests alternative accounts before presenting the author's explanation. Kadivar's Tunisia chapter has a full "Other Explanations" section reviewing four alternatives.

**Verdict:** State the theoretical extension explicitly. Kadivar: "An alternate pathway for democratization ... legacy organizations could improve the likelihood of successful democratization."

**Bridge:** Close on the theoretical extension; acknowledge the case's limits.

**Benchmarks:** Moderate length. Highest methodological explicitness. Highest density of alternative-explanations engagement.

### Recipe for the Negative Case Chapter

The negative case demonstrates what happens when the key factor is absent.

**Opening:** Signal the methodological logic explicitly. Mucciaroni (Military): introduces a case where "despite public support" the outcome was failure. The framing tells the reader this is a case of absence, not presence.

**Evidence:** Organize evidence to demonstrate the absence of the factors that produced success in the positive cases. Mucciaroni structures the Military chapter around the institutional variables that blocked reform: hostile stakeholder (military), unfavorable venue (national government), passive courts.

**Verdict:** Deliver the necessity claim: "This case is in crucial respects the reverse image of the [positive cases]." The negative case demonstrates that without the key factor, the outcome does not materialize.

**Bridge:** Summarize the full set of cases examined so far, or let the next chapter's opening perform this function.

**Benchmarks:** Length comparable to or slightly longer than the positive cases (the negative case often requires more elaborate institutional analysis to explain why the favorable outcome did not occur).

## Quick-Reference Drafting Checklist

- [ ] **Opening:** Case named in first two sentences. Claim announced by paragraph 2--3. Position signaled.
- [ ] **Theory connection:** Framework variables named. Prediction stated for this case. Uses the framework's own language.
- [ ] **Evidence body:** Organized in analytically purposeful subsections. Every evidence paragraph contains interpretation. Quotes framed and interpreted. Transitions between subsections advance the argument.
- [ ] **Verdict:** Quotable in 1--2 sentences. Uses signal phrase. Maps onto the framework's variables.
- [ ] **Calibration:** Limits or alternatives acknowledged. At least one rival explanation addressed.
- [ ] **Bridge:** Reader knows where the argument goes next (or chapter closes on the verdict if it is the last case).

### Cross-Section Checks

- [ ] The claim announced in the opening is the claim the verdict delivers.
- [ ] The theoretical variables named in the theory connection are the variables assessed in the verdict.
- [ ] The evidence in the body is organized by the same categories named in the theory connection.
- [ ] The bridge (if present) follows from the verdict's implications.
- [ ] The chapter's word count and subsection count fall within the cluster's benchmarks.
