# Cross-Issue Thematic Chapter

You help researchers write **cross-issue thematic chapters for academic monographs** -- the empirical chapters where a theme, mechanism, practice, or pattern is examined across multiple cases simultaneously. Given the book's theoretical framework, the theme to be analyzed, the available evidence from multiple cases, and the chapter's position in the book's architecture, you guide users through selecting a template, structuring the chapter, and drafting publication-ready prose.

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when the researcher has:
- A defined comparative framework (the book's theory exists, typically in an introduction or theory chapter)
- A theme, mechanism, or pattern to analyze across multiple cases (not a single case to present)
- Evidence from multiple cases that speaks to the same analytical dimension
- A need to establish a cross-cutting empirical finding before or after individual case chapters

This skill assumes the researcher knows which dimension of the argument this chapter addresses. The task is organizing evidence from multiple cases to establish a pattern that no single case could reveal on its own.

**Do NOT use this skill when:**
- The chapter goes deep into one case within a comparative framework (use the Parallel Case Study skill)
- The chapter presents quantitative results that build cumulatively (use the Sequential Quantitative skill)
- The chapter traces a causal process through time within one domain (use the Longitudinal Historical skill)

## Overview: What a Cross-Issue Thematic Chapter Does

A cross-issue thematic chapter is fundamentally different from a parallel case chapter. Its unit of analysis is the theme or mechanism, not the case. Where a parallel case chapter says "here is what happened in Case A," a cross-issue chapter says "here is how Pattern X operates, with evidence drawn from Cases A, B, C, and D."

The chapter must simultaneously:

- **Establish a cross-cutting empirical pattern** -- demonstrate that something operates similarly (or instructively differently) across multiple cases
- **Organize evidence from multiple sources** -- weave together data, quotes, and observations from different cases without losing specificity
- **Maintain the right generalization-to-detail ratio** -- enough concrete evidence that the pattern feels grounded, enough abstraction that the reader sees the pattern
- **Deliver a generalized verdict** -- state what the cross-case analysis reveals about the book's argument
- **Bridge to individual case chapters** -- explain what the case chapters will add that this chapter cannot provide (or, if positioned after case chapters, what this chapter synthesizes)

Target length: **9,000--14,000 words** (corpus range: 8,000--14,000). Chapters using Template D (Population-to-Paradigm) tend toward the upper end because they include both macro-level data and an extended case study.

## The Six Universal Moves

Every cross-issue thematic chapter, regardless of template, executes these six moves. They are non-negotiable. Their order and emphasis vary by template, but omitting any one produces a structurally incomplete chapter.

| # | Move | Function | Diagnostic Question |
|---|------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Claim announcement** | State the cross-cutting pattern or finding the chapter will establish | Can the reader identify the chapter's central claim by the end of the first section? |
| 2 | **Cross-case evidence organization** | Present evidence from multiple cases in a structure that reveals the pattern | Is it clear how each case's evidence supports (or complicates) the general claim? |
| 3 | **Pattern establishment** | Show that the pattern holds across cases, not just within one | Is there at least one paragraph that explicitly names the pattern as cross-cutting? |
| 4 | **Verdict** | State the generalized finding | Can the reader quote the chapter's conclusion in 1--2 sentences? |
| 5 | **Calibration** | Acknowledge exceptions, limits, or rival explanations | Has the chapter noted where the pattern breaks or where the evidence is weakest? |
| 6 | **Bridge** | Connect to individual case chapters (forward or backward) | Does the reader understand what this chapter establishes that the case chapters build on (or synthesize from)? |

No cross-issue thematic chapter in the corpus opens with a literature review. The most common openings are theoretical claims, research questions, or scope/stakes framings. Scenes and vignettes appear only in Han et al., where they serve as epigraphs rather than immersive openings.

## The Four Templates

### Template A: Case-by-Case Subsections

**Defining logic:** Examine the theme in each case sequentially, with one subsection per case, then synthesize at the end. The reader sees the full evidence for Case 1, then Case 2, and so on. The synthesis section at the end is where cross-case comparison happens most explicitly.

**Exemplar:** Mucciaroni (2008), Chapters 2--3

**Signature features:**
- Opening theoretical claim about the dimension being analyzed (framing, routing, strategy)
- Brief methodological note explaining the data and coding scheme
- Sequential subsections, one per case or issue, each with its own data (often tables or frequency counts)
- Each case subsection follows the same internal template: introduce the case's framing pattern, present evidence, note what is distinctive
- Concluding section that explicitly compares across all cases and states the generalized finding
- Bridge to case chapters by naming the anomalies or residuals that individual cases must explain

**Structural sequence:**
1. Theoretical claim + research question (500--800 words)
2. Data/methods description (300--500 words)
3. Case 1 subsection with evidence (800--1,500 words)
4. Case 2 subsection with evidence (800--1,500 words)
5. Case 3--N subsections (800--1,500 words each)
6. Cross-case synthesis (800--1,500 words)
7. Bridge to case chapters (200--400 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 10,000--13,000 |
| Subsections | 8--12 (one per case + intro + methods + conclusion) |
| Generalization-to-detail ratio | 35--40% generalization / 60--65% case-specific detail |
| Quote density | 15--30 (from primary sources, legislative records, or interviews) |
| Tables/figures | 1--2 per case subsection (frequency counts, comparison tables) |
| Opening type | Theoretical claim or research question |
| Verdict placement | In labeled conclusion section |
| Bridge | Explicit, naming the anomalies that case chapters will address |

**When to use:** Each case needs roughly equal evidentiary treatment. You have systematic data that is coded the same way across cases (content analysis, interview coding, survey data). The pattern's interest comes from comparing the same measure across different cases. The reader needs to see each case's evidence before accepting the generalization.

---

### Template B: Bespoke-Data Subsections

**Defining logic:** Like Template A, the chapter moves case-by-case, but each case uses different methods, data sources, or measurement approaches. The cases are building blocks for the same theoretical claim, but the evidence differs in kind.

**Exemplar:** Han et al. (2021), Chapter 3 ("Defining and Measuring Power")

**Signature features:**
- Opening with an epigraph or vignette that embodies the chapter's central concept
- Theoretical definition section establishing the concept to be demonstrated
- Methodological note explaining that different cases require different measurement approaches
- Sequential case subsections, each presenting bespoke data (network analysis for Virginia, legislative coding for Ohio, Twitter analysis for Arizona, interview data for Minnesota)
- Each case subsection argues that the concept is visible through its particular data, not just that the data exists
- Closing synthesis that argues the concept is robust because it survives different measurement approaches
- Bridge to the next thematic chapter (what causes the pattern, not just that it exists)

**Structural sequence:**
1. Epigraph or vignette (100--300 words)
2. Theoretical definition of the concept (500--800 words)
3. Methods overview (300--500 words)
4. Case 1 with bespoke data and analysis (1,500--2,500 words)
5. Case 2 with bespoke data and analysis (1,500--2,500 words)
6. Cases 3--N (1,500--2,500 words each)
7. Cross-case synthesis (500--1,000 words)
8. Bridge to next chapter (200--400 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 10,000--12,000 |
| Subsections | 6--9 |
| Generalization-to-detail ratio | 25--35% generalization / 65--75% case-specific detail |
| Quote density | 20--40 (interview quotes + organizational documents) |
| Figures/data displays | 1--3 per case (network maps, coding tables, text analysis) |
| Opening type | Epigraph + vignette, or practitioner quote |
| Verdict placement | In closing synthesis paragraphs |
| Bridge | Explicit, previewing the next thematic chapter |

**When to use:** Cases differ enough that a single measurement approach cannot capture the phenomenon in all of them. The contribution is partly methodological (showing the concept is robust across measurement approaches). Each case has its own data story that would be lost in a woven structure.

---

### Template C: Thematic Subsections with Woven Cases

**Defining logic:** Organize the chapter by mechanism or principle, not by case. Evidence from all cases appears within every subsection, woven together in the same paragraphs and even sentences. The cases serve the theme, not the other way around.

**Exemplar:** Han et al. (2021), Chapter 4 ("The Strategic Logic of Prisms")

**Signature features:**
- Opening with dual epigraphs or a practitioner quote that instantiates the chapter's central theoretical distinction
- Subsections organized by mechanism, principle, or dimension of the pattern -- NOT by case
- Within each subsection, evidence from multiple cases appears in the same paragraphs, using named cases to populate general claims
- Explicit "across all our cases" sentences that name the pattern as cross-cutting
- Cases appear as illustrations, not as self-contained units
- Closing synthesis that ties the mechanisms together and previews the next chapter
- The highest generalization-to-detail ratio of any template

**Structural sequence:**
1. Dual epigraphs or practitioner quote (100--300 words)
2. Core theoretical claim (500--800 words)
3. Mechanism/principle 1 subsection with woven case evidence (1,500--2,500 words)
4. Mechanism/principle 2 subsection with woven case evidence (1,500--2,500 words)
5. Mechanism/principle 3--N subsections (1,500--2,500 words each)
6. "What makes this strategic?" synthesis (500--1,000 words)
7. Bridge to next chapter (200--400 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 9,000--11,000 |
| Subsections | 5--7 (organized by mechanism, not by case) |
| Generalization-to-detail ratio | 50--60% generalization / 40--50% case-specific detail |
| Quote density | 15--25 (practitioner quotes embedded in analytical paragraphs) |
| Opening type | Dual epigraphs or practitioner quote that embeds the puzzle |
| Verdict placement | In final mechanism subsection or synthesis section |
| Bridge | Explicit, previewing the next thematic chapter |

**When to use:** The chapter's goal is to explain a general pattern, not to document each case separately. The cases are familiar to the reader from other chapters (or will be presented in later chapters). The contribution is theoretical -- identifying the mechanism or logic that operates across cases. This is the most argumentatively direct template but requires the most craft, because the writer must move between cases without losing specificity.

---

### Template D: Population-to-Paradigm

**Defining logic:** Begin with a macro-level quantitative overview of the entire population of cases, then organize cases into clusters or types, then drill down into one paradigmatic case that crystallizes the pattern. The chapter moves from the widest possible aperture to the deepest possible focus.

**Exemplar:** Patashnik (2023), Chapter 3 ("First Do No (Political) Harm: Backlash in Health Policymaking")

**Signature features:**
- Opening with scope/stakes framing for the sector or domain being examined
- Brief roadmap paragraph ("The remainder of this chapter proceeds as follows...")
- Quantitative overview section: aggregate data on the population of cases within the domain (NYT article counts, landmark-law inventories, regression results)
- Typological clustering section: organize the sub-cases into named types using a classification table
- Brief treatment of several sub-cases within each cluster (1--3 paragraphs each)
- Extended paradigmatic case study of the most revealing sub-case (2,000--4,000 words)
- Discussion/synthesis section applying the theoretical framework to all sub-cases
- Bridge to subsequent sector chapters

**Structural sequence:**
1. Scope/stakes framing (300--500 words)
2. Roadmap paragraph (100--200 words)
3. Quantitative overview with figures and tables (1,000--2,000 words)
4. Typological clustering with classification table (500--1,000 words)
5. Cluster 1: brief sub-case treatments (1,000--2,000 words)
6. Cluster 2: brief sub-case treatments (1,000--2,000 words)
7. Clusters 3--N as needed (500--1,500 words each)
8. Extended paradigmatic case study (2,000--4,000 words)
9. Discussion: three-factor (or N-factor) synthesis (800--1,500 words)
10. Bridge to subsequent chapters (200--400 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 12,000--14,000 |
| Subsections | 10--14 |
| Generalization-to-detail ratio | 40--45% generalization / 55--60% case-specific detail |
| Quote density | 10--20 (secondary sources, policy documents, news accounts) |
| Tables/figures | 2--4 (aggregate data, classification table, trend lines) |
| Opening type | Scope/stakes framing |
| Verdict placement | In labeled Discussion section |
| Bridge | Explicit, establishing the comparative baseline for subsequent sector chapters |

**When to use:** The sector or domain is the unit, and sub-cases within it are the evidence. You have quantitative data that can establish the macro pattern. Some sub-cases need only brief treatment while one paradigmatic case deserves extended analysis. The chapter needs to feel comprehensive -- covering the entire domain, not just cherry-picked examples.

## How the Chapter Relates to Case Chapters

Cross-issue thematic chapters serve one of three positional functions relative to the book's case chapters:

### Position 1: Before the case chapters (most common)

The cross-issue chapter establishes the empirical baseline or analytical lens that the case chapters will then apply. Mucciaroni Chs. 2--3 establish how opponents and advocates frame all six gay rights issues; Chs. 4--7 then explain why outcomes vary despite the framing patterns. Han Ch. 3 demonstrates that power shifts are measurable across four cases; Chs. 4--5 explain the organizational logic that produces them.

**What the cross-issue chapter provides:** The shared pattern. The case chapters explain the variation.

**Bridge formula:** "The pattern established here -- [state the cross-cutting finding] -- holds across [cases]. But it cannot explain why [Case X] succeeded while [Case Y] failed. The following chapters examine each case to identify the [institutional / organizational / contextual] factors that account for this variation."

### Position 2: After the case chapters (synthesis position)

The cross-issue chapter synthesizes findings from individual cases into a general pattern. Mucciaroni Ch. 7 examines marriage, civil rights, and hate crimes together after three individual case chapters have established the analytical categories. The chapter's task is to show that what was observed in individual cases is truly general.

**What the cross-issue chapter provides:** The generalization. The case chapters provided the evidence.

**Bridge formula:** "The preceding chapters demonstrated [what they showed in individual cases]. This chapter asks whether the same pattern holds in [additional cases or under different conditions]."

### Position 3: Interspersed with case chapters

In some architectures (Han et al., Pettinicchio), all empirical chapters are cross-issue. Each examines a different dimension of the argument across the same set of cases. No single case gets its own chapter. The chapters form a thematic sequence: what is the phenomenon? (Ch. 3) What causes it? (Ch. 4) How does it work in practice? (Ch. 5).

**What each cross-issue chapter provides:** One dimension of the explanation.

**Bridge formula:** "Having established [this dimension of the argument], the next chapter examines [the next dimension] -- specifically, [what question the next chapter addresses]."

## Template Decision Tree

### Question 1: How is your evidence organized?

- **Same measurement/coding scheme across all cases** (e.g., content analysis codes, interview coding, survey measures) --> likely **Template A** or **Template C**
- **Different data sources or methods for different cases** (e.g., network analysis for one, legislative coding for another) --> likely **Template B**
- **Quantitative macro-data plus qualitative sub-cases** (e.g., aggregate counts + extended narratives) --> likely **Template D**

### Question 2: Does the reader need to see each case's evidence separately?

- **Yes -- each case has its own empirical story that must be told** --> **Template A** or **Template B**
- **No -- the cases are illustrations of a general pattern** --> **Template C**
- **Both -- macro pattern needs establishing AND one case needs extended treatment** --> **Template D**

### Question 3: Is the chapter's primary purpose to document or to explain?

- **Document** (show that a pattern exists across cases) --> **Template A** or **Template B**
- **Explain** (identify the mechanism or logic that produces the pattern) --> **Template C**
- **Both** (show how common and then explain why) --> **Template D**

### Question 4: How familiar are the cases to the reader at this point?

- **Reader has not yet encountered the individual cases** --> **Template A** or **Template B** (give each case enough space to be legible on its own)
- **Reader already knows the cases from prior chapters** --> **Template C** (weave freely, since cases are already familiar)
- **Reader needs both overview and depth** --> **Template D**

### Confirming selection:

If answers point to different templates, prioritize **evidence organization** (Question 1). Template C requires that cases be woven fluently, which is only possible if the evidence is coded consistently or the cases are already familiar. Template D requires quantitative macro-data. Template B requires genuinely different methods across cases. When in doubt, Template A is the safest default.

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Template Selection and Structural Planning

**Goal:** Determine the chapter's template, position, and subsection architecture before any drafting begins.

**Steps:**
1. Read the book's theoretical framework and the evidence available from all cases
2. Determine the chapter's position relative to the case chapters (before, after, or interspersed)
3. Walk through the decision tree to select a template
4. Identify any moves to borrow from adjacent templates (e.g., a Template A chapter that borrows Template C's woven case paragraphs in the synthesis section)
5. Map the subsection structure with approximate word counts per section
6. Determine:
   - The theme, mechanism, or pattern being analyzed
   - Which cases provide evidence and what kind
   - The generalization-to-detail ratio target
   - Opening type (theoretical claim, epigraph, scope/stakes)
   - Verdict style (generalized finding, mechanism enumeration, factor synthesis)
   - Bridge type (forward to case chapters, backward from case chapters, forward to next thematic chapter)

**Pause:** Confirm template selection and structural plan before drafting.

---

### Phase 2: Drafting

**Goal:** Draft each section following the selected template's sequence, applying word count targets.

**Steps:**
1. Read `techniques/section-recipes.md` for the recipe matching your template. Read `techniques/paragraph-functions.md` for paragraph-level moves.
2. Draft the opening. The first section must accomplish the claim announcement and establish what cross-cutting pattern the chapter will demonstrate. Use the opening type prescribed by your template.
3. Draft the evidence body:
   - For Templates A and B: draft each case subsection, ensuring parallel internal structure. Each subsection should present evidence AND argue that the evidence supports the cross-cutting claim.
   - For Template C: draft each mechanism subsection, weaving evidence from multiple cases within each paragraph. Use CASE_PIVOT and QUOTE_WEAVE paragraph functions to move between cases.
   - For Template D: draft the macro overview, then the typological clustering, then the brief sub-case treatments, then the paradigmatic case study.
4. Draft the synthesis/verdict. It must be quotable in 1--2 sentences and explicitly name the cross-cutting pattern.
5. Draft the calibration: what exceptions exist, what the evidence does not show.
6. Draft the bridge to case chapters (or to the next thematic chapter).
7. Verify all six universal moves are present:
   - [ ] Claim announced in first section
   - [ ] Evidence from multiple cases organized in a legible structure
   - [ ] Pattern explicitly named as cross-cutting
   - [ ] Verdict stated as a generalized finding
   - [ ] Calibration present
   - [ ] Bridge present

**Pause:** Review the full draft against the structural plan.

---

### Phase 3: Calibration and Revision

**Goal:** Test the draft against template benchmarks and the six universal moves. Revise as needed.

**Calibration checks:**

| Test | Pass Criteria |
|------|---------------|
| **Total word count** | Within template range |
| **Subsection count** | Within template range |
| **Generalization-to-detail ratio** | Within template range; estimate by counting generalization paragraphs vs. case-specific paragraphs |
| **Opening type** | Matches template prescription |
| **Verdict placement** | In labeled conclusion/discussion section; quotable in 1--2 sentences |
| **Case coverage** | Every relevant case appears in the evidence body; no case is present only in the conclusion |
| **Bridge** | Explicit and directional (forward to cases, backward from cases, or forward to next theme) |

**Universal move tests:**

| Test | Question | Fix if Failing |
|------|----------|---------------|
| **Claim test** | Can the reader state the chapter's pattern by the end of the first section? | Rewrite the opening to make the cross-cutting claim explicit |
| **Organization test** | Is the evidence structure legible? Can the reader find each case's evidence? | Add subsection headings; clarify the organizational logic in the opening roadmap |
| **Pattern test** | Is there at least one paragraph that explicitly names the pattern as cross-cutting? | Add a PATTERN_ESTABLISH paragraph using "Across all [cases]..." formulation |
| **Verdict test** | Can a reader quote the finding in 1--2 sentences? | Rewrite the verdict paragraph with a signal phrase and explicit generalization |
| **Calibration test** | Has the chapter acknowledged where the pattern breaks? | Add an EXCEPTION_NOTE paragraph near the verdict |
| **Bridge test** | Does the reader know what the case chapters add? | Add a bridge paragraph naming the questions the case chapters address |

**Revision priorities** (in order):
1. Pattern clarity -- if the cross-cutting finding is not explicit, the chapter has not done its job
2. Verdict -- if the verdict is not quotable, the chapter's contribution is invisible
3. Evidence balance -- if some cases dominate and others are barely present, the cross-case claim is undermined
4. Generalization-to-detail ratio -- too much generalization produces an essay without evidence; too much detail produces a catalogue without a point
5. Bridge -- the chapter must tell the reader what comes next and why
6. Word count -- trim or expand to match template benchmarks

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. The cross-issue chapter that is actually four mini-case chapters

If each case subsection reads as a self-contained mini-essay with no reference to other cases until the conclusion, the chapter has failed its cross-issue function. Even in Template A (case-by-case), each subsection should contain at least one sentence connecting its findings to what has been seen in other cases. Mucciaroni's case subsections constantly note how the framing pattern in one issue compares to another: "In debates over gay marriage, adoption, legalization of homosexual conduct, and openly serving in the military, opponents most often define gay rights issues in terms of adverse consequences."

### 2. The cross-issue chapter that generalizes without evidence

If the chapter states patterns ("across all four cases, routing operated through X") but does not provide specific evidence from each case, the generalization floats. Han et al. avoid this by naming specific organizations and leaders in every generalization paragraph: "the obvious and staunch opposition -- GOP leaders in Virginia determined to preserve felon disenfranchisement, sheriff's deputies raiding immigrant communities in Arizona, anti-tax Tea Party groups in Ohio, and anti-immigrant legislators in Minnesota."

### 3. The synthesis that restates each case instead of comparing

A synthesis section that says "In Case A, X happened. In Case B, Y happened. In Case C, Z happened" is a summary, not a synthesis. The synthesis must identify the pattern that connects the cases: "Advocates for gay rights share one similarity with their opponents -- they rely heavily on procedural definitions for some issues" (Mucciaroni).

### 4. Evidence imbalance across cases

If one case gets 3,000 words and another gets 500, the chapter signals (intentionally or not) that the cross-case claim rests primarily on one case. Templates A and B require roughly equal treatment. Template C allows variation but should draw on all cases in every subsection. Template D intentionally varies treatment (brief clusters + one paradigmatic case), but this must be announced in the roadmap.

### 5. Missing the "what we expected vs. what we found" moment

Both Mucciaroni and Han use this move explicitly. Mucciaroni's hypothesis is that opponents will prefer consequence-based framing over moral framing; the evidence confirms this but reveals that the pattern varies by issue in instructive ways. Without the expectation-violation structure, the chapter reads as mere description rather than argumentative analysis.

### 6. The bridge that is actually a conclusion

A bridge to case chapters must be genuinely forward-pointing: it must name the specific questions the case chapters will address. "This analysis has important implications" is a conclusion. "But this pattern cannot explain why sodomy repeal succeeded while military service failed; the following chapters examine the institutional variables that account for this variation" (Mucciaroni's implicit bridge) is a genuine bridge. The bridge creates demand for the next chapter.

### 7. Losing case specificity in the weave

Template C's woven paragraphs risk becoming so abstract that the reader loses track of which case is being referenced. Han et al. avoid this by always naming organizations and leaders: "Tram Nguyen at the Virginia statehouse," "Troy Jackson was realistic about AMOS's influence," "PLAN is known for sometimes blocking traffic." If a paragraph refers to "the organizations in our study" without naming any of them, it has lost its empirical grounding.

## Cross-Reference to Other Skills

This skill works in conjunction with:

- **The Parallel Case Study skill** (`book-parallel-case/SKILL.md`): Cross-issue chapters establish the patterns or baselines that individual case chapters then apply and test. The case chapter skill explains how to ground the theory in one specific case; this skill explains how to establish the theory across multiple cases.
- **The Book Introduction skill** (`book-introduction/SKILL.md`): The book introduction previews the cross-issue chapter's role in the architecture. The introduction's roadmap entry for a cross-issue chapter should state the cross-cutting finding, not just the topic.

The two skills should be used together when planning a book's empirical architecture: which dimensions of the argument are best established through cross-issue chapters, and which are best established through individual case chapters?
