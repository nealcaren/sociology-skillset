# Section Recipes for Cross-Issue Thematic Chapters

This guide provides section-by-section drafting recipes for cross-issue thematic chapters, organized by the four templates described in the SKILL.md. Each recipe includes the section's purpose, its paragraph-by-paragraph structure, and real verbatim examples showing how published authors executed each section.

Read `paragraph-functions.md` alongside this guide for the paragraph-level mechanics.

---

## 1. The Opening: How to Frame a Cross-Issue Chapter

The opening section of a cross-issue chapter must accomplish three things in its first 500--800 words: (a) name the analytical dimension being examined, (b) signal that the analysis spans multiple cases, and (c) state or strongly imply the chapter's central claim. Unlike parallel case chapters, which open with a case-specific scene or vignette, cross-issue chapters almost always open with a theoretical claim, a research question, or a scope/stakes framing.

### Opening Recipe: Template A (Case-by-Case)

**Target:** 500--800 words. 2--3 paragraphs.

**Paragraph 1: THEME_ANNOUNCE.** Name the analytical dimension and explain why it matters.

Mucciaroni (2008), Ch. 2:

> "Advocates who debate gay rights issues fashion their appeals to arouse or dampen the perception that gay rights pose a threat. They must convince people to see an issue as 'about' one thing rather than another because social conditions are inherently ambiguous. Ambiguity gives rise to conflict over which facts are the most relevant and what they mean. Because politics is 'a struggle over alternative realities,' how advocates define or frame issues is a crucial part of their strategy to win public policy battles."

This paragraph does not mention any specific case. It operates entirely at the level of the analytical dimension (issue framing). The reader knows within three sentences that the chapter is about framing strategy across cases, not about any single issue.

**Paragraph 2: Hypothesis or expectation statement.** Tell the reader what the cross-case analysis is testing or expecting to find.

Mucciaroni (2008), Ch. 2:

> "Hence, the next section explores the following hypothesis: Opponents will prefer to define gay rights issues most often in terms of negative consequences for society and procedural aspects of issues rather than 'sanctioning the immorality of homosexuality.' Only when they have no opportunity to link their position to the welfare of positively constructed groups or institutions will opponents turn to morality-based definitions."

This paragraph sets up the "what we expected vs. what we found" structure that gives the chapter its argumentative spine. Without an explicit expectation, the evidence presentation becomes mere description.

**Paragraph 3 (optional): Methodological signal.** Briefly describe the data and how it was collected.

Mucciaroni (2008) inserts a brief methods note explaining that he coded congressional debate transcripts and newspaper editorials across all six issues. This paragraph is short (100--200 words) and points forward to the tables that will appear in each case subsection.

### Opening Recipe: Template B (Bespoke-Data)

**Target:** 500--1,000 words. 3--4 paragraphs, often beginning with an epigraph.

**Epigraph:** A practitioner or authority quote that instantiates the chapter's conceptual problem.

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 3:

> "Here's the interesting thing that happens when you're president. . . . The nature of human dynamics does not change from level to level. . . . The way power works at every level, at the United Nations or in your neighborhood, is, 'Do you have a community that stands behind what you stand for?' And if you do, you'll have more power. And if you don't, you won't."
>
> FORMER PRESIDENT BARACK OBAMA, NOVEMBER 2018

**Paragraph 1: Vignette or practitioner scene.** A brief narrative that embodies the chapter's theme. Han et al. follow the Obama epigraph with a vignette of a specific organizer.

**Paragraph 2: THEME_ANNOUNCE.** Name the theoretical concept the chapter will develop and demonstrate.

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 3:

> "Our argument about why prisms are helpful for understanding power building begins with a definition of power that focuses on its dynamic and somewhat elusive nature. In defining power, we focus on two key attributes: (a) power is expressed as the interactional relationship between (at least) two political actors, and (b) power has, as theorist Steven Lukes argues, multiple faces, some of which are largely obscured from immediate view."

**Paragraph 3: Cross-case scope signal.** State that the concept will be demonstrated across multiple cases, and briefly preview the data.

### Opening Recipe: Template C (Thematic Woven)

**Target:** 500--800 words. 2--3 paragraphs, often beginning with dual epigraphs.

**Dual epigraphs:** Two quotes from different sources that together frame the chapter's theoretical problem.

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4:

> "In the context of Minnesota right now . . . [other organizations say they] have 20,000 people . . . [or] 70,000 members. We don't have a base like that. But they can't deliver that base. Not even close."
>
> ISAIAH LEADER, JANUARY 2018
>
> "Members of Congress alter their customary patterns of [access] only when events fulfill two conditions. First, new informants must prove their competitive advantage over old informants. . . . Second, the circumstances that made the competitive advantage of new informants possible must recur."
>
> JOHN MARK HANSEN, GAINING ACCESS (1991, 108)

The dual-epigraph structure juxtaposes a practitioner voice and a scholarly authority on the same point. The reader encounters the chapter's argument twice before the author's analytical prose begins.

**Paragraph 1: Theoretical bridge from the epigraphs to the chapter's research question.**

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4:

> "As Hansen's work would predict, the leaders in our cases recognized that their power at the proverbial negotiating table with political elites depended on their ability to deliver an authentic constituency base. . . . How did this recognition of the dynamic nature of political power and the need to link constituency actions to elite negotiations shape the strategic choices leaders made?"

**Paragraph 2: Scope signal + preview of the chapter's structure.**

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4:

> "This chapter examines the patterns we see in leadership choices in order to uncover the underlying strategic logic of prisms. Looking across cases that are operating in different political environments, on varied issues, and with distinct constituencies, what patterns emerge about how leaders identified goals, analyzed their options, and made strategic choices?"

### Opening Recipe: Template D (Population-to-Paradigm)

**Target:** 500--700 words. 2--3 paragraphs.

**Paragraph 1: Scope/stakes framing.** Establish the sector's significance and the puzzle it presents.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3:

> "This chapter explores the politics of backlash in federal health policymaking. As is well known, the United States spends substantially more on health care than other wealthy countries yet leaves millions of people without coverage and ranks at or near the bottom on many population health outcomes, such as infant mortality. Any effort to understand the evolution of U.S. health care policymaking -- and the reasons why alternative roads were not taken -- must recognize how backlash politics, and its anticipation, have constrained policymakers' behavior."

**Paragraph 2: Preview of the chapter's sub-case scope.** Name the types of backlash (or whatever the pattern is) that the chapter will examine.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3:

> "There have been backlashes against proposals to restructure the health care system, such as Clinton's health reform proposal and the Affordable Care Act (ACA). There have been backlashes against efforts to dismantle safety-net protections, such as the Republican Party's attempt to retrench Medicare and Medicaid in [1995]. And there have even been backlashes against proposals to expand benefits for an organized constituency, such as the attempt to offer senior citizens new benefits through the ill-fated Medicare Catastrophic Coverage Act (MCCA)."

**Paragraph 3: Roadmap.** State what the chapter will do, in order.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3:

> "The remainder of this chapter proceeds as follows. I first canvas the New York Times's articles mentioning health policy backlash between [1960] and [2019]. Next, I use Mayhew's list of landmark enactments to identify which major health policy changes have aroused negative reactions. Finally, I examine how backlash dynamics shaped the struggle over the passage and entrenchment of the ACA."

Template D is the only template that routinely includes an explicit roadmap paragraph in the opening. This is because the chapter's structure is complex (macro to micro) and the reader needs orientation.

## 2. The Evidence Body: Organized by Template

### Evidence Body Recipe: Templates A and B (Case-by-Case)

**Target:** 6,000--10,000 words total across all case subsections.

**Each case subsection follows the same internal template:**

1. **Subsection heading** that names the case or issue (e.g., "Legalizing Homosexual Conduct," "Hate Crimes," "Arizona," "Virginia")

2. **Opening paragraph: case introduction + DATA_ANCHOR.** Introduce the case's evidence and the key finding for this case. In Template A, this often involves introducing a table:

   Mucciaroni (2008) introduces each issue subsection with a sentence identifying the issue and then immediately presents the frequency table showing how opponents (or advocates) framed the issue.

   In Template B, the opening describes the bespoke method used for this case:

   Han et al. (2021), Ch. 3 uses network analysis for Virginia, showing how NVM's position in the legislative network changed over the course of the campaign; uses legislative agenda coding for Ohio, tracking how AMOS's priorities appeared on the city council agenda.

3. **Evidence paragraphs (2--5 paragraphs).** Present the case's evidence in detail. Include specific data points, quotations from primary sources or interviews, and analytical interpretation. Each evidence paragraph should connect the case-level finding to the chapter's cross-cutting claim.

4. **Closing CASE_PIVOT paragraph.** Before moving to the next case, include at least one sentence that connects this case's finding to the emerging pattern. This prevents each subsection from reading as a self-contained essay.

   Mucciaroni (2008) includes comparative observations within each issue subsection: how the framing pattern in one issue compares to others already discussed.

**Template B variation:** Each case subsection in Template B includes a brief methods paragraph explaining why this particular data source was chosen for this case. Han et al. explain that network analysis is used for Virginia because the legislative access data is available, while Twitter analysis is used for Arizona because the campaign was primarily conducted through social media.

### Evidence Body Recipe: Template C (Thematic Woven)

**Target:** 5,000--8,000 words total across mechanism subsections.

**Each mechanism subsection follows this internal template:**

1. **Subsection heading** that names the mechanism or principle (e.g., "Constituency as an Independent Base of Power," "Civic Feedbacks," "Pragmatism," "Learning Loops")

2. **Opening THEME_ANNOUNCE paragraph.** State the mechanism this subsection examines.

   Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4, "Constituency as an Independent Base of Power":

   > "Our formulation of the prism metaphor examines the strategic linkages between the choices an organization makes about how to build its constituency and its ability to refract political power in the public arena. What are the conditions under which designing people's prisms is strategic for building power?"

3. **Evidence paragraphs with woven cases (3--6 paragraphs).** Each paragraph draws on 2--3 cases. The key technique is the CASE_PIVOT within paragraphs:

   Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4, on pragmatism across cases:

   > "He was not alone in his orientation. During our data collection, we spent time shadowing Tram Nguyen at the Virginia statehouse and observed the way she maneuvered among elected officials. . . . However, the willingness of the leaders in our case studies to play the inside game did not preclude outside-the-box strategies. In Nevada, for example, PLAN is known for sometimes blocking traffic in their protests. . . . At the same time, PLAN has its own statehouse lobbyist on staff."

   Notice that this single paragraph moves from Virginia to Nevada, connected by the analytical thread of inside/outside strategy. The CASE_PIVOT is "However, the willingness of the leaders in our case studies..." which bridges from one case to the general pattern to a different case.

4. **QUOTE_WEAVE paragraph.** Within each mechanism subsection, include at least one paragraph that weaves quotes from different cases:

   Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4:

   > "Just as Troy wrote weekly reflections that forced a disciplined consideration of his strategy, other leaders had their own mechanisms for learning. All of the organizations in the study had collective meetings after major moments in a campaign in order to reflect on what had happened, what they had learned, and what that meant for their work going forward."

5. **Closing PATTERN_ESTABLISH sentence.** End the subsection with a sentence that names the mechanism as cross-cutting.

**The craft challenge of Template C** is maintaining specificity while weaving. Every generalization must be anchored to named organizations, leaders, or events. The paragraph from Han et al. above works because "Troy wrote weekly reflections" names a specific leader with a specific practice. If it said "leaders reflected on their strategy," the paragraph would lose its empirical grounding.

### Evidence Body Recipe: Template D (Population-to-Paradigm)

**Target:** 8,000--11,000 words total.

The evidence body has four layers, each at a different level of generality:

**Layer 1: Quantitative overview (1,000--2,000 words).** Present aggregate data on the population of cases.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3 presents NYT article counts over time, identifies three high-points (ACA years and managed care era), and runs a regression on polarization, Democratic presidencies, and health salience. This layer establishes that the pattern is common and systematic, not anecdotal.

**Layer 2: Typological clustering (500--1,000 words).** Organize the sub-cases into named types. Often includes a classification table.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3 organizes backlash episodes into four clusters: coverage expansion, cost control, managed care, and public health. Each cluster is a category of sub-cases that share a type of backlash dynamic.

**Layer 3: Brief sub-case treatments (2,000--4,000 words across all clusters).** Within each cluster, give 1--3 paragraphs to each sub-case. The sub-cases illustrate the cluster's pattern.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3 gives the MCCA its own extended treatment within the coverage-expansion cluster, showing how the catastrophic coverage act provoked senior backlash. Medicare Part D gets a shorter treatment showing how policymakers learned from MCCA's mistakes. The managed care backlash gets its own cluster treatment.

**The key technique in Layer 3** is the policy-learning CASE_PIVOT -- showing how one sub-case's lessons shaped the next:

> "Policymakers drew important lessons from the MCCA backlash that influenced the design of Medicare Part D, the prescription drug bill enacted as part of the Medicare Modernization Act (MMA) of [2003]. One lesson is that seniors resist paying for their own drug benefits. Policymakers thus subsidized the prescription drug program through general revenues."

**Layer 4: Extended paradigmatic case study (2,000--4,000 words).** One sub-case receives deep treatment as the paradigmatic illustration of the chapter's argument.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3 gives the ACA its own extended section -- the longest in the chapter. The ACA section includes the passage of the law, the backlash against it, the consequences of the backlash, the role of polarization, the self-undermining feedbacks, and the counterbacklash against repeal. This is a mini-case-study within the cross-issue chapter.

The paradigmatic case works because it has already been situated within the typological framework (it belongs to the coverage-expansion cluster) and the macro data (it is the highest-backlash episode in the NYT data). The reader encounters it not as an isolated case but as the most intense expression of a documented pattern.

## 3. The Synthesis/Verdict

**Target:** 800--1,500 words. 3--6 paragraphs.

The synthesis section is where the cross-issue chapter delivers its payoff. It must do three things: (a) state the generalized finding, (b) name the mechanism or explanation, and (c) acknowledge exceptions or limits.

### Synthesis Recipe: Templates A and B

**Paragraph 1: PATTERN_ESTABLISH.** Open the synthesis by stating the cross-case finding explicitly.

Mucciaroni (2008), Ch. 2 synthesis:

> "Advocates for gay rights share one similarity with their opponents -- they rely heavily on procedural definitions for some issues. They argue that the federal government has an important role in prosecuting hate crimes, but not in influencing states' marriage laws, and that Congress should leave defense policy matters to the executive branch."

**Paragraph 2: EXCEPTION_NOTE.** Identify where the pattern varies and explain why.

Mucciaroni (2008), Ch. 3 synthesis:

> "Defining issues in terms of principles seems to work for some issues more than others, however. To the extent that strategies of political discourse matter for policy decisions, gay rights advocates must align their definitions with particular issues and institutional contexts. Reliance on liberty and equality principles makes most sense for issues related to the treatment of gays and lesbians in the marketplace and other impersonal social spaces."

**Paragraph 3: VERDICT_GENERALIZE.** State the chapter's central finding in generalizable form.

Mucciaroni (2008), Ch. 3 verdict:

> "Advocates have been much more effective in gaining adoption rights than marriage, in part perhaps because they have eschewed the language of 'equal rights' and 'freedom' and talked about how adoption affects one of society's most positively constructed groups -- children -- and how gay couples can make good parents."

### Synthesis Recipe: Template C

**Paragraph 1: Restate the research question.**

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4 synthesis:

> "In this chapter, we sought to show why it was strategic for the leaders in our case studies to follow the logic of preparing for uncertainty."

**Paragraph 2: VERDICT_GENERALIZE.** State the answer.

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4:

> "We argued that understanding the distinction between a constituency as an independent source of power and a constituency as a tool for access was key to answering this question. For organizations representing constituencies like the ones in our case studies, the bid for political power does not stop with access to power. Our case organizations knew they would have to constantly work to hold decision makers accountable, and that would necessitate an ability to wield resources that the organizations controlled. The key resource these leaders could control was their own constituency."

**Paragraph 3: EXCEPTION_NOTE or calibration.**

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 3 calibration:

> "While we acknowledge the fragility of these power shifts, this chapter also suggests that understanding movement influence only through the lens of visible wins or losses understates the level and type of change for which a movement may be responsible."

### Synthesis Recipe: Template D

**The Discussion section has a distinctive three-factor (or N-factor) structure.**

**Paragraph 1: Name the factors.**

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3 Discussion:

> "Three key factors -- policy attributes, the participatory and organizational capacities of stakeholder groups, and partisan polarization -- have combined to make health policymaking highly susceptible to the politics of backlash."

**Paragraphs 2--4: One paragraph per factor.** Each paragraph explains how the factor operated across sub-cases.

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3:

> "First, American health care is enormously expensive. The need to keep health spending from destabilizing the federal budget can lead policymakers to impose concentrated costs to offset rising expenditures. . . . Second, the health care sector is awash with vested interests, including physicians and hospitals that receive substantial public money under programs like Medicare. . . . Finally, health policymaking is highly polarized."

**Paragraph 5: VERDICT_GENERALIZE + forward implication.**

Patashnik (2023), Ch. 3:

> "While health policy changes carry significant risks of backlash, policymakers find it hard to resist the call of reform. The glaring weaknesses of the U.S. health care system -- including its jaw-dropping costs, inconsistent quality, and failure to guarantee universal access -- weaken the base of support for existing arrangements, which motivates policymakers to search for more efficient and equitable alternatives. Advancing policy solutions without instigating backlash has been a decades-long challenge of governance, leading to alternating cycles of reform and countermobilization."

## 4. The Bridge to Case Chapters

**Target:** 200--400 words. 1--2 paragraphs.

The bridge paragraph is the final move of the chapter. It must create demand for what follows -- either the individual case chapters or the next thematic chapter.

### Bridge Recipe: Cross-Issue Chapter Positioned Before Case Chapters

**Purpose:** Name the residuals or anomalies that the cross-issue analysis revealed but cannot explain.

**Mucciaroni (2008), Ch. 2 bridge (implicit structure):**

The final sentences of Chapter 2 name the anomalous cases -- adoption, sodomy, military -- where the framing pattern does not predict outcomes, posing specific questions that Chapters 4, 5, and 6 will answer. The bridge works because it names a gap: framing theory explains some outcomes but not others. The case chapters fill the gap by adding institutional variables.

**Formula:**

> "The pattern established in this chapter -- [state the finding] -- holds across [cases]. But it cannot account for the variation in outcomes between [Case X] and [Case Y]. Why did [specific anomaly]? The following chapters examine each [case/issue/movement] to identify the [institutional / organizational / contextual] factors that account for this variation."

### Bridge Recipe: Cross-Issue Chapter Positioned After Case Chapters

**Purpose:** Show what the synthesis reveals that individual cases did not.

**Formula:**

> "The preceding chapters demonstrated [what individual cases showed]. Examining [the theme] across all [cases] reveals a pattern that no single case could establish: [state the cross-cutting finding]. This finding [extends / complicates / confirms] the book's central argument by showing that [implication]."

### Bridge Recipe: Cross-Issue Chapter Bridging to the Next Thematic Chapter

**Purpose:** Name the question the next chapter addresses, given what this chapter established.

Han et al. (2021), Ch. 4 bridge:

> "The next chapter explores exactly what it means to do this slow work of cultivating a base that serves as a durable and independent source of political power rather than an ephemeral ingredient of political stagecraft."

**Formula:**

> "Having established [what this chapter showed -- the pattern or mechanism], the question becomes [the next question]. The next chapter examines [specific topic] to show [what the next chapter argues]."

## 5. The Generalization-to-Detail Ratio: The Key Craft Challenge

The defining craft challenge of a cross-issue chapter is calibrating the ratio between generalization (analytical claims about the pattern) and case-specific detail (concrete evidence from individual cases). Too much generalization produces an essay that asserts without demonstrating. Too much detail produces a catalogue that describes without arguing.

### Ratio Benchmarks by Template

| Template | Generalization | Case-Specific Detail | Why |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|-----|
| A: Case-by-Case | 35--40% | 60--65% | Each case needs full evidentiary treatment; synthesis section does the generalizing |
| B: Bespoke-Data | 25--35% | 65--75% | Different methods require more space to present; the evidence IS the contribution |
| C: Thematic Woven | 50--60% | 40--50% | The argument is the contribution; cases serve as illustrations |
| D: Population-to-Paradigm | 40--45% | 55--60% | Macro data is inherently generalizing; the paradigmatic case anchors the detail |

### How to Diagnose the Ratio

**Count generalization paragraphs.** A paragraph is a "generalization paragraph" if its main claim is about the pattern across cases. It is a "detail paragraph" if its main claim is about one specific case's evidence. Divide generalization paragraphs by total paragraphs to get the ratio.

**Warning signs of too much generalization:**
- Three or more consecutive paragraphs with no named case, organization, or individual
- Sentences like "the organizations in our study found that..." without naming which organizations
- The reader feels they are reading a theoretical essay, not an empirical chapter

**Warning signs of too much detail:**
- Five or more consecutive paragraphs about a single case with no cross-case comparison
- The synthesis section is only one paragraph
- The reader cannot state the cross-cutting pattern after reading the chapter

### How Published Authors Manage the Ratio

**Mucciaroni (40% generalization / 60% detail)** manages the ratio by having heavy detail in the case subsections (frequency tables, verbatim testimony) but heavy generalization in the opening and closing sections. The theoretical frame and the synthesis section together account for nearly all the generalization.

**Han et al., Ch. 4 (55% generalization / 45% detail)** manages the ratio by embedding case evidence within theoretical paragraphs. The paragraphs are analytically framed (generalization) but populated with named leaders and specific practices (detail). A single paragraph might contain both a theoretical claim and two case-specific examples.

**Han et al., Ch. 3 (30% generalization / 70% detail)** manages the ratio in the opposite direction -- the chapter is evidence-heavy because its function is to *demonstrate* that power shifts are measurable. The theoretical sections are brief; most of the word count is in case-specific data presentation.

**Patashnik (40% generalization / 60% detail)** manages the ratio through the layered structure: the macro overview and clustering sections are generalizing; the sub-case treatments and the ACA section are detail-heavy. The Discussion section at the end returns to generalization.

### The Golden Rule

Every generalization paragraph should contain at least one named case reference. Every case-detail paragraph should contain at least one sentence connecting the case evidence to the cross-cutting claim. This prevents the chapter from splitting into disconnected halves -- theoretical essay above, evidence catalogue below. The most effective cross-issue chapters weave generalization and detail within the same paragraphs.

Han et al. (2021) exemplify this at the paragraph level:

> "Across all of our cases, leaders like Tram recognized the importance of having what scholars might call a simultaneous 'inside and outside' game if they wanted to build durable political power."

This single sentence contains a cross-case generalization ("Across all of our cases"), a named individual ("Tram"), and a theoretical concept ("inside and outside game"). It is simultaneously general and specific. That is the target.
