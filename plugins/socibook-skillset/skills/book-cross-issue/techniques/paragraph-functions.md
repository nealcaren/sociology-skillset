# Paragraph Functions in Cross-Issue Thematic Chapters

This guide identifies eight paragraph-level functions that recur across cross-issue thematic chapters in political sociology and American politics monographs. Each function has a defined purpose, a characteristic internal structure, and real verbatim examples drawn from Mucciaroni (2008), Han et al. (2021), and Patashnik (2023).

Use this guide in two ways: (1) when drafting, to decide what each paragraph needs to accomplish; (2) when revising, to diagnose paragraphs that feel flat, repetitive, or disconnected from the cross-case argument.

---

## The Eight Paragraph Functions

### 1. THEME_ANNOUNCE

**Purpose:** State the cross-cutting pattern or dimension the chapter will analyze. This paragraph tells the reader: "The unit of analysis in this chapter is X, examined across all cases."

**Typical position:** Paragraph 1 or 2. In Template C (Thematic Woven) chapters, it follows the opening epigraph. In Templates A and B, it is often the first paragraph.

**How it works in practice:**

Mucciaroni (2008) opens Chapter 2 with a THEME_ANNOUNCE that names the dimension (issue framing), explains why it matters, and signals the cross-case scope:

> "Advocates who debate gay rights issues fashion their appeals to arouse or dampen the perception that gay rights pose a threat. They must convince people to see an issue as 'about' one thing rather than another because social conditions are inherently ambiguous. Ambiguity gives rise to conflict over which facts are the most relevant and what they mean. Because politics is 'a struggle over alternative realities,' how advocates define or frame issues is a crucial part of their strategy to win public policy battles (Rochefort and Cobb 1994, 9; see also Nelson and Kinder 1996). If gay rights supporters convince policymakers and citizens that an issue is about 'ending invidious discrimination,' for example, they are likely to succeed; if their opponents convince them that the issue boils down to 'condoning an immoral lifestyle,' gay rights advocates are almost certain to lose."

Han et al. (2021) open Chapter 4 with a practitioner quote followed by a THEME_ANNOUNCE that names the chapter's theoretical target:

> "In 1991, University of Chicago professor John Mark Hansen published a book examining why certain interest groups had greater access to elected officials than others. His case study was the farm lobby in the twentieth century. Why, he asked, were farm lobby groups persistently able to gain influence in the US Congress disproportionate to their size? 'Lobbies gain access when, in the judgment of congressional elites, they represent constituents,' he concludes (Hansen 1991, 3). Constituency-based interest groups differentiate themselves from others when they are able to establish what Hansen calls a 'competitive advantage' with 'recurrence.'"

Patashnik (2023) opens Chapter 3 with a THEME_ANNOUNCE that establishes the sector scope and names the pattern:

> "This chapter explores the politics of backlash in federal health policymaking. As is well known, the United States spends substantially more on health care than other wealthy countries yet leaves millions of people without coverage and ranks at or near the bottom on many population health outcomes, such as infant mortality. Any effort to understand the evolution of U.S. health care policymaking -- and the reasons why alternative roads were not taken -- must recognize how backlash politics, and its anticipation, have constrained policymakers' behavior."

**Internal structure:**

1. **Name the dimension** -- what analytical category is being examined across cases (framing, strategy, power, backlash).
2. **Explain why it matters** -- connect the dimension to outcomes the reader cares about.
3. **Signal the cross-case scope** -- indicate that the analysis spans multiple cases, issues, or domains.
4. **Set expectations** -- hint at the finding or pose the question the chapter will answer.

**Critical rule:** The THEME_ANNOUNCE must name a *dimension of analysis*, not a case. "This chapter examines opponent framing across all six issues" is a THEME_ANNOUNCE. "This chapter examines sodomy law reform" is a case opening and belongs in a parallel case chapter.

**Diagnostic:** After reading this paragraph, does the reader know what analytical dimension the chapter examines -- and that it examines this dimension across multiple cases?

### 2. CASE_PIVOT

**Purpose:** Move the reader from one case to another within a paragraph or between paragraphs, maintaining analytical continuity. The pivot signals: "The same pattern is visible in a different case."

**Typical position:** Throughout the evidence body, especially in Template C (Thematic Woven) chapters. In Templates A and B, CASE_PIVOTs appear within case subsections (comparing to other cases) and in the synthesis section.

**How it works in practice:**

Han et al. (2021) pivot between cases within a single paragraph by naming organizations and leaders:

> "He was not alone in his orientation. During our data collection, we spent time shadowing Tram Nguyen at the Virginia statehouse and observed the way she maneuvered among elected officials, advocating her constituents' views while simultaneously inserting herself in the middle of a number of different debates, as revealed in the network maps in chapter 3. . . . However, the willingness of the leaders in our case studies to play the inside game did not preclude outside-the-box strategies. In Nevada, for example, PLAN is known for sometimes blocking traffic in their protests, for their executive director going to trial . . . for disrupting a meeting on fossil fuel lease sales. . . . At the same time, PLAN has its own statehouse lobbyist on staff."

The pivot here moves from Virginia (Tram Nguyen at the statehouse) to Nevada (PLAN blocking traffic) in a single paragraph, connected by the analytical point about inside/outside strategy.

Patashnik (2023) pivots between sub-cases using a policy-learning chain:

> "Policymakers drew important lessons from the MCCA backlash that influenced the design of Medicare Part D, the prescription drug bill enacted as part of the Medicare Modernization Act (MMA) of [2003]. One lesson is that seniors resist paying for their own drug benefits. Policymakers thus subsidized the prescription drug program through general revenues. A second lesson is that people resent being forced to pay for coverage they believe they do not need. Policymakers therefore made participation in Medicare Part D voluntary."

Here the pivot from MCCA to Medicare Part D is causal -- one case literally caused design choices in the next.

Mucciaroni (2008) pivots between issues using a comparative enumeration:

> "In debates over gay marriage, adoption, legalization of homosexual conduct, and openly serving in the military, opponents most often define gay rights issues in terms of adverse consequences. . . . In debates over hate crimes, they define the issue most often in terms of liberal principles."

**Internal structure:**

1. **Complete the current case's point** -- finish the analytical observation about Case A.
2. **Pivot marker** -- a transition phrase that signals movement to another case ("He was not alone," "Policymakers drew important lessons from X that influenced Y," "In debates over [other issues]").
3. **Name the new case** -- the new case must be identified by name (organization, leader, policy, issue), not referenced generically.
4. **Connect analytically** -- the new case must illustrate the same point, extend it, or provide a contrast.

**Critical rule:** Every CASE_PIVOT must serve an analytical purpose. The pivot is never mere variety ("meanwhile, in Ohio..."). It must show that the same mechanism operates in a different context, or that the pattern varies in an instructive way. Han et al.'s pivot from Virginia to Nevada works because both illustrate the inside/outside strategy but through different expressions of it.

**Diagnostic:** Does the pivot advance the argument, or is it just moving to a different case for the sake of coverage?

### 3. PATTERN_ESTABLISH

**Purpose:** State explicitly that a finding holds across multiple cases. This is the paragraph that lifts the analysis from case-level observation to cross-case generalization.

**Typical position:** After at least two cases have been presented (or after multiple cases have been cited within a thematic subsection). Often appears at the end of an evidence subsection or at the opening of the synthesis section.

**How it works in practice:**

Han et al. (2021) use an "across all" formulation that names specific cases while stating the general pattern:

> "Across all of these venues, the leaders in our cases recognized that (a) exercising power required negotiation, and (b) their ability to build, maintain, and flexibly deploy a loyal constituency base constituted their power."

Han et al. also use named cases to populate a general claim in a single sentence:

> "Across our cases, we found that the obvious and staunch opposition -- GOP leaders in Virginia determined to preserve felon disenfranchisement, sheriff's deputies raiding immigrant communities in Arizona, anti-tax Tea Party groups in Ohio, and anti-immigrant legislators in Minnesota -- were rarely the actors with whom movement leaders directly negotiated power."

Patashnik (2023) establishes the pattern through a population-level count:

> "In sum, nearly one-third of the landmark health laws enacted between [1960] and [2019] sparked (or were the casualty of) backlash politics in one fashion or another. The mobilization of opposition forces in response to shifts in the health policy status quo is clearly not an atypical pattern. Rather, it happens regularly."

Mucciaroni (2008) establishes the pattern by naming the general finding and specifying the exceptions:

> "Advocates for gay rights share one similarity with their opponents -- they rely heavily on procedural definitions for some issues. They argue that the federal government has an important role in prosecuting hate crimes, but not in influencing states' marriage laws, and that Congress should leave defense policy matters to the executive branch."

**Internal structure:**

1. **Cross-case signal** -- a phrase that explicitly names the generality: "Across all," "In sum," "Across our cases," "In every case we examined."
2. **The pattern stated** -- the finding, phrased as a general claim rather than a case-specific observation.
3. **Case evidence embedded** -- specific cases or examples named to ground the generalization.
4. **Scope qualified** (optional) -- "for some issues," "in one fashion or another," signaling that the pattern is not monolithic.

**Critical rule:** A PATTERN_ESTABLISH paragraph must use a cross-case signal phrase. "The organization recognized the importance of constituency" is a case observation. "Across all of our cases, leaders recognized the importance of constituency" is a pattern establishment. The signal phrase is what transforms case evidence into a cross-cutting claim. Without it, the reader cannot distinguish a finding about one case from a finding about the pattern.

**Diagnostic:** Does this paragraph contain a sentence that explicitly states the finding holds across multiple cases? If not, it is an evidence paragraph, not a PATTERN_ESTABLISH paragraph.

### 4. EXCEPTION_NOTE

**Purpose:** Acknowledge that the cross-cutting pattern does not hold uniformly. Name the exception and explain why it exists, thereby strengthening the general claim by showing the author is aware of its limits.

**Typical position:** Immediately after a PATTERN_ESTABLISH paragraph, or in the calibration section near the verdict.

**How it works in practice:**

Mucciaroni (2008) notes exceptions through issue-specific variation:

> "Defining issues in terms of principles seems to work for some issues more than others, however. To the extent that strategies of political discourse matter for policy decisions, gay rights advocates must align their definitions with particular issues and institutional contexts. Reliance on liberty and equality principles makes most sense for issues related to the treatment of gays and lesbians in the marketplace and other impersonal social spaces. Despite Americans' continued support for these principles in the abstract, they may not find them sufficiently compelling and relevant when advocates ask them to legitimate intimate sexual and family relationships."

Han et al. (2021) note the exception through the modesty of their own subjects:

> "While we acknowledge the fragility of these power shifts, this chapter also suggests that understanding movement influence only through the lens of visible wins or losses understates the level and type of change for which a movement may be responsible."

Patashnik (2023) builds the exception into the chapter's architecture -- the section on "counterbacklash" shows that the backlash pattern can reverse:

> "While the House of Representatives did succeed in passing such a bill, Republicans initially could not get similar legislation through the Senate. . . . [T]he GOP's attack on the ACA 'had the ironic effect of generating consistent majority support for the program for the first time since its enactment in [2010].'"

**Internal structure:**

1. **Concede the general pattern** -- restate or reference the pattern just established.
2. **Name the exception** -- identify specifically which case, issue, or condition does not fit.
3. **Explain why** -- the exception is not random; it is instructive. State the conditions that produce the exception.
4. **Preserve the general claim** -- show that the exception does not undermine the pattern but reveals its scope conditions.

**Critical rule:** An EXCEPTION_NOTE must explain, not just note. "The exception is Case X" is insufficient. "The exception is Case X, because [condition Y] changes the dynamic" is the required form. Mucciaroni does not just say principles work for some issues -- he explains that they work for "impersonal social spaces" but not for "intimate sexual and family relationships." The explanation IS the analytical contribution.

**Diagnostic:** Does the exception paragraph explain why the exception exists -- and does the explanation strengthen rather than undermine the general claim?

### 5. DATA_ANCHOR

**Purpose:** Introduce quantitative data (a table, a figure, a count, a percentage) to ground the cross-case claim in systematically collected evidence. This paragraph converts impressionistic cross-case observation into empirically documented pattern.

**Typical position:** Early in each case subsection (Templates A and B) or in the quantitative overview section (Template D). In Template C, DATA_ANCHOR paragraphs appear within mechanism subsections to support the theoretical claim with numbers.

**How it works in practice:**

Mucciaroni (2008) uses DATA_ANCHOR paragraphs to introduce frequency tables for each issue:

> "Table 2.1 shows the frequency of different types of arguments made by opponents of gay rights in congressional debate and newspaper editorials. The most striking finding is that opponents rely most heavily on consequence-based arguments across four of the six issues."

The DATA_ANCHOR establishes that the finding is not impressionistic -- it comes from a systematic content analysis with coded data.

Patashnik (2023) uses a DATA_ANCHOR to introduce the macro-level quantitative overview:

> "My research assistants and I searched for the word backlash in New York Times articles that also contained terms related to health, health care, and health policy. The search generated 1,044 articles published between 1960 and 2019."

And a second DATA_ANCHOR for the landmark-law inventory:

> "In sum, nearly one-third of the landmark health laws enacted between [1960] and [2019] sparked (or were the casualty of) backlash politics in one fashion or another."

Han et al. (2021) use a DATA_ANCHOR differently -- introducing network analysis visualizations:

> "In this chapter, we employed original data to make visible these dynamic negotiations among the diverse sets of actors across our four primary cases: Arizona, Minnesota, Ohio, and Virginia."

**Internal structure:**

1. **Data source identification** -- what was collected, by whom, from where.
2. **Measure description** -- what variable or dimension the data captures.
3. **Key finding** -- the number, percentage, or pattern the data reveals.
4. **Analytical interpretation** -- what the data means for the chapter's argument.

**Critical rule:** A DATA_ANCHOR paragraph must name the source and the measure. "Many opponents used consequence-based arguments" is an assertion. "Table 2.1 shows the frequency of different types of arguments made by opponents of gay rights in congressional debate and newspaper editorials" is a DATA_ANCHOR -- it points the reader to specific, verifiable evidence. The authority of the cross-case claim rests on the reader's trust that the evidence was systematically collected, not impressionistically gathered.

**Diagnostic:** Does this paragraph point the reader to a specific, named source of evidence -- and does it interpret the data rather than merely reporting it?

### 6. QUOTE_WEAVE

**Purpose:** Combine quotations from different cases within a single paragraph or adjacent paragraphs to demonstrate that the pattern is visible in the voices of multiple actors, not just in the analyst's interpretation.

**Typical position:** Throughout the evidence body, especially in Template C. Most effective when two or three quotes from different cases appear in the same analytical paragraph. Also appears in the synthesis section of Templates A and B.

**How it works in practice:**

Han et al. (2021) weave quotes from multiple organizations within analytical paragraphs:

> "As Joy Cushman observed in an interview, 'the organizations that are building more power are able to wield different types of power. It's not all voting, it's not all civil disobedience.' Leaders in our study recognized both the value in articulating clear, measurable strategies for power and the need to do so in ways that maintained their strategic flexibility."

Then, in the same section:

> "One longstanding ISAIAH leader told us, 'I feel like the seeds of our destruction are in our success.' The pace of political work is so fast, he was arguing, that it is hard to find time to do the slow work that base building requires: 'What I really want to do is have like fifty clergy that I'm actually developing, and like they're becoming, not just better ISAIAH leaders but better human beings, and better clergy, and better pastors.'"

And from a different case:

> "Similarly, despite moving from the margins to the center of one power network in Ohio, Troy Jackson was realistic about AMOS's influence and lack thereof: 'Our whole idea was, "Who owns Cincinnati?"' he said. 'I'm still convinced we didn't change that dynamic one iota in this campaign.'"

The three quotes -- from Joy (Arizona/general), ISAIAH leader (Minnesota), and Troy (Ohio) -- are woven within a single subsection to show that the same awareness of power's fragility characterizes leaders across all cases.

Mucciaroni (2008) weaves testimony from different legislative records:

> "Those who make these arguments may genuinely feel that they are important, but procedural definitions also supply plausible and seemingly apolitical rationales for their positions that allow both sides in debate to sidestep difficult and controversial substantive policy questions."

This sentence synthesizes testimony from debates across multiple issues into a single interpretive claim about what procedural framing accomplishes for advocates.

**Internal structure:**

1. **Analytical claim** -- state the point the quotes will illustrate.
2. **First quote** -- from Case A, with speaker identified by name and organizational affiliation.
3. **Interpretive bridge** -- what the quote shows, and how it connects to the next case.
4. **Second quote** -- from Case B, showing the same pattern in a different voice.
5. **Synthesis** -- what the combined quotes demonstrate about the cross-case pattern.

**Critical rule:** Quotes from different cases must be connected by an analytical thread, not just juxtaposed. "Leader A said X. Leader B said Y." is a list. "Leader A said X, showing the pattern of [analytical point]. Similarly, Leader B -- working in a different state with different adversaries -- described the same recognition: Y." is a QUOTE_WEAVE. The "similarly" and the analytical frame are doing the cross-case work.

**Diagnostic:** Are quotes from different cases linked by an explicit analytical point -- or are they merely placed next to each other?

### 7. VERDICT_GENERALIZE

**Purpose:** State the chapter's central finding as a cross-case generalization. This is the paragraph a reviewer would quote to summarize the chapter's contribution.

**Typical position:** In the labeled Conclusion, Discussion, or final synthesis section. Never before the evidence is presented. Often the opening paragraph of the conclusion section.

**How it works in practice:**

Mucciaroni (2008) delivers the verdict as a generalized strategic finding:

> "Advocates for gay rights share one similarity with their opponents -- they rely heavily on procedural definitions for some issues. They argue that the federal government has an important role in prosecuting hate crimes, but not in influencing states' marriage laws, and that Congress should leave defense policy matters to the executive branch. Those who make these arguments may genuinely feel that they are important, but procedural definitions also supply plausible and seemingly apolitical rationales for their positions that allow both sides in debate to sidestep difficult and controversial substantive policy questions."

Han et al. (2021) deliver the verdict as a theoretical claim supported by the cross-case evidence:

> "In this chapter, we sought to show why it was strategic for the leaders in our case studies to follow the logic of preparing for uncertainty. In other words, what makes the investment in constituency strategic for these leaders? We argued that understanding the distinction between a constituency as an independent source of power and a constituency as a tool for access was key to answering this question. For organizations representing constituencies like the ones in our case studies, the bid for political power does not stop with access to power."

Patashnik (2023) delivers the verdict as a three-factor synthesis:

> "Three key factors -- policy attributes, the participatory and organizational capacities of stakeholder groups, and partisan polarization -- have combined to make health policymaking highly susceptible to the politics of backlash."

**Internal structure:**

1. **Signal phrase** -- "In this chapter, we sought to show," "Three key factors," "In sum," "The analysis reveals."
2. **The generalized finding** -- stated as a claim about the pattern across cases, not about any single case.
3. **The mechanism or logic** -- why the pattern holds (the "because" clause).
4. **Scope indication** -- what the finding applies to and what it does not.

**Critical rule:** The VERDICT_GENERALIZE must be a *generalization*, not a case summary. "The MCCA backlash influenced Medicare Part D design" is a case finding. "Three key factors have combined to make health policymaking highly susceptible to backlash" is a cross-case verdict. The verdict must be pitched at the level of the chapter's unit of analysis (the theme, mechanism, or pattern), not at the level of any individual case.

**Diagnostic:** Is this paragraph a generalization about the pattern -- or a summary of what happened in specific cases?

### 8. BRIDGE_TO_CASES

**Purpose:** Hand off from the cross-issue analysis to the individual case chapters (or to the next thematic chapter). The bridge creates demand for what follows by naming what the cross-issue analysis established and what it cannot explain.

**Typical position:** Final paragraph or final few sentences of the chapter.

**How it works in practice:**

Mucciaroni (2008) bridges to case chapters by naming the anomalies the cross-issue analysis revealed:

The final sentences of Chapter 2 explicitly name the anomalous cases (adoption, sodomy, military) that the individual case chapters will explain -- posing the questions ("Why did gay rights opponents fail to translate the threat...?") that the case chapters (4, 5, 6) are designed to answer.

Han et al. (2021) bridge from Chapter 4 to Chapter 5 with a direct handoff sentence:

> "The next chapter explores exactly what it means to do this slow work of cultivating a base that serves as a durable and independent source of political power rather than an ephemeral ingredient of political stagecraft."

Patashnik (2023) bridges to subsequent sector chapters by establishing the comparative baseline:

The Discussion section of Chapter 3 explicitly sets up subsequent chapters: the three-factor framework identified in health policymaking will be applied to trade and immigration (Chapter 4) and grassroots cases (Chapter 5). Chapter 3 establishes the comparative baseline: health is a sector where backlash is common and intense; subsequent chapters test whether the same framework applies elsewhere.

**Internal structure:**

1. **Restate the finding** -- compressed version of the verdict.
2. **Name the limit** -- what the cross-issue analysis established but cannot explain.
3. **Preview the next step** -- what the case chapters (or next thematic chapter) will add.
4. **Create demand** -- frame the transition as a question the reader should want answered.

**Critical rule:** A BRIDGE_TO_CASES must be genuinely forward-pointing. It must name specific questions the next chapters address, not vaguely gesture at "further analysis." Han et al.'s bridge works because "what it means to do this slow work" is a specific question. Mucciaroni's bridge works because it names specific anomalous cases the reader wants explained. If the bridge could be deleted without the reader noticing something is missing, it is too vague.

**Diagnostic:** Does the bridge create demand for the next chapter -- or could the reader stop reading without feeling they are missing something?

## Paragraph Sequences by Template

The four templates deploy these paragraph functions in characteristic sequences.

### Template A: Case-by-Case Subsections

> Present each case's evidence separately, then synthesize.

```
THEME_ANNOUNCE --> DATA_ANCHOR (methods) -->
[Case 1: DATA_ANCHOR --> evidence --> CASE_PIVOT (brief comparison)] -->
[Case 2: DATA_ANCHOR --> evidence --> CASE_PIVOT] -->
[Case N: same pattern] -->
PATTERN_ESTABLISH --> EXCEPTION_NOTE --> VERDICT_GENERALIZE --> BRIDGE_TO_CASES
```

**Exemplar:** Mucciaroni Ch. 2 follows this sequence almost exactly: theoretical framing, then sequential issue subsections each with frequency tables, then a synthesis section that uses PATTERN_ESTABLISH, EXCEPTION_NOTE, and VERDICT_GENERALIZE in rapid succession.

### Template B: Bespoke-Data Subsections

> Like Template A, but each case uses different evidence types.

```
THEME_ANNOUNCE (via epigraph or vignette) --> theoretical definition -->
DATA_ANCHOR (methods overview) -->
[Case 1: DATA_ANCHOR (bespoke method) --> evidence --> CASE_PIVOT] -->
[Case 2: DATA_ANCHOR (different method) --> evidence --> CASE_PIVOT] -->
[Case N: same pattern] -->
PATTERN_ESTABLISH --> QUOTE_WEAVE (cross-case voices) -->
VERDICT_GENERALIZE --> BRIDGE_TO_CASES
```

**Exemplar:** Han Ch. 3 opens with epigraph and theoretical definition, then presents each case with its own data (network analysis for Virginia, legislative coding for Ohio, Twitter analysis for Arizona), then synthesizes with PATTERN_ESTABLISH and VERDICT_GENERALIZE.

### Template C: Thematic Subsections with Woven Cases

> Organize by mechanism; cases appear within every subsection.

```
THEME_ANNOUNCE (via dual epigraphs + theoretical claim) -->
[Mechanism 1: THEME_ANNOUNCE (sub) --> QUOTE_WEAVE --> CASE_PIVOT -->
    PATTERN_ESTABLISH] -->
[Mechanism 2: THEME_ANNOUNCE (sub) --> QUOTE_WEAVE --> CASE_PIVOT -->
    PATTERN_ESTABLISH] -->
[Mechanism N: same pattern] -->
VERDICT_GENERALIZE --> EXCEPTION_NOTE --> BRIDGE_TO_CASES
```

**Exemplar:** Han Ch. 4 opens with dual epigraphs, then uses thematic subsections (independent power, civic feedbacks, pragmatism, learning loops) where cases from Arizona, Virginia, Ohio, Minnesota, Nevada, and Kentucky appear woven together within each subsection's paragraphs.

### Template D: Population-to-Paradigm

> Move from macro to micro, ending with one paradigmatic case.

```
THEME_ANNOUNCE (scope/stakes) --> roadmap paragraph -->
DATA_ANCHOR (quantitative overview) --> DATA_ANCHOR (landmark-law inventory) -->
PATTERN_ESTABLISH (population-level) -->
[Cluster 1: brief sub-case treatments with CASE_PIVOTs] -->
[Cluster 2: same] -->
[Paradigmatic case: extended evidence with QUOTE_WEAVE] -->
EXCEPTION_NOTE (counterbacklash or reversal) -->
VERDICT_GENERALIZE (three-factor synthesis) --> BRIDGE_TO_CASES
```

**Exemplar:** Patashnik 2023 Ch. 3 opens with scope/stakes, presents NYT article data and Mayhew landmark-law data, clusters sub-cases (coverage, cost control, managed care, public health), then gives the ACA extended treatment as the paradigmatic case, before synthesizing with the three-factor verdict.
