# Section-by-Section Drafting Recipes for Book Conclusion Chapters

Detailed instructions for drafting each section of a book conclusion. Each recipe specifies what the section accomplishes, its internal structure, word count guidance, and real examples from 22 published books showing how authors execute each move.

Use this file alongside `paragraph-functions.md` (for paragraph-level guidance). This file tells you how the sections combine into a complete conclusion chapter.

---

## Section 1: The Opening Frame

**Target length:** 200--600 words | **Paragraphs:** 1--3

### What This Section Accomplishes

The opening frame re-enters the book's argument. By the end of the opening section, the reader must know: (1) the book's central claim is being restated or dramatized, (2) the conclusion will do more than summarize, and (3) there is a reason to keep reading. Every conclusion in the 22-book corpus performs this move. The opening frame sets the emotional and intellectual register for everything that follows.

### Recipe by Opening Type

---

#### Type A: Thesis Restatement / Case Inventory (7/22 -- 32%)

**When to use:** When the book's comparative architecture is its primary selling point and the reader needs to see the full set of cases held together before the synthesis begins. Best for books with multiple parallel cases or a systematic framework.

**Structure:** Restate central claim in sentence 1--2 --> Inventory the cases --> Signal what the conclusion will add.

**Paragraph 1:** State the thesis directly. Do not warm up. The first sentence should contain the book's core proposition or central question.

Patashnik (2008) opens with the thesis in sentence one:

> "The enactment of a policy reform, then, signals not the end of the political story, only the start of a new chapter. As this book has shown, the fates of major policy reforms across diverse domains can vary dramatically. Some reforms--Medicare, the deregulation of airlines and trucking, and tax reform in the 1980s--have proven quite durable. Others--including farm price supports, trade protectionism, and, to a lesser degree, campaign finance reform--have been substantially rolled back."

Note the "then" in the first sentence -- it signals this is a continuation, not a new beginning. The case inventory follows immediately.

Kadivar (2022) uses a question-answer format:

> "Why do some young democracies fail and others survive? This book proposes that a democracy's success or failure depends on the specific attributes of its pathway into existence. Democracies can emerge through protest mobilization or top-down processes such as international interventions or interactions between the elite."

Patashnik (2023) inventories three specific cases to anchor the synthesis:

> "The backlashes against the Affordable Care Act (ACA), the No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act, and the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), just three of the many cases explored in this book, were noteworthy events in recent American political history."

**Paragraph 2 (optional):** Signal what the conclusion will do beyond summary. Grumbach (2023) moves immediately from recap to forward-looking framing:

> "in the preceding chapters, I have argued that the nationalization of the Democratic and Republican party coalitions increased the importance of state-level policymaking in American federalism. The evidence I presented suggests that this state policymaking--whether it is in the area of health policy, the environment, or the rules of American democracy itself--has been driven by national groups with national concerns rather than the localized incentives that animate traditional theories of federalism."

**Total opening:** 150--400 words, 1--2 paragraphs.

---

#### Type B: Anecdote / Fieldwork Scene / Current Event (7/22 -- 32%)

**When to use:** When the book has a strong narrative or ethnographic register, when a new event has occurred that crystallizes the argument, or when the introduction opened with a parallel scene and the conclusion needs to bookend it.

**Structure:** Scene or vignette (new or callback) --> Pivot to the book's argument --> Signal the stakes.

**Paragraph 1:** Enter the scene. Use concrete details -- names, dates, places. The reader should not immediately know why this scene matters; the pivot comes in paragraph 2 or 3.

Tesler (2016) opens with Ferguson:

> "For the second consecutive summer of Barack Obama's second term in the White House, the country's precarious racial fault line ruptured over the fatal shooting of an unarmed black teenager. The killing of Michael Brown in Ferguson, Missouri, and the protests that followed thrust race back into the national spotlight in ways that had seemed unimaginable just a few years earlier, when Barack Obama became the first African American elected president."

Rodriguez-Muniz (2024) opens with the El Paso massacre:

> "On the morning of August 3, 2019, Patrick Crusius, a twenty-one-year-old white man, arrived in El Paso, Texas. He had driven over ten hours from his home in an affluent suburb of Dallas. Armed with a 'civilian' cousin of the AK-47 assault rifle, Crusius opened fire in a popular shopping mall miles from the U.S.-Mexico border and detention centers housing hundreds of unaccompanied minors."

Ternullo (2024) returns to a named informant:

> "in august 2021, the 2020 presidential election was in the rearview mirror, Joe Biden was installed in the White House, and the Covid-19 pandemic had briefly receded from center stage amid the widespread availability of vaccines. That month, I spoke to Isaac from Motorville. Isaac is a Democrat who, during every single conversation we had from October 2019 through the November 2020 presidential election, was preoccupied with protecting the working and middle classes."

Fisher (2019) returns to the field site:

> "I MET LINDA SARSOUR (a co-chair of the National Women's March) at the CNN studios in Washington, D.C. early in the morning of the third Women's March in January 2019."

Mettler (2011) introduces a family vignette:

> "When health care reform was debated in 2009 and 2010, many Americans knew little about the policies that already provided them with government assistance. In Overland Park, Kansas, an affluent suburb of Kansas City, people like Susan and Jeff Thompson benefited richly from such policies."

**Paragraph 2--3:** Pivot from the scene to the book's argument. The pivot sentence explicitly connects the anecdote to the thesis.

**Total opening:** 250--600 words, 2--3 paragraphs.

**Key technique:** If the introduction opened with one scene, the conclusion should open with a parallel or contrasting scene. Tesler's introduction opens with Trayvon Martin; the conclusion opens with Michael Brown. Fisher's introduction opens with her sister canvassing; the conclusion opens with the author at the 2019 Women's March. This structural bookending is the most reliable signal of a well-crafted conclusion.

---

#### Type C: Broadening Normative / Democratic Claim (5/22 -- 23%)

**When to use:** When the book's theoretical ambition is larger than its empirical scope and the conclusion needs to claim territory in a bigger scholarly conversation. Best for theory-building conclusions.

**Structure:** Pose a sweeping question or paradox --> Locate the book's contribution within it --> Signal the argument to come.

**Paragraph 1:** Step back to the broadest formulation of the problem. Use language that signals philosophical reach.

Han et al. (2021):

> "Democracy, demos kratia, is rooted in the idea that people have some control over the basic conditions and laws that rule our lives. Although the rhetoric of US democracy promises government 'of, by, and for' the people, the reality is that people rarely rule. In twenty-first-century America, the link between democratic participation and power seems broken."

Brown-Saracino (2018):

> "we live in a fascinating and contradictory moment. Much of american culture has taken vast strides in awareness about and acceptance of people who live outside the sexual norm. Many lesbians and gay men have moved into the mainstream of american culture and life, and their presence has become a matter of course in popular media and political discourse."

Mucciaroni (2008):

> "This book stands an old question on its head. Instead of asking, When can a nation's politics bring about social change? it has asked, Why does social change sometimes succeed and sometimes fail to help a group's political fortunes?"

Lichterman (2021):

> "Around the world, researchers are using something like the conceptual box of civic action to study social advocacy and participatory planning. What do we gain from adopting this alternative to the view of social advocates as strategic entrepreneurs?"

Krimmel (2024):

> "this book is about a theoretically pro-democratic development--the rise of party competition over public policy--in the United States, which has the highest level of such competition in the world."

**Total opening:** 200--400 words, 1--2 paragraphs.

---

#### Type D: Canonical Quote / Epigraph Entry (2/22 -- 9%)

**When to use:** When a foundational authority (a constitutional framer, a seminal theorist, a movement leader) provides the frame through which the book's contribution is best understood.

**Structure:** Invoke the authority --> Show how the book responds to or complicates the authority's claim.

Bolton & Thrower (2022):

> "Addressing a nascent nation wary of both monarchial presidents and tyrannical legislatures, James Madison and the other constitutional framers advocated for a system dividing power among three independent branches of government. This design, he hoped, would check rapacious political ambitions and prevent the aggrandizement of power in any one person or institution. Over the last century, however, deep and persistent reservations have emerged about whether the separation of powers system adequately limits executive power."

Pettinicchio (2019) uses an epigraph from a disability rights leader:

> Epigraph: "You've come a long way, baby... but there's still a very long way to go. Democracy is always unfinished business." (Alan Reich, 1988)

Then the body: "Disability rights represents the last frontier in America's so-called minority rights revolution."

**Total opening:** 150--350 words, 1--2 paragraphs.

---

### Opening Frame: Common Mistakes

- **Opening with pure summary.** "In this book, I argued that..." repeated for three paragraphs. None of the 22 books does this. Even thesis-restatement openings immediately add something new -- a case inventory, a question-answer structure, a paradox.
- **Opening with throat-clearing.** "This conclusion will discuss the implications of..." is never the first sentence. Start with the argument, not with a preview of the conclusion's structure.
- **Mismatching register.** If the book is ethnographic, do not open the conclusion with an abstract theoretical claim (use Type B, not Type C). If the book is comparative-institutional, do not open with a fieldwork scene.

## Section 2: Synthesis of Findings

**Target length:** 1,500--3,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 8--20 | **Proportion:** 20--35% of the conclusion

### What This Section Accomplishes

The synthesis section is the conclusion's analytical core. It produces a new cross-case comparison that was not available in any single empirical chapter -- a comparative table, a typology, a matrix, a set of meta-level propositions, or a numbered list of findings. This section demonstrates that the whole is greater than the sum of the parts. Nineteen of 22 conclusions (86%) include this move.

### How to Build a Cross-Case Synthesis

#### Step 1: Choose a Synthesis Device

The corpus uses five main devices:

**Comparative Table.** A new table that lines up all cases against the framework's key variables.
- Patashnik (2008): Table 9-1 summarizes shifts in institutions, interest-group feedback, and investment stimulation across all reform cases.
- Kadivar (2022): Table 8 (Case Summaries) covers South Africa, Poland, Pakistan, Egypt, and Tunisia along the same dimensions.
- Mettler (2011): Table 6.1 presents a "Rubric for Reform" -- criteria for evaluating proposals to reconstitute the submerged state.

**2x2 Matrix.** A typology that cross-classifies two key variables.
- Mucciaroni (2008): Combines issue type with institutional context to produce a matrix of alternative causal pathways for LGBT rights outcomes.

**Numbered Propositions.** A set of discrete findings enumerated as general claims.
- Patashnik (2023): Seven "broad conclusions" synthesized from the book's evidence on backlash politics.
- Kadivar (2022): Numbered implications for activists and policymakers drawn from the case comparisons.

**Paradox or Tension Set.** A list of paradoxes that the cases collectively reveal.
- Patashnik (2008): "Paradoxes of Reform" section identifies contradictions inherent in the reform process.

**Typological Synthesis.** A classification of outcomes or patterns across the cases.
- Smith & King (2024): Six future scenarios for America's racial politics, generated from the Protect/Repair alliance framework.

#### Step 2: Structure the Synthesis Section

**Recipe:**

1. **Transition paragraph** (1 paragraph, ~100--200 words): Signal that you are moving from the opening to the synthesis. Name the analytical framework and announce that you will now apply it across all cases. Patashnik (2008) does this with the heading "EXPLAINING PATTERNS OF REFORM SUSTAINABILITY" followed by a paragraph that names the three mechanisms being compared.

2. **Variable-by-variable analysis** (3--8 paragraphs, ~800--2,000 words): Work through each of the framework's key variables, showing how each operated across the cases. Use one paragraph per variable or per case comparison. Include the table, matrix, or numbered propositions here.

3. **Emergent patterns** (2--4 paragraphs, ~400--800 words): Identify patterns that only become visible when cases are compared -- paradoxes, unexpected alignments, scope conditions. Patashnik (2008) identifies five paradoxes of reform that emerge from the cross-case comparison. Mucciaroni (2008) identifies alternative causal pathways visible only in the matrix.

#### Step 3: Write the Synthesis Paragraphs

Each synthesis paragraph should follow a consistent internal structure:

1. Name the variable or dimension being assessed
2. Show how it operated across two or more cases (with brief evidence)
3. State the comparative finding

**Example of effective cross-case synthesis prose (Mucciaroni 2008):**

The conclusion systematically works through three institutional variables -- stakeholders, federalism, judicial policymaking -- comparing how each shaped outcomes across the sodomy, adoption, military, and marriage cases. Each variable gets its own thematic block, and within each block, the author compares the "success" cases (sodomy repeal, adoption expansion) against the "failure" cases (military ban, marriage restriction).

**Example of effective synthesis prose (Patashnik 2008):**

> "If reformers want carbon pricing to stick, they need to make it stick. They should take a cue from the designers of Medicare and build automatic escalators into their proposals wherever possible, seek to stimulate private investments in clean technologies that create their own domestic political constituencies, and put in place international commitments that raise the political costs of future backsliding."

This passage synthesizes across Medicare (success case) and tax reform (failure case) to generate a prescriptive claim applicable to a new policy domain.

### Word Count Guidance

| Conclusion Type | Typical Synthesis Length | % of Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| Policy Architect | 2,000--3,000 words | 25--35% |
| Ethnographic Bookend | 800--1,500 words | 20--30% |
| Theory Builder | 1,500--2,500 words | 20--25% |
| Democratic Reckoning | 1,500--3,000 words | 20--30% |
| Institutional Historian | 1,500--2,500 words | 25--30% |

### Common Mistakes

- **Restating each chapter in order.** "Chapter 3 showed... Chapter 4 showed..." is a summary, not a synthesis. Synthesis reorganizes findings around the framework's variables, not around the book's chapter sequence.
- **Synthesis without a device.** If the synthesis section does not produce a table, matrix, numbered list, or explicit set of propositions, it is doing descriptive summary, not analytical synthesis.
- **Over-qualifying.** "Of course, each case is unique..." repeated for every comparison. State caveats once (see Section 2b), then commit to the comparative claims.

## Section 3: New Material

**Target length:** 1,000--3,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 5--15

### What This Section Accomplishes

All 22 conclusions (100%) introduce material not found in the empirical chapters. This is a universal genre feature, not an exception. The new material section demonstrates that the book's framework has analytical reach beyond the cases already studied. It also keeps the conclusion from being purely backward-looking.

### Types of New Material and When to Use Each

#### Type 1: New Empirical Case or Vignette (15/22 -- 68%)

Introduce an entirely new case, event, or interview that was not analyzed in the body chapters. This is typically a case from after the main research period, or a case that was outside the original scope but illuminates the argument.

**When to use:** When events have occurred since the fieldwork that dramatically illustrate the argument's stakes, or when a brief new case can serve as a "stress test" of the framework.

**Examples:**

Rodriguez-Muniz (2024) introduces the El Paso massacre:
> "On the morning of August 3, 2019, Patrick Crusius, a twenty-one-year-old white man, arrived in El Paso, Texas."

This new case crystallizes the book's argument about "demodystopian" white supremacist demographic imaginaries. The El Paso vignette runs approximately 4 paragraphs and connects to the book's core concepts without requiring a full case study.

Fisher (2019) introduces 2019 Women's March survey data:
> "I MET LINDA SARSOUR (a co-chair of the National Women's March) at the CNN studios in Washington, D.C. early in the morning of the third Women's March in January 2019."

Fisher's new data -- a complete survey from after the book's main empirical period -- makes the conclusion function partly as a research update.

Grumbach (2023) introduces two new cases: Florida's Amendment 4 (ex-felon disenfranchisement) and the COVID-19/Cuomo case study (~8 paragraphs) to illustrate federalism's accountability failures.

Tesler (2016) opens the entire conclusion with Ferguson -- an event that occurred during the Obama presidency but after the book's main empirical analysis.

Lowande (2024) devotes the conclusion's final section to the Idaho Lt. Governor Janice McGeachin vignette (~6 paragraphs) -- an entirely new empirical case about subnational executive power.

**Recipe for introducing a new case:**

1. Open with concrete narrative details (names, dates, places)
2. Let the case unfold for 2--4 paragraphs before interpreting it
3. Connect explicitly to the book's framework: "This case illustrates..."
4. Keep the new case shorter than the empirical chapter cases (2--6 paragraphs, not 20--40 pages)

#### Type 2: Contemporary Event Update (9/22 -- 41%)

Introduce material from after the main empirical period -- Trump-era developments, COVID-19, January 6, election results.

**When to use:** When the political landscape has shifted since the fieldwork and the book must demonstrate awareness of what happened next.

**Examples:**

Bolton & Thrower (2022) introduce Trump's COVID-19 executive orders, the border wall emergency declaration, and January 6 as tests of their legislative capacity framework. The Trump material runs approximately 10 paragraphs and includes specific policy references (EO 13945, CDC eviction moratorium).

Grumbach (2023) discusses Cuomo and COVID-19 in ~8 paragraphs to illustrate decentralized accountability failures.

Smith & King (2024) devote substantial space to the 2022 midterm elections, DeSantis's Florida victory, and January 6 convictions as updates to their racial alliances framework.

**Recipe for contemporary updates:**

1. Signal explicitly that you are extending the timeline: "Since the completion of this research..."
2. Apply the framework rather than merely reporting events
3. Be honest about what the new events confirm and what they complicate
4. Keep updates to 500--1,500 words unless the book's structure demands more (Smith & King's two-chapter conclusion is the outlier, not the model)

#### Type 3: Cross-Case Synthesis Devices (19/22 -- 86%)

New tables, matrices, typologies, or meta-level propositions that require the prior chapters as foundation but are not presented elsewhere. These are covered in Section 2 above.

#### Type 4: Prescriptive Frameworks (16/22 -- 73%)

New normative recommendations, strategies, or tactical advice. These are covered in Section 5 below.

### Word Count Guidance for New Material

| New Material Type | Typical Length | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Brief illustrative vignette | 300--600 words | Lowande (McGeachin), Ternullo (summer 2022 follow-up) |
| Extended new case | 800--2,000 words | Rodriguez-Muniz (El Paso + Census 2020 battle), Tesler (Ferguson) |
| Contemporary update | 500--1,500 words | Bolton & Thrower (Trump), Grumbach (COVID/Cuomo) |
| Full new empirical section | 2,000--5,000+ words | Smith & King (2022 elections, international material), Fisher (2019 survey) |

### Common Mistakes

- **Introducing so much new material that the conclusion becomes a new chapter.** The conclusion should demonstrate the framework's reach, not extend the empirical analysis. Smith & King's 27,000-word two-chapter conclusion is the outlier. Most new material sections run 1,000--2,500 words.
- **New material without framework connection.** Every new case, event, or data point must be explicitly connected to the book's analytical framework. A Ferguson opening without connecting it to the "most-racial" thesis is just journalism.
- **No new material at all.** All 22 conclusions introduce something new. A conclusion that only looks backward is incomplete.

## Section 4: Theoretical Broadening

**Target length:** 1,000--2,500 words | **Paragraphs:** 5--12

### What This Section Accomplishes

The theoretical broadening section connects the book's findings to scholarly conversations not centrally addressed in the empirical chapters. It demonstrates that the book contributes to literatures beyond its primary one. Nineteen of 22 conclusions (86%) include this move. This is where the conclusion earns its place as more than a summary -- it claims intellectual territory.

### How to Engage New Literatures Without Starting a New Argument

The key discipline: the theoretical broadening section extends the book's existing framework to new terrain. It does not introduce a new framework, start a new argument, or pivot to a different research program. The existing findings do the work; the new literature provides the context.

#### Pattern 1: The Parallel Framework Comparison

Connect the book's framework to an existing theoretical tradition by showing structural parallels.

Vogler (2021) connects her classification framework to Science and Technology Studies (STS):

> "Sorting Sexualities has argued that the techno-legal classification practices that state actors engage in naturalize social distinctions along the lines of sexuality and engender new subject positions and forms of social control based around those classifications."

The conclusion then develops an extended comparison between sexuality classification and racial formation theory (Omi & Winant), culminating in the new concept of "sexuality as technology" (paralleling critical race scholars on race as technology -- Ruha Benjamin, Wendy Hui Kyong Chun, Beth Coleman).

**Recipe:** Name the parallel tradition --> Show the structural similarity --> Propose the conceptual bridge --> Cite 3--5 key works from the new tradition.

#### Pattern 2: The Epistemological Defense

Defend the book's conceptual approach by engaging with philosophy of science or methodology debates.

Lichterman (2021) invokes Dewey's pragmatism and feminist epistemologist Helen Longino to defend conceptual pluralism. The conclusion argues that the "entrepreneurial actor model's background assumptions are limiting" and that the book's "civic action" framework offers a "bigger box" for understanding social advocacy. This is not a new argument -- it is an epistemological justification for the book's existing approach.

**Recipe:** Name the methodological or conceptual challenge --> Invoke the philosophical tradition that supports your approach --> Show how your empirical findings illustrate the philosophical point.

#### Pattern 3: The Neighboring Discipline Extension

Show what the findings mean for a neighboring discipline or subfield.

Brown-Saracino (2018) connects her place-identity findings to the urban ecology tradition (Wirth, Park) and to assimilation theory:

> "we must inquire into local cultures--past, present, and future--and especially into the city ecologies that generate them."

She proposes a "differentiated social geography of identity" that challenges the universal assimilation narrative. The engagement with urban sociology is new to the conclusion -- the empirical chapters stay within the sociology of sexuality and identity.

Mucciaroni (2008) extends from LGBT politics to general social movement theory, situating his institutional findings within the movement outcomes literature.

Pettinicchio (2019) engages Selznick's co-optation theory as an explanation for post-ADA demobilization -- a theoretical frame mentioned but not developed in the empirical chapters.

**Recipe:** Name the neighboring literature --> State what your findings add to it --> Propose a specific proposition or concept that the neighboring field should adopt --> Cite 3--8 key works.

#### Pattern 4: The Rival Theory Engagement

Evaluate rival explanations that the empirical chapters did not fully address.

Smith & King (2024) devote a full chapter section to assessing three rival theories of racial politics: capitalism-focused theories, white hegemony theories, and intersectional theories. Each gets a dedicated subsection with evidence from the book's cases.

Rodriguez-Muniz (2024) critiques "demography is destiny" naturalism by engaging with Appelbaum, Eatwell/Goodwin, Kaufmann, and Richard Alba -- scholars whose work provides the intellectual foil for his "population politics" alternative.

**Recipe:** Name the rival theory --> Summarize its prediction --> Show where your evidence supports or contradicts it --> State your verdict.

### Word Count Guidance

| Conclusion Type | Typical Theoretical Broadening Length |
|---|---|
| Theory Builder | 2,000--4,000 words (the conclusion's center of gravity) |
| Policy Architect | 500--1,500 words (compact theory section) |
| Democratic Reckoning | 1,000--2,500 words |
| Ethnographic Bookend | 500--1,000 words |
| Institutional Historian | 800--1,500 words |

### Common Mistakes

- **Starting a new argument.** If the theoretical broadening section requires more than 5--8 new citations, it may be becoming a new literature review. The conclusion extends; it does not originate.
- **Name-dropping without engagement.** Citing Bourdieu or Foucault without showing what the book's findings specifically add to their frameworks. Each cited scholar must be connected to a specific finding.
- **Engaging only the book's primary literature.** The theoretical broadening section should reach beyond the main conversation. If the book is about policy feedback, this section should connect to democratic theory, movement theory, or institutional design -- not rehearse the policy feedback literature again.

## Section 5: Prescriptive / Implications Section

**Target length:** 800--2,500 words | **Paragraphs:** 4--12

### What This Section Accomplishes

The prescriptive section shifts the conclusion's register from analytical to advisory. It addresses practitioners, policymakers, advocates, or the broader public and states what should be done in light of the book's findings. Sixteen of 22 conclusions (73%) include this move. This is where the author's normative commitments become most visible.

### How to Shift to Practitioner / Policy Voice

#### Step 1: Signal the Register Change

Most authors signal the shift explicitly with a heading or a transitional sentence:

- Patashnik (2008): "SOME PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS" (heading)
- Kadivar (2022): Separate sections on "Implications for activists" and "Implications for policymakers"
- Grumbach (2023): "Centralizing American Democracy" (heading for policy proposals)
- Patashnik (2023): "Strategic Approaches to Backlash Risks" (heading) followed by "Tactics for Managing Backlash Risks"

#### Step 2: Structure the Prescriptions

The corpus reveals three structural patterns:

**Numbered Strategies (Patashnik model).** The most common format. List discrete, actionable recommendations as numbered items.

Patashnik (2008) offers five strategies for reformers -- the conclusion's most-cited section:
> "They should take a cue from the designers of Medicare and build automatic escalators into their proposals wherever possible, seek to stimulate private investments in clean technologies that create their own domestic political constituencies, and put in place international commitments that raise the political costs of future backsliding."

Patashnik (2023) provides four strategic options and five tactical recommendations:
1. Uproot institutional bases of opposition
2. Fragment opponents
3. Balance responsiveness to base and marginal supporters
4. Increase progressivity after embedding
5. Recognize that expansions can threaten existing beneficiaries

**Audience-Differentiated Implications (Kadivar model).** Separate sections for different audiences.

Kadivar (2022) divides implications into two dedicated sections:
- For activists: formal organizing, election vs. boycott dilemma, unity in transition
- For policymakers: trade unions vs. NGOs for democracy promotion

**Institutional Reform Proposals (Bolton & Thrower model).** Specific policy or institutional reforms with detailed analysis.

Bolton & Thrower (2022) devote substantial space to:
- Evaluating the 2019 House Select Committee on the Modernization of Congress
- Assessing prospects for OTA (Office of Technology Assessment) revival
- Recommending strengthening GAO instead of OTA
- Specific staffing recommendations (associate staff, chair term limits, salary increases)

Grumbach (2023) proposes specific institutional reforms:
- Expansionary fiscal federalism (historical ACIR framework)
- A Federal Elections Agency
- Anti-gerrymandering enforcement
- Electoral and organizing recommendations for pro-democracy coalitions

His closing prescription:

> "pro-democracy coalitions, whether partisan or nonpartisan, should use the power they gain at the national level to shift authority upward and away from the state level, where budgets are constrained, voters have less information, business and the wealthy can quickly flood political battles with money--and where threats to democracy continue to arise."

#### Step 3: Ground Prescriptions in Evidence

Every prescription must trace back to a specific finding from the empirical chapters. Abstract advice ("policymakers should be more attentive") does not work. Effective prescriptions name the mechanism:

Mucciaroni (2008):
> "advocates can hasten success if they have a clear understanding of the constraints they face, devise strategies and tactics to mitigate them, and are ready when auspicious historical moments hold out realistic possibilities for reform."

The "constraints" are the institutional variables analyzed across six chapters. The "strategies" are derived from the successful cases.

Mettler (2011): Table 6.1 provides a rubric for evaluating reform proposals -- visibility, equity, cost, political feasibility -- directly derived from the "submerged state" framework.

### Word Count Guidance

| Conclusion Type | Typical Prescriptive Section Length |
|---|---|
| Policy Architect | 2,000--4,000 words (the conclusion's center of gravity) |
| Democratic Reckoning | 1,000--2,500 words |
| Institutional Historian | 800--2,000 words |
| Theory Builder | 500--1,000 words (often folded into research agenda) |
| Ethnographic Bookend | 300--800 words |

### Common Mistakes

- **Prescriptions without evidence.** If the empirical chapters did not study the mechanism being prescribed, the prescription is speculation, not implication.
- **Generic advice.** "Policymakers should pay more attention to X" without specifying what "attention" means operationally. Name the specific institutional lever, strategic choice, or tactical move.
- **Sounding like an op-ed.** The prescriptive section should maintain the author's scholarly authority. Bolton & Thrower discuss OTA revival with the same analytical precision they bring to their quantitative analysis. Grumbach's fiscal federalism proposals cite specific historical precedents (ACIR).

## Section 6: Forward-Looking Close

**Target length:** 150--500 words | **Paragraphs:** 1--3

### What This Section Accomplishes

The close is the last thing the reader remembers. It must land. Twenty of 22 conclusions (91%) include a forward-looking element. The close should crystallize the book's contribution in a single quotable moment and leave the reader with a sense of why the argument matters beyond the academy.

### Recipe by Closing Type

---

#### Type 1: Conditional Optimism / Forward-Looking Application (8/22 -- 36%)

**When to use:** When the book's findings suggest that positive outcomes are possible but contingent. Most common closing type.

**Structure:** Apply the framework to a prospective case or emerging challenge --> State the condition for success --> End on a cautiously hopeful note.

Patashnik (2008):
> "Reform sustainability requires forethought--but it is not beyond reach."

Kadivar (2022):
> "Prolonged mobilization and an organized civil and political society can give new democracies a stronger starting point, but, as democracies age, their health depends on the vigilance and care of social and political forces, the ones that shape and maintain democratic institutions through organizing, mobilizing, and coalition building."

Fisher (2019):
> "Collaboration among a presidential campaign, the Democratic Party, and resistance groups could extend the distributed organizing model to cultivate a strong progressive infrastructure in American communities. If successful, it will leave as its legacy a lasting root structure when the 2020 election is behind us."

Smith & King (2024):
> "there is no reason to conclude that these efforts must fail. To the contrary, there is every reason to try to help them succeed."

Abrams (2022):
> "the ability to perceive, reflect, and adapt, in response to new information or changing circumstances, exemplifies the strength that undocumented activists will carry with them as they move into an uncertain future."

Mucciaroni (2008):
> "advocates can hasten success if they have a clear understanding of the constraints they face, devise strategies and tactics to mitigate them, and are ready when auspicious historical moments hold out realistic possibilities for reform."

**Key technique:** The conditional clause ("if," "when," "provided that") is doing the work. It signals that the author is not naive but refuses despair. The most effective versions name the specific condition.

---

#### Type 2: Call to Action / Democratic Imperative (5/22 -- 23%)

**When to use:** When the book diagnoses a democratic crisis or institutional failure and the author's normative commitments demand an explicit exhortation.

**Structure:** State the stakes --> Name the audience --> Issue the call.

Mettler (2011):
> "Only by making government visible and ensuring that it promotes social equity can we reinvigorate democracy and revitalize citizenship."

Han et al. (2021):
> "We sought to generate a set of propositions about how organizations acting as prisms of people power operate in order to deepen understanding of how participation translates into political influence. By making the way these metaphorical prisms work more visible, we hope to give people some of the tools needed to become more intentional about rebuilding our civic capacities. Our democracy depends on it."

Grumbach (2023):
> "pro-democracy coalitions... should use the power they gain at the national level to shift authority upward and away from the state level."

Lichterman (2021):
> "We need people who figure out what civic action can and cannot do to bend history's arc toward greater justice, solidarity, and sustainability. There is a lot to do."

Vogler (2021):
> "Sexuality as a technology of social organization, domination, and resistance is everywhere. Our job is to start recognizing it."

**Key technique:** The shift to "we" or "our" is the register signal. The author steps from analyst to participant. Use this move only when the book's evidence has earned the authority to prescribe.

---

#### Type 3: Thesis Crystallization / Verdict (3/22 -- 14%)

**When to use:** When the book poses a question in its title or introduction that demands a definitive answer. The closing restates the answer with maximum force.

**Structure:** Return to the book's central question --> Deliver the verdict in one or two sentences.

Tesler (2016):
> "Far from ushering in the onset of a post-racial society, then, Obama's presidency should stand out as yet another profound and sobering reminder of the ongoing importance of race in America."

Brown-Saracino (2018):
> "we make places, but places also--and far more than we might like to believe--make us."

Ternullo (2024):
> "the product of an often-invisible web of social organization, social structure, and party politics that weave all Americans into the polity."

**Key technique:** The verdict sentence should echo the book's title or central formulation. Tesler answers "Post-Racial or Most-Racial?" Brown-Saracino answers "How Places Make Us." The echo creates closure.

---

#### Type 4: Diagnostic Warning / Pessimism (2/22 -- 9%)

**When to use:** When the book's evidence points to institutional failure or democratic decline and honest assessment demands sobriety rather than hope.

Dearborn (2021):
> "If we have lost that capacity, we may be in real trouble. For any of our ideas of institutional reform to become 'more powerful than is commonly understood,' they must be able to pivot off of a shared indictment of what has gone wrong with American government."

Bolton & Thrower (2022):
> "Without capacity, the promise of separation of powers will wither. Indeed, checks are in the balance."

**Key technique:** The warning closing works only when the preceding analysis has been balanced. If the entire conclusion has been pessimistic, the closing will feel repetitive. If the conclusion has been largely optimistic, the closing warning will feel abrupt. The diagnostic warning works best after a section of institutional analysis that has honestly assessed both strengths and weaknesses.

---

#### Type 5: Epistemological / Open-Ended Reflection (2/22 -- 9%)

Rodriguez-Muniz (2024):
> "Can we envision demography without guarantees? In this spirit, this book does not tell what the future holds. Its author does not have the powers of Tiresias. I am only confident that the shape of what is to come will bear, in some way, the signature of the racialized population politics being carried out today in its name."

Krimmel (2024):
> "We must continue the conversation about how and why party systems evolve, and how we can define and promote healthy party competition in the United States and around the world."

---

#### Type 6: Narrative Vignette (1/22 -- 5%)

Lowande (2024) closes with the McGeachin story's punchline:
> "I am one heartbeat away from the governor's chair."

This is the rarest closing type. It works only when the vignette so perfectly embodies the thesis that no analytical statement could improve on it.

---

#### Type 7: Scholarly Contribution Statement (1/22 -- 5%)

Patashnik (2023):
> "The study of backlash politics thus expands and refines our understanding of the multiple ways in which 'new policies create a new politics.'"

This is the rarest deliberate closing. It works for books whose primary audience is disciplinary and whose contribution claim is strong enough to stand as a final statement.

---

### Common Mistakes in Closing

- **Ending with a research agenda.** "Future research should examine..." is a penultimate move, not a final sentence. Place the research agenda before the close, not as the close.
- **Ending with a long summary paragraph.** The close should crystallize, not rehash. If the final paragraph begins "In sum, this book has argued that..." the reader has already stopped reading.
- **False triumphalism.** "This book has definitively shown..." is almost never the right register. Even Tesler's "most-racial" verdict is framed as "a sobering reminder," not a celebration.
- **No close at all.** Some academic books simply stop rather than ending. None of the 22 in this corpus does this. The close is a deliberate rhetorical act.

## Cluster-Specific Full Recipes

These recipes show the complete section sequence for each of the five conclusion clusters, with word count targets and exemplar references.

---

### Recipe: The Policy Architect Conclusion

**Exemplars:** Patashnik 2008, Patashnik 2023, Bolton & Thrower 2022, Kadivar 2022

**Total target length:** 6,000--10,000 words | **Named sections:** 4--8

**Distinguishing feature:** The prescriptive section is the conclusion's center of gravity. The Policy Architect conclusion devotes its largest and most original section to *what should be done*.

| Section | Target Length | % of Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening: Thesis restatement + case inventory | 200--400 words | 3--5% |
| 2. Cross-case synthesis (table or matrix) | 2,000--3,000 words | 25--35% |
| 3. Paradoxes or complications | 500--1,000 words | 8--12% |
| 4. **Prescriptive strategies and tactics** | **2,000--4,000 words** | **30--40%** |
| 5. Forward-looking application | 500--1,000 words | 8--12% |
| 6. Close: Cautious optimism or contribution statement | 150--300 words | 2--3% |

**Step-by-step recipe:**

1. **Open with thesis restatement.** Name the central claim and inventory the cases. Use the Patashnik model: thesis in sentence 1, case parade in sentences 2--4.

2. **Build the synthesis.** Produce a new comparative table or matrix. Patashnik (2008) creates Table 9-1; Kadivar creates Table 8; Mucciaroni creates the 2x2 matrix. Work through each variable systematically. This is the analytical foundation for the prescriptions that follow.

3. **Identify paradoxes or complications.** State what the comparison reveals that is counterintuitive. Patashnik (2008) identifies five "paradoxes of reform." This section earns the reader's trust before the prescriptive turn.

4. **Write the prescriptive section.** This is the conclusion's main deliverable. Use numbered strategies (Patashnik model) or audience-differentiated sections (Kadivar model). Ground every prescription in a specific finding. Patashnik (2023) provides four strategic options plus five tactics. Bolton & Thrower provide specific institutional reform proposals (OTA, staffing, salary).

5. **Apply the framework forward.** Take the framework to a prospective case. Patashnik (2008) applies the reform sustainability framework to cap-and-trade/carbon tax policy. Patashnik (2023) speculates about "the coming liberal backlash."

6. **Close with conditional optimism or a contribution statement.** "Reform sustainability requires forethought--but it is not beyond reach" (Patashnik 2008). "The study of backlash politics thus expands and refines our understanding..." (Patashnik 2023).

### Recipe: The Ethnographic Bookend Conclusion

**Exemplars:** Ternullo 2024, Fisher 2019, Abrams 2022

**Total target length:** 3,500--6,000 words | **Named sections:** 2--4

**Distinguishing feature:** These are the shortest conclusions. They privilege narrative continuity over theoretical ambition. The new material is experiential (follow-up interviews, updated fieldwork) rather than analytical.

| Section | Target Length | % of Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening: Anecdote or fieldwork callback | 300--600 words | 8--15% |
| 2. Compact theoretical synthesis | 800--1,500 words | 20--30% |
| 3. Implications for practitioners or the public | 500--1,000 words | 12--20% |
| 4. Humanizing or reflective close | 150--400 words | 4--8% |

**Step-by-step recipe:**

1. **Open with a return to the field.** Revisit a named informant (Ternullo returns to Isaac from Motorville; Fisher returns to the Women's March; Abrams inventories the movement's "uncanny victories"). If the introduction opened with a specific person or scene, the conclusion should mirror it. The new fieldwork material goes here -- Ternullo's summer 2022 follow-up conversations, Fisher's 2019 survey data.

2. **Provide a compact theoretical synthesis.** State what the cases collectively demonstrate about the book's framework. Keep this section tight -- 800--1,500 words. Ternullo synthesizes a "Theory of Place-Based Partisanship." Abrams applies Marshall Ganz's "strategic capacity" framework to the Phoenix movement as a whole.

3. **Address implications.** Shift briefly to what the findings mean for practitioners, the public, or the broader polity. Abrams draws lessons for "legal actors, to those who organize immigrant and other 'challenger' movements, and to those who study them." Ternullo discusses implications for understanding heartland politics beyond the three studied cities.

4. **Close with a humanizing or reflective gesture.** Return to the people studied. Ternullo closes by reframing political difference as ordinary:
> "the product of an often-invisible web of social organization, social structure, and party politics that weave all Americans into the polity."

Abrams closes with forward-looking uncertainty:
> "the ability to perceive, reflect, and adapt... exemplifies the strength that undocumented activists will carry with them as they move into an uncertain future."

Fisher closes with conditional optimism:
> "If successful, it will leave as its legacy a lasting root structure when the 2020 election is behind us."

### Recipe: The Theory Builder Conclusion

**Exemplars:** Vogler 2021, Lichterman 2021, Brown-Saracino 2018

**Total target length:** 7,000--13,000 words | **Named sections:** 4--5

**Distinguishing feature:** These conclusions read like the theoretical chapter the book has been building toward. They are the most heavily cited conclusions. The new material is primarily theoretical propositions and engagement with new literatures.

| Section | Target Length | % of Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening: Broadening normative or epistemological claim | 200--500 words | 3--5% |
| 2. Engagement with rival theories or new literatures | 2,000--4,000 words | 25--35% |
| 3. New theoretical proposition (the main deliverable) | 2,000--3,000 words | 25--30% |
| 4. Research agenda or field-expanding call | 1,000--2,000 words | 12--18% |
| 5. Close: Field-expanding call or epistemological reflection | 200--500 words | 3--5% |

**Step-by-step recipe:**

1. **Open with a broadening claim.** Lichterman opens with a global claim about researchers using civic action frameworks "around the world." Brown-Saracino opens with the paradox of increased acceptance alongside persistent difference. The opening should signal that the conclusion will operate at a higher level of abstraction than the empirical chapters.

2. **Engage rival theories.** This is where the Theory Builder conclusion does its heaviest lifting. Vogler engages racial formation theory (Omi & Winant), homonationalism (Puar), and STS literature on classification. Lichterman engages Dewey's pragmatism and feminist epistemology. Brown-Saracino engages urban ecology (Wirth, Park), assimilation theory, and the persistence-of-enclaves debate. Smith & King (secondary Theory Builder) evaluate capitalism-focused, white hegemony, and intersectional theories of racial politics.

   **Recipe for rival theory engagement:**
   1. Name the rival framework (1 paragraph)
   2. Summarize its prediction for your cases (1 paragraph)
   3. Show where your evidence aligns and where it diverges (2--3 paragraphs)
   4. State your verdict: does the rival theory explain what yours explains? (1 paragraph)

3. **Introduce the new theoretical proposition.** This is the conclusion's main deliverable. Vogler proposes "sexuality as technology." Brown-Saracino proposes "differentiated social geography of identity." Lichterman proposes a researcher-practitioner dialogue model grounded in civic action concepts. The proposition should be nameable in a phrase and should emerge clearly from the rival-theory engagement.

4. **Set the research agenda.** State what remains to be studied. Frame this as an invitation to the field, not a laundry list. Vogler names the domains where "sexuality as technology" should be applied (FOSTA-SESTA, facial recognition, algorithmic discrimination). Brown-Saracino raises the historical question ("Have Places Always Made Us?") and the digital question (what happens to place-based identities online?).

5. **Close with a field-expanding call.**

   Vogler: "Sexuality as a technology of social organization, domination, and resistance is everywhere. Our job is to start recognizing it."

   Lichterman: "There is a lot to do."

   Brown-Saracino: "we make places, but places also--and far more than we might like to believe--make us."

### Recipe: The Democratic Reckoning Conclusion

**Exemplars:** Tesler 2016, Grumbach 2023, Han et al. 2021, Smith & King 2024, Rodriguez-Muniz 2024

**Total target length:** 5,000--12,000 words (high variance) | **Named sections:** 4--8+

**Distinguishing feature:** These conclusions are animated by political urgency. They introduce the most new empirical material because the political landscape has shifted since the fieldwork. The scope shift is always toward "what this means for democracy."

| Section | Target Length | % of Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening: Scene of crisis or new case | 300--800 words | 5--10% |
| 2. Summary of findings | 1,000--2,000 words | 15--20% |
| 3. New empirical material (elections, crises, events) | 1,500--5,000 words | 20--35% |
| 4. Broadening to democratic theory | 1,000--2,500 words | 15--20% |
| 5. Prescriptions or coalition advice | 800--2,000 words | 10--15% |
| 6. Close: Call to action or cautious hope | 200--500 words | 3--5% |

**Step-by-step recipe:**

1. **Open with a scene of political crisis or a new case.** Tesler opens with Ferguson. Rodriguez-Muniz opens with El Paso. Han et al. open with a broadening democratic claim ("Democracy, demos kratia..."). SoRelle opens with the FDR/Obama comparison. The opening must signal urgency without abandoning analytical rigor.

2. **Summarize findings compactly.** Unlike the Policy Architect, whose synthesis is the centerpiece, the Democratic Reckoning conclusion moves quickly through the synthesis to get to the new material. Grumbach summarizes in two paragraphs before announcing what the conclusion will do. Tesler includes a "Qualifying the Results" section with three formal caveats to avoid overclaiming.

3. **Introduce new empirical material.** This is what distinguishes the Democratic Reckoning from other clusters. The material is from after the main empirical period and is chosen to demonstrate the argument's ongoing relevance.

   Tesler (2016): Ferguson protests, spillover effects beyond race.
   Grumbach (2023): Florida Amendment 4, Cuomo/COVID (~8 paragraphs each).
   Rodriguez-Muniz (2024): El Paso massacre (~4 paragraphs) + Census 2020 citizenship question battle (~15 paragraphs).
   Han et al. (2021): One PHX ID municipal ID card collapse in Phoenix.
   Smith & King (2024): 2022 midterm elections, global nationalism (Budapest CPAC, NatCon conferences), hate crime data, six future scenarios.

4. **Broaden to democratic theory.** Connect the specific findings to what they mean for participation, representation, legitimacy, or democratic health. Han et al. discuss authoritarian tendencies in committed civil society (citing Berman, Armony, Riley). Tesler asks whether racialization will persist. Rodriguez-Muniz critiques "demography is destiny" naturalism.

5. **Offer prescriptions or coalition advice.** Grumbach proposes expansionary fiscal federalism and a Federal Elections Agency. Han et al. propose rebuilding civic capacities through "prism" organizations. The prescriptions are about democratic health rather than specific policy design (distinguishing this cluster from the Policy Architect).

6. **Close with a call to action or cautious hope.**

   Han et al.: "Our democracy depends on it."
   Grumbach: "pro-democracy coalitions... should use the power they gain at the national level to shift authority upward."
   Rodriguez-Muniz: "I am only confident that the shape of what is to come will bear, in some way, the signature of the racialized population politics being carried out today in its name."

### Recipe: The Institutional Historian Conclusion

**Exemplars:** Dearborn 2021, SoRelle 2020, Mettler 2011, Lowande 2024, Pettinicchio 2019

**Total target length:** 5,000--12,000 words | **Named sections:** 4--7

**Distinguishing feature:** These conclusions extend a historical or institutional narrative into the present. Their new material tends to be archival, legal, or institutional rather than ethnographic or statistical. The emotional register tends toward sobriety or warning rather than hope.

| Section | Target Length | % of Conclusion |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening: Counterintuitive synthesis or anecdote | 200--600 words | 3--8% |
| 2. Historical/institutional narrative extension | 2,000--4,000 words | 25--35% |
| 3. Assessment of contemporary developments | 1,500--3,000 words | 20--30% |
| 4. Policy implications or reform assessment | 800--2,000 words | 10--18% |
| 5. Close: Diagnostic warning or cautionary reflection | 150--400 words | 2--5% |

**Step-by-step recipe:**

1. **Open with a counterintuitive synthesis or a scene.** Dearborn opens with the book's most provocative formulation: "Congress was the prime mover in the creation of the 'modern presidency.'" SoRelle opens with the FDR/Obama *Time* magazine comparison. Mettler opens with the Thompson family anecdote. Lowande opens with a rhetorical question about presidential power.

2. **Extend the institutional narrative.** This is the section that distinguishes the Institutional Historian. Continue the book's historical story beyond the empirical chapters' endpoint.

   Dearborn (2021) traces the unitary executive theory from Nixon's Ehrlichman memo through Reagan/Meese's formalism to the CFPB Supreme Court decision (*Seila Law v. CFPB* 2020). This narrative extends the book's timeline by decades.

   SoRelle (2020) narrates the Mulvaney takeover of the CFPB and subsequent policy reversals -- events post-dating the fieldwork.

   Pettinicchio (2019) traces Trump's attacks on ADA enforcement as the latest iteration of the "policy-participation cycle."

3. **Assess contemporary institutional developments.** Apply the framework to the present moment. Bolton & Thrower evaluate Trump's COVID orders, the border wall emergency declaration, and January 6 through their legislative capacity framework. Dearborn evaluates Howell and Moe's constitutional amendment proposal against the book's historical analysis.

4. **State policy implications.** The Institutional Historian's prescriptions tend to be about institutional design rather than coalition strategy. Mettler offers Table 6.1's "Rubric for Reform." Bolton & Thrower recommend strengthening GAO and congressional staffing. SoRelle proposes reframing consumer finance as "economic security."

5. **Close with a diagnostic warning.**

   Dearborn: "If we have lost that capacity, we may be in real trouble."
   Bolton & Thrower: "Indeed, checks are in the balance."
   SoRelle: "the outcome of today's battles will make a lasting impression not only on the economic fortunes of future generations of American borrowers but on their politics as well."

## Quick-Reference Drafting Checklist

- [ ] **Opening:** Thesis restated or dramatized in first 1--3 paragraphs. Register matches book's dominant mode.
- [ ] **Synthesis:** New analytical device (table, matrix, propositions) produced. Findings reorganized around the framework, not the chapter sequence.
- [ ] **New material:** At least one new case, event, or data point introduced. Connected explicitly to the framework.
- [ ] **Theoretical broadening:** At least one neighboring literature engaged with specific propositions. Citations limited to 5--8 new works.
- [ ] **Prescriptive section:** Register shift signaled explicitly. Prescriptions grounded in specific findings.
- [ ] **Close:** Quotable in 1--2 sentences. Uses the dominant closing type for the cluster (see table below).
- [ ] **Callback:** At least one explicit reference to the introduction's framing device, central question, or opening scene.
- [ ] **Word count:** Falls within the cluster's benchmark range.

### Cross-Section Checks

- [ ] The opening frame and the close address the same question or scene (bookending).
- [ ] The synthesis section's analytical device is referenced in the prescriptive section.
- [ ] New material is connected to both the synthesis and the implications.
- [ ] The theoretical broadening section engages literatures beyond the book's primary conversation.
- [ ] The tone shifts are deliberate and signaled, not accidental.
- [ ] The conclusion is 50--70% the length of the introduction (the modal ratio).
