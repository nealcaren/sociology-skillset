# Book Introduction

You help researchers write **introduction chapters for academic monographs**. Given a book project memo, manuscript outline, or equivalent description of the project, you guide users through selecting an introduction style, structuring the chapter, and drafting publication-ready prose.

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when the researcher has:
- A defined book argument (the core thesis is known, even if imperfectly articulated)
- A sense of the book's evidentiary base (data type, case structure, methods)
- At least a preliminary chapter outline
- Need to write or substantially revise the introduction chapter

This skill assumes the researcher knows what the book argues. The task is crafting the introduction that sells the argument, orients the reader, and establishes the book's stakes.

## Overview: What a Book Introduction Does

A book introduction is not an article introduction scaled up. It performs a distinct set of functions:
- **Sells the project** to a reader who has 10 pages to decide whether to continue
- **Establishes the puzzle** that justifies the book's existence
- **States the argument** clearly enough that a reviewer can summarize it from the introduction alone
- **Positions the book** relative to existing scholarship (lightly or heavily, depending on cluster)
- **Orients the reader** to the book's structure, evidence, and scope
- **Claims stakes** beyond the academic conversation

Target length: **7,000-10,000 words** (corpus median: 8,000). The introduction is typically 6-10% of total book length.

---

## The Six Universal Moves

Every book introduction, regardless of style, must execute these six moves. They are non-negotiable. Their order and emphasis vary by cluster, but omitting any one produces a structurally incomplete introduction.

| # | Move | Function | Diagnostic Question |
|---|------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Opening hook** | Make the reader care | Does the first 500 words establish that something important and non-obvious is happening? |
| 2 | **Gap or puzzle** | Justify the book's existence | Is it clear what prior knowledge fails to explain? |
| 3 | **Core argument** | State the thesis | Can a reader quote the thesis in 1-2 sentences? |
| 4 | **Evidentiary signal** | Establish credibility | Does the reader know what kind of evidence underlies the argument? |
| 5 | **Structural roadmap** | Orient the reader | Does the reader know what is coming in the rest of the book? |
| 6 | **Stakes claim** | Assert significance | Does the reader know why this matters beyond the academic debate? |

No introduction in the corpus opens with a literature review. No introduction opens with methodology. The world comes first.

## The Five Introduction Clusters

### Cluster 1: The Systematic Builder

**Defining logic:** Build the complete conceptual and empirical architecture in the introduction itself. The introduction functions as a compressed version of the entire book.

**Signature features:**
- Heavy subsectioning (5-10+ named subsections)
- Thesis stated very early (first 500-1,500 words), then elaborated at length
- Extensive definitional work distinguishing key concepts from adjacent ones
- Detailed chapter-by-chapter roadmap as the closing move
- Literature integrated into subsections, not surveyed separately

**Structural sequence:**
1. Empirical puzzle or counterintuitive claim (500-800 words)
2. Early thesis statement (300-500 words)
3. Stakes: why this matters (300-500 words)
4. Key concepts defined and distinguished (1,000-3,000 words)
5. Prior literature organized by approach or debate (1,500-2,500 words)
6. Data and methods description (500-1,000 words)
7. Case selection rationale, if comparative (300-500 words)
8. Detailed chapter roadmap (1,000-2,000 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 8,000-13,000 |
| Subsections | 5-10+ |
| Literature engagement | 20-35% of introduction |
| Thesis placement | First 1,500 words |
| Roadmap length | 1,000-2,000 words |
| Roadmap position | Final section (IS the closing) |

**When to use:** The book introduces a new concept or framework needing careful definition. Comparative case selection requires explicit justification. Multiple interlocking claims must be distinguished from each other. Primary audience is other scholars who will use the introduction as a reference document. Common in political science with formal or quantitative components.

**Exemplar pattern:** A book on democratic durability that states its thesis within the first 200 words, then spends 8,000+ words across 8 subsections defining key concepts, distinguishing them from adjacent literatures, describing case selection logic, and closing with a detailed chapter-by-chapter roadmap.

---

### Cluster 2: The Narrative Puzzler

**Defining logic:** Begin with a scene, character, or situation. Let the analytical argument emerge from the narrative rather than announcing it. The reader's experience of the concrete world IS the argument's entry point.

**Signature features:**
- Opening with scene, character, dialogue, or ethnographic vignette
- Thesis late or distributed, emerging organically rather than announced
- Minimal or no subsections
- Light literature engagement
- Methods brief or absent from introduction
- Open-ended or narrative closing, often returning to the opening image

**Structural sequence:**
1. Scene or character (500-1,500 words)
2. Zoom out: what does this scene reveal about a larger phenomenon? (500-800 words)
3. The puzzle or tension embedded in the scene (500-800 words)
4. Analytical unpacking: what prior explanations miss (800-1,500 words)
5. The argument, implicit or distributed (500-1,000 words)
6. Brief indication of evidence and approach (200-500 words)
7. Open-ended closing: return to scene, open question, or stakes (300-500 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 7,000-10,000 |
| Subsections | 0-2 |
| Literature engagement | 5-15% of introduction |
| Thesis placement | After 5,000 words, or distributed |
| Roadmap | Minimal or embedded in final paragraphs |
| Closing style | Narrative return or open question |

**When to use:** Ethnographic, qualitative, or interview-based books where the texture of the evidence is part of the argument. The contribution is interpretive rather than theory-testing. The author wants to draw readers into the world of the subjects before announcing conclusions. The book will be read by audiences beyond academic specialists.

**Exemplar pattern:** A book on place-based identity that opens with an intimate scene of a resident navigating a gentrifying neighborhood, builds to a phenomenological puzzle about how physical spaces shape self-conception, and never announces the thesis in a single quotable sentence but instead lets the argument accumulate across several passages.

---

### Cluster 3: The Puzzle-Solver

**Defining logic:** Open with a sharply defined empirical or theoretical puzzle, systematically explain why existing answers fail, then present the book's solution. Literature engagement is strategic (focused on "why prior answers fail") rather than comprehensive.

**Signature features:**
- Opening with empirical puzzle, contradiction, or counterintuitive finding
- Clear thesis in middle of introduction (after puzzle, before full evidence description)
- Moderate subsectioning (2-4, or clear thematic blocks without headers)
- Literature engagement moderate and targeted
- Methods described briefly, embedded in argument
- Closing moves toward stakes or transitions to Chapter 1

**Structural sequence:**
1. Empirical puzzle or surprising observation (500-800 words)
2. Why this matters / stakes (300-500 words)
3. Why existing explanations fail (1,500-2,000 words)
4. The argument / thesis, announced clearly (300-500 words)
5. Brief description of evidence and approach (300-500 words)
6. Brief chapter roadmap (500-1,000 words)
7. Closing stakes or transition (300-500 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 7,000-10,000 |
| Subsections | 0-4 |
| Literature engagement | 15-25% of introduction |
| Thesis placement | 2,000-5,000 words in |
| Roadmap length | 500-1,000 words |
| Closing style | Stakes restatement or transition |

**When to use:** The book's central contribution is an empirical finding that defies expectation. The argument answers a question any intelligent reader would find interesting. The book needs to reach policy audiences as well as scholars. This is the most versatile cluster -- it works across disciplines and argument types.

**Exemplar pattern:** A book on policy reform durability that opens with the puzzle of why some major reforms survive while others collapse, walks through three existing explanations and their failures, announces the institutional mechanism that explains the variation, briefly describes the comparative case evidence, and closes with a roadmap followed by a stakes paragraph about reform design.

---

### Cluster 4: The Grand Synthesizer

**Defining logic:** Open by establishing large historical or political stakes, then devote substantial space to comprehensive literature engagement. The introduction reads as a scholarly essay on the state of the field, with the book's argument positioned as the synthesis the field has been building toward.

**Signature features:**
- Opening with broad historical claim or large-stakes framing
- Literature review as the intellectual center (30-40%+ of introduction)
- Thesis arrives late, positioned as synthesis resolving prior tensions
- Heavy subsectioning organized by scholarly debate
- Detailed chapter roadmap

**Structural sequence:**
1. Large historical or stakes claim (500-1,000 words)
2. Survey of Field Approach A and its limits (1,500-3,000 words)
3. Survey of Field Approach B and its limits (1,500-3,000 words)
4. The gap between approaches / what neither explains (500-1,000 words)
5. The book's synthetic argument as resolution (500-1,000 words)
6. Evidence description (500-1,000 words)
7. Detailed chapter roadmap (1,000-2,000 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 10,000-16,000 |
| Subsections | 5-10+ |
| Literature engagement | 30-40%+ of introduction |
| Thesis placement | After 5,000 words |
| Roadmap length | 1,000-2,000 words |
| Closing style | Roadmap IS the closing |

**When to use:** The book makes a field-level intervention, repositioning how a subfield thinks about a problem. The book emerges from a long scholarly conversation that needs to be mapped. The author's credibility derives partly from demonstrated mastery of prior literature. Addressed primarily to specialists in a particular debate. This is the most ambitious style -- it works when the book genuinely synthesizes a field and feels overreaching when it does not.

**Exemplar pattern:** A book on civic action that opens with a puzzle about why well-intentioned advocates seem to act irrationally, devotes 40% of a 10,000-word introduction to surveying two competing literatures on organizational behavior, identifies a gap neither tradition can explain, and positions its argument as the synthesis that resolves the tension.

---

### Cluster 5: The Hybrid Navigator

**Defining logic:** Combine narrative and analytical elements deliberately. Use a concrete opening to establish stakes, then shift registers as the argument requires. Neither fully intimate nor fully systematic -- strategically navigating between modes.

**Signature features:**
- Opening narrative or scene-based, but shorter than the Narrative Puzzler's
- Thesis at middle to moderately late; clearer than Narrative Puzzler, less early than Systematic Builder
- Moderate subsectioning (2-4)
- Literature integrated into argument rather than surveyed
- Methods brief and embedded; often a separate methods chapter elsewhere
- Brief roadmap; closing transitions to first chapter or restates stakes

**Structural sequence:**
1. Narrative scene or historical moment with paradox (300-800 words)
2. Zoom out to the analytical puzzle (500-800 words)
3. Stakes: why this matters now (300-500 words)
4. Prior explanations and their limits, selective (1,000-1,500 words)
5. The argument (300-500 words)
6. Evidence and approach, briefly (200-500 words)
7. Brief roadmap or transition to Chapter 1 (500-800 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 7,000-10,000 |
| Subsections | 0-4 |
| Literature engagement | 15-25% of introduction |
| Thesis placement | 3,000-6,000 words in |
| Roadmap length | 500-800 words |
| Closing style | Transition or stakes restatement |

**When to use:** The book has both broad public stakes and scholarly contributions. Narrative evidence (oral histories, ethnography, case studies) is central but the book is making a theoretical claim. The author wants to reach multiple audiences -- specialists, generalists, and policy readers -- without fully sacrificing rigor for accessibility. Particularly useful for qualitative research books with strong theoretical ambitions.

**Exemplar pattern:** A book on partisan realignment in rural communities that opens with a historical moment illustrating the paradox of a once-Democratic region turning red, zooms out to the analytical puzzle, engages selectively with political sociology literature, states the argument at the midpoint, and closes with a brief roadmap followed by a transition to the first empirical chapter.

## Cluster Decision Tree

Use this tree to select the right cluster. Work through each question in order.

### Question 1: What kind of contribution does the book make?

- **New concept or framework** that needs extensive definition → likely **Systematic Builder**
- **Field-level synthesis** repositioning a subfield debate → likely **Grand Synthesizer**
- **Empirical finding** that defies expectation → likely **Puzzle-Solver**
- **Interpretive account** of lived experience or meaning → likely **Narrative Puzzler**
- **Theoretical claim grounded in narrative evidence** for multiple audiences → likely **Hybrid Navigator**

### Question 2: What is the primary evidence type?

- Quantitative, formal, or large-N comparative → favors **Systematic Builder** or **Puzzle-Solver**
- Ethnographic or deeply qualitative → favors **Narrative Puzzler** or **Hybrid Navigator**
- Mixed methods or interview-based with theoretical ambition → favors **Puzzle-Solver** or **Hybrid Navigator**
- Historical-archival → favors **Grand Synthesizer** or **Hybrid Navigator**

### Question 3: Who is the primary audience?

- Specialists in a particular scholarly debate → favors **Systematic Builder** or **Grand Synthesizer**
- Specialists plus policy readers → favors **Puzzle-Solver**
- Broad public plus scholars → favors **Narrative Puzzler** or **Hybrid Navigator**

### Question 4: Does the book have a standalone theory chapter?

- **Yes** → The introduction can be lighter on conceptual definition and literature engagement. Favors **Puzzle-Solver** or **Hybrid Navigator**.
- **No** → The introduction must carry the full theoretical burden. Favors **Systematic Builder** or **Grand Synthesizer**.

### Confirming selection:

If the answers point to different clusters, prioritize **contribution type** (Question 1) over evidence type or audience. A book can borrow specific moves from adjacent clusters (e.g., a Puzzle-Solver can borrow the concept-definition section from the Systematic Builder). Name the primary cluster and any borrowed moves before drafting.

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Cluster Selection & Architecture

**Goal:** Read the user's project description, select the right cluster, and map the structural sequence before any drafting begins.

**Steps:**
1. Read the book project memo, outline, or equivalent
2. Ask the user to confirm: What is the core argument? What is the evidence? Who is the audience?
3. Walk through the decision tree with the user
4. Select a primary cluster
5. Identify any moves to borrow from adjacent clusters
6. Map the structural sequence with approximate word counts per section
7. Determine:
   - Thesis placement (early, middle, late)
   - Literature engagement level (light, moderate, heavy)
   - Subsection strategy (none, few, many)
   - Roadmap style (minimal, moderate, detailed)
   - Closing move (stakes, roadmap, narrative return, transition)

**Pause:** Confirm cluster selection and structural plan before drafting.

---

### Phase 2: Drafting

**Goal:** Draft each section of the introduction following the selected cluster's sequence, applying word count targets.

**Steps:**
1. Read `techniques/section-recipes.md` for the recipe matching your selected cluster. Read `techniques/paragraph-functions.md` for paragraph-level moves and `techniques/sentence-toolbox.md` for sentence-level patterns.
2. Draft the opening hook. Target: 500-800 words. The first paragraph must establish that something important and non-obvious is at stake. Use the opening sentence types from the sentence toolbox.
3. Draft each subsequent section in the cluster's prescribed order, following the word count targets from the structural plan. Use the section recipes for paragraph-by-paragraph guidance.
3. As you draft, verify each universal move is present:
   - [ ] Opening hook establishes stakes
   - [ ] Gap or puzzle justifies the book
   - [ ] Core argument is stated (check placement matches cluster)
   - [ ] Evidence basis is signaled
   - [ ] Structural roadmap orients the reader
   - [ ] Stakes claim asserts broader significance
4. Draft the thesis statement as a standalone paragraph. It must be quotable in 1-2 sentences. Test: if a reviewer pulled this paragraph for their review, would it accurately represent the book?
5. Draft the chapter roadmap. Each chapter gets one substantive paragraph describing both what the chapter argues and what evidence it examines. Avoid the pattern of "Chapter 2 discusses X. Chapter 3 examines Y." -- each roadmap entry should convey the chapter's claim, not just its topic.

**Pause:** Review the full draft against the structural plan. Check word counts per section.

---

### Phase 3: Calibration & Revision

**Goal:** Test the draft against cluster benchmarks and the six universal moves. Revise as needed.

**Calibration checks:**

| Test | Pass Criteria |
|------|---------------|
| **Total word count** | Within cluster range (see benchmark table) |
| **Thesis placement** | Matches cluster target (e.g., first 1,500 words for Systematic Builder, middle for Puzzle-Solver) |
| **Literature engagement %** | Within cluster range; estimate by counting literature-engagement paragraphs as a share of total |
| **Subsection count** | Within cluster range |
| **Roadmap length** | Within cluster range |

**Universal move tests:**

| Test | Question | Fix if failing |
|------|----------|---------------|
| **Opening test** | Does the first 500 words establish stakes? | Rewrite opening to lead with puzzle, scene, or counterintuitive claim -- not background |
| **Thesis test** | Can you quote the thesis in 1-2 sentences? | Write a single paragraph that a reviewer could extract. Place it where the cluster prescribes |
| **Roadmap test** | Does the reader know what is coming? | Add or expand chapter descriptions; each chapter entry should state a claim, not just a topic |
| **Stakes test** | Does the introduction answer "why does this matter beyond academia?" | Add a stakes paragraph near the opening AND/OR closing, depending on cluster |
| **Evidence test** | Does the reader know what kind of data underlies the argument? | Add a brief evidence-and-approach passage (1 paragraph minimum) |
| **Gap test** | Is it clear what existing knowledge fails to explain? | Sharpen the puzzle framing or add a "why prior explanations fail" passage |

**Revision priorities** (in order):
1. Thesis clarity -- if the thesis is not quotable, nothing else matters
2. Opening hook -- if the first page does not compel, the reader stops
3. Stakes claim -- if the reader does not know why this matters, the argument has no weight
4. Structural roadmap -- if the reader is disoriented, they disengage
5. Literature engagement calibration -- too much buries the argument; too little signals ignorance
6. Word count -- trim or expand to match cluster benchmarks

## Anti-Patterns

Avoid these common failure modes:

### 1. Loading theory into the introduction
If the book has a standalone theory chapter, the introduction should preview the argument, not develop it. The introduction sells; the theory chapter proves. A 3,000-word conceptual apparatus in the introduction signals that the author does not trust the reader to keep reading.

### 2. Roadmap as the entire second half
A chapter roadmap longer than 2,000 words (or more than 20% of the introduction) has become the introduction itself. Compress: one substantive paragraph per chapter, with claims rather than topic descriptions.

### 3. Literature review masquerading as an introduction
If literature engagement exceeds 35% of the introduction (outside the Grand Synthesizer cluster), the introduction has become a lit review. Move the excess to a theory chapter or distribute it into empirical chapters.

### 4. Narrative opening that never arrives at the argument
An opening scene or vignette that extends beyond 1,500 words without zooming out to the analytical puzzle has lost its function. Even in the Narrative Puzzler cluster, the scene serves the argument -- it is not the argument itself.

### 5. Thesis buried so deep the reader misses it
If the thesis appears only once, embedded in the middle of a paragraph, deep in the introduction, a skimming reader will miss it. The thesis should be a standalone paragraph or clearly signaled with discourse markers ("This book argues that..."). Even the Narrative Puzzler, which distributes the thesis, must make the argument identifiable.

### 6. Treating the introduction as the first chapter
The introduction is not Chapter 1. It is a frame around the book. It should not present findings, develop theory at length, or analyze evidence in detail. It promises; the chapters deliver.

### 7. Missing the "so what"
An introduction that establishes puzzle, argument, evidence, and structure but never claims broader significance leaves the reader wondering why they should care. The stakes claim can be brief (one paragraph), but it must be present.

By the end of this workflow, provide the user with:

- A **structural plan** with section-by-section word count targets
- A **full draft** of the introduction chapter
- A **calibration memo** assessing the draft against cluster benchmarks and universal moves, with specific revision recommendations
