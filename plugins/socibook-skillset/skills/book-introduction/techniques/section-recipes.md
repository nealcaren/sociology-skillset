# Section-by-Section Drafting Recipes

Detailed instructions for drafting each section of a book introduction in political sociology and American politics. Each recipe specifies what the section accomplishes, its internal paragraph structure, and real examples from the 22-book corpus showing how published authors execute each move.

Use this file alongside `paragraph-functions.md` (for paragraph-level guidance) and `sentence-toolbox.md` (for within-paragraph craft). This file tells you how the paragraphs combine into sections, and how the sections combine into a complete introduction.

---

## Section 1: The Opening Hook

**Target length:** 500–800 words | **Paragraphs:** 2–4

### What This Section Accomplishes

The opening hook establishes the puzzle, contradiction, or stakes that the entire book addresses. It gives the reader a reason to care before they know anything about the argument, the literature, or the evidence. A successful hook makes the reader feel that a question urgently needs answering.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Puzzle-Solver Opening

Mucciaroni (2008) opens with the leanest puzzle statement in the corpus — a single declarative sentence:

> "Efforts to secure legal rights and protections for gays, lesbians, and bisexuals have met with mixed results."

"Mixed results" is the puzzle. The reader immediately asks: what determines which efforts succeed? The next sentences inventory the contradictions — wins on anti-discrimination, losses on marriage — making the "mixed" concrete. By the end of the first paragraph, the reader cannot avoid the "why" question.

Kadivar (2022) opens with temporal expectation then reversal:

> "Less than a decade ago, as protests swept through the Middle East, the world appeared to be on the verge of a wave of democratization."

The "appeared to be" signals that the expectation was defeated. The reader is waiting for "yet" — and it comes within a few sentences.

#### Narrative Puzzler Opening

Han et al. (2021) open with character-in-motion:

> "It was April 13, 2010. Jeff was at the airport heading home to Arizona when he heard the news."

The reader is inside a scene. They don't know who Jeff is yet, but they know something is about to happen. Han et al. sustain the scene for several paragraphs before zooming out to the analytical question: "This book is about the strategic choices that leaders like Jeff make."

Ternullo (2024) uses ethnographic present tense:

> "It's late on a summer afternoon in 2019, and I'm heading to a bar in Motorville City, Wisconsin, to meet Arthur, the former chair of Motorville's Democratic Party and a retired machinist."

The specificity of "former chair of Motorville's Democratic Party and a retired machinist" is doing work — it locates Arthur at the intersection of party and class that the book will analyze.

#### Reported Speech Opening

Grumbach (2023) opens with a quotation from a political actor:

> "'I guarantee you we can draw four Republican congressional maps,' Republican Kansas State Senate leader Susan Wagle told donors at a closed-door fundraiser in 2020."

The quote reveals the mechanism (partisan redistricting as strategic action) in the actor's own words. It is more damning than any paraphrase.

#### Historical Scene Opening

Patashnik (2008) opens with a ceremonial moment:

> "On October 22, 1986, lawmakers from both parties gathered on the South Lawn of the White House and applauded as President Ronald Reagan signed into law the most comprehensive revision of the federal tax code in a half century."

The celebration is the setup. The book's argument is that these reforms were subsequently undermined — the signing ceremony becomes ironic in retrospect.

### After the Opening: The Stakes Move

Whatever opening type you choose, the second or third paragraph must escalate stakes. The most common pattern:

**Concede → Complicate → Escalate.**

Mucciaroni (2008) concedes social progress, then re-centers on formal politics:

> "These changes are striking and have improved the political climate for gay rights, but they are no substitute for the recognition, rights, and benefits that the gay rights movement can secure only through the public policy process."

SoRelle (2020) escalates from individual harm to systemic risk through a concrete comparison:

> "The absence of safeguards on consumer bank accounts and loans like those we enjoy on goods from children's pajamas to toaster ovens is particularly dangerous because the consequences of bad financial products extend well beyond harms to individuals; they threaten the stability of entire economies."

Patashnik (2008) uses a numbered-consequences structure:

> "When the commitment to a general-interest reform unravels, three bad things happen. First, the substantive achievements of the reform are lost. . . . Second, the undoing of a reform undermines the stability that people need to plan for the future. . . . Finally, the unraveling of reforms causes ordinary Americans to lose faith in the ability of public leaders to solve public problems."

### Opening Sentence Rule (All Clusters)

The first sentence of the introduction must be about the world — about events, people, institutions, contradictions, or patterns — never about scholarship, the book itself, or the author's intentions. All 22 introductions in the corpus follow this rule. Even Lichterman (2021), the most analytical opener in the corpus, starts with social advocates, not with scholars:

> "Social advocates turn conditions into social problems. They craft compelling claims about the problems, and build campaigns to solve them."

## Section 2: Why Existing Explanations Fail

**Target length:** 1,500–2,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 3–5

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section does the work of a literature review but is organized as an argument, not as a survey. Its purpose is to demonstrate that existing explanations — however valuable — cannot fully account for the puzzle. Each paragraph engages a body of work, concedes its contributions, and identifies what it leaves unexplained. The section ends by naming the specific gap the book fills.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### The Catalogue-then-Pivot (SoRelle)

SoRelle (2020) devotes a full paragraph to cataloguing what scholars have done:

> "Over the last two decades, scholars have paid increasing attention to the cultural and economic trends associated with growing consumer credit usage in the United States. They have traced the evolution of American attitudes toward borrowing. . . . Scholars have also shown that credit is an essential component of the modern U.S. economy. . . . And in the absence of a more generous 'traditional' welfare state, access to consumer credit serves as an essential form of financial support for many Americans."

Then pivots in the next paragraph:

> "Beyond the established cultural and economic implications of consumer credit, however, this book is motivated by an underlying contention that the expansion . . . is fundamentally a political issue as well. And while some of the political causes of growing consumer credit markets in the United States have been explored by scholars . . . very little research attends to the explicitly political consequences."

Two paragraphs, one structural move: here is what they have done → here is what they have not done.

#### The Strand Mapper (Patashnik 2023)

Patashnik (2023) maps the literature into distinct strands and then positions himself:

> "The literature on backlash politics contains two distinct strands. The first analyzes backlash as an intrinsically conservative (reactionary) response. . . . The second body of research adopts a more inclusive, neutral definition of backlash. . . . My analysis builds primarily on the latter strand."

He then evaluates the leading scholar within the chosen strand — crediting her while identifying the limit:

> "Weaver persuasively demonstrates the coalitional linkages between contestation over civil rights and crime policy. Her analysis places less emphasis, however, on the agency of ordinary citizens."

"Persuasively demonstrates . . . places less emphasis, however" — this is the concede-distinguish move at its cleanest.

#### The Multi-Foil (Patashnik 2008)

Patashnik (2008) names eight scholars, concedes their contributions, then differentiates from each in sequence:

> "My analysis differs in subtle but important ways from these prior studies. In providing a detailed examination of how the rules, incentives, and norms embedded in reforms channel and constrain political dynamics over extended periods of time, my analysis departs from Maltzman and Shipan's important quantitative study. . . . An emphasis on how sustainable reforms . . . disempower and divide rent-seeking clientele groups distinguishes this account from the compelling positive feedback studies of Campbell and Mettler."

Each differentiation names what is different and why. The phrases "departs from," "distinguishes this account from," "diverges from," and "In contrast to" create a rhythmic sequence of positioning moves.

#### The Critical Redirection (Grumbach)

Grumbach (2023) engages critical federalism scholarship, then identifies a gap in its reception:

> "More recently, historical institutionalist scholars in political science have engaged in critical studies of federalism. In *Fragmented Democracy* (2018), Jamila Michener uses the case of Medicaid administration to investigate how federalism creates inequality. . . . Lisa Miller's *The Perils of Federalism* (2008) points to the potential for a greater decentralization and numerosity of political venues to disincentivize ordinary people's political participation. . . . Rob Mickey's *Paths Out of Dixie* (2015) investigates the 'authoritarian enclaves' of the Jim Crow South."

Then:

> "These critical federalism studies, however, have remained mostly outside of the political science mainstream."

Three books named with precise characterizations, then a single sentence that turns them into a gap.

### The Gap Statement

The section ends by naming the hole explicitly. The best gap statements in the corpus:

> "Despite their substantive and theoretical importance, these questions have received only limited attention in the American politics literature. Most political scientists who study the politics of policy reform in the United States have focused on the logically prior question of how major general-interest reforms come to be enacted in the first place."
> — Patashnik (2008)

> "Remarkably, we know little about it. . . . The answer, I suspect, is that programmatic partisanship has become entangled with the notion of polarization in the American politics literature such that it appears to have been addressed by work on that subject. But, it has not; rather, it has been hiding in plain sight."
> — Krimmel (2024)

> "Each of these puzzles . . . is of significant consequence for American political economy, yet scholars have largely ignored them."
> — SoRelle (2020)

The gap must be exactly the shape of the argument you are about to offer. If the gap is "no one has studied X through Y," then the thesis must announce a study of X through Y.

### Common Mistakes

- **Surveying by author rather than by approach.** Each paragraph should represent an intellectual tradition, not a scholar's career.
- **Equal time regardless of relevance.** The closest competitor deserves the most detail. Tangential literatures get a subordinate clause.
- **Failing to connect back to the puzzle.** Every foil paragraph must return to the specific puzzle from Section 1.
- **Letting the section grow too long.** In a Puzzle-Solver introduction, literature engagement should be 15–25% of total word count. If it exceeds 2,000 words, you are surveying rather than arguing.

## Section 3: The Argument

**Target length:** 300–500 words | **Paragraphs:** 1–2

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section states the book's thesis — the core claim and its causal mechanism — in language clear enough that a reviewer could quote it to accurately represent the book. Brevity is a feature. The thesis section is the shortest named section of the introduction and the most carefully written.

### How Published Authors Do It

**The numbered-consequences thesis (Grumbach):**

> "I argue, in brief, that the nationalization of the Democratic and Republican parties — the increased national coordination among activists, groups, and candidates in each party coalition — has produced three consequences: a resurgence of state governments as the center of American policymaking, reduced policy learning between states controlled by opposing parties, and democratic backsliding in states controlled by the Republican Party."

One sentence. Signal phrase, parenthetical definition, three numbered consequences, full scope. This is the model for a theory-driven thesis.

**The "because" thesis (Kadivar):**

> "I argue that democracies that emerge from long periods of protest mobilization have a better chance of survival and improvement in democratic quality than democracies that emerge out of quiescence or short episodes of protest mobilization. This is because, when movements mobilize over a long period, they are required to build an organizational infrastructure that provides a firmer foundation for democratic institutions."

Two sentences — claim then mechanism. The "This is because" is explicit and unambiguous.

**The "instead" thesis (Bolton and Thrower):**

> "In this book, we argue ambition is not enough to prevent the aggrandizement of executive power, even in a system where shared responsibilities impel institutional rivalries. Instead, legislatures must possess the political will and the institutional capacity to assert their authority and effectively constrain the executive branch."

The "Instead" creates a two-part structure: what is insufficient → what is necessary.

**The multi-layered thesis (SoRelle):**

> "I argue that the politics of U.S. consumer financial protection, from bureaucratic decision making to mass political behavior, are shaped by the historical development of consumer lending policies and the political reverberations, or feedback effects, those policies produced. . . . I argue that, in doing so, they unleashed a path-dependent process that would dramatically limit the political incentives for protecting consumers' finances in the future."

Two "I argue" statements, each adding a layer — first the general claim, then the historical mechanism.

### Common Mistakes

- **Stating a topic instead of a claim.** "This book examines the role of institutions" is a topic. Bolton and Thrower's "ambition is not enough" is a claim — it can be wrong.
- **Missing the "because" clause.** A thesis without a mechanism is an observation. Kadivar's "This is because" shows how to make the mechanism explicit.
- **Burying the thesis.** The thesis should appear in its own short section, not inside a long paragraph about something else.
- **Hedging.** This is the one place where full assertiveness is warranted. No author in the corpus writes "This book suggests that it may be the case that..." They write "I argue."

## Section 4: Concept Definition

**Target length:** 500–1,500 words (when present) | **Paragraphs:** 1–3 | **Presence:** Required for Systematic Builder, common when a new term is introduced, absent in many Narrative Puzzler introductions.

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section defines and distinguishes any concept central to the argument that is either new or used in a distinctive way. It earns its place only if the reader cannot fully understand the thesis without it.

### How Published Authors Do It

**The direct definition (Patashnik 2008):**

> "By general-interest reform, I mean a non-incremental change of an existing line of policymaking intended to rationalize governmental undertakings or to distribute benefits to some broad constituency."

Clean, quotable, one sentence. "By X, I mean" is the standard formulation.

**The continuum definition (Krimmel):**

> "The core principle of programmatic partisanship is an emphasis on policy positions as a basis of competition. Given the complexity of real world party systems, this dynamic is rarely if ever fully dominant or absent, but exists on a continuum."

This avoids the trap of treating a variable as a binary.

**The term-plus-mechanism definition (Ternullo):**

> "I use the term place-based partisanship to refer to the portion of one's partisan identity that is explained by where they live. I argue that place-based partisanship emerges as residents make sense of local structural conditions and national politics from within their organizational contexts, leading them to cohere around two cultural frameworks: (1) diagnostic frames that they use to define their social problems and identify political solutions; and (2) narratives of community identity."

The definition is immediately followed by the mechanism that produces it — and the mechanism has numbered components.

**The scope-encompassing definition (Grumbach):**

> "By nationalized parties, I mean political parties in which aligned groups, activists, candidates, and incumbents — in all offices at all levels of government — share similar policy agendas and see themselves engaged in broader political conflict with the other national party."

The em-dash aside ("in all offices at all levels of government") specifies the scope of the concept, preventing the reader from limiting it to Congress.

**The descriptive definition (Lichterman):**

> "When people work together, voluntarily, to address problems they think should matter to others, they are engaging in civic action. There are different ways to do civic action. Civic action may or may not be contentious."

No "By X, I mean" — the definition is conversational, matching the Narrative Puzzler register.

### Common Mistakes

- **Defining without distinguishing.** A definition alone invites the objection "Isn't this just [existing concept]?" Every definition needs at least one "this is distinct from" move.
- **Spending more time on the concept than on the evidence.** If the concept section is longer than the thesis and evidence sections combined, the proportions are wrong.
- **Defining standard terms.** Do not define "social movement" or "path dependence" unless you are using them in a genuinely nonstandard way.

## Section 5: Evidence and Approach

**Target length:** 300–500 words | **Paragraphs:** 1–2

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section establishes that the argument rests on empirical ground. It is deliberately brief — a signal of the data's existence and its value, not a full methodological discussion.

### How Published Authors Do It

**The compressed multi-method signal (SoRelle):**

> "In *Democracy Declined* I draw on multiple sources of data — from historical records to survey experiments — to demonstrate the development and ongoing dynamics of the U.S. political economy of credit."

One sentence. Type + range + purpose.

**The detailed qualitative signal (Ternullo):**

> "Over the course of eighteen months, from May 2019 through the November 2020 presidential election, I interviewed just over 175 residents and community leaders across Motorville, Lutherton, and Gravesend. Before the onset of the Covid-19 pandemic, I conducted nearly six months of fieldwork and interviewed more than 90 'community leaders'. . . . But the book's core arguments come from four waves of interviews with 86 White 'residents.'"

Duration, N, respondent types, and wave structure — all in one paragraph. The distinction between "community leaders" and "residents" signals analytical intent.

**The ethnographic signal (Lichterman):**

> "I spent four years observing and participating alongside social advocates working on housing problems in Los Angeles. My observations gave me close-up views of four campaigns, three coalitions — two in depth — and twelve organizations. . . . To contextualize the ethnographic findings, the study draws in evidence from hundreds of documents, and dozens of hours of audio- and videotaped city hall deliberation."

Duration + scope + supplementary data. The specificity ("four campaigns, three coalitions — two in depth — and twelve organizations") creates trust.

**The case-justification move (Han et al.):**

> "To understand the strategic logic of powerful collective action, we examine outliers. Specifically, we bring an analytic lens to six case studies of improbable collective action."

"Outliers" and "improbable" justify the case selection logic in two words.

### Common Mistakes

- **Turning this into a methods chapter.** If the section exceeds 500 words, move the detail.
- **Describing methods before the thesis.** Evidence should appear after the claim it supports.
- **Listing data types without explaining their value.** "I conducted 85 interviews" is less useful than "I conducted 85 interviews with organizers and elected officials, providing direct access to the deliberative processes that archival records do not capture."

## Section 6: Chapter Roadmap

**Target length:** 500–1,000 words | **Paragraphs:** One per chapter (typically 6–10 short paragraphs)

### What This Section Accomplishes

The roadmap previews the book's structure so that the reader understands how the argument unfolds across chapters. Each entry must contain a claim, not merely a topic.

### How Published Authors Do It

**Opening transition:**

> "The remainder of this chapter more fully advances the argument of the book and previews how that argument unfolds in the remaining chapters."
> — SoRelle (2020)

**Theory chapter entry:**

> "Chapter 2 examines general-interest reform as a political project. After providing a more complete definition of reform, the chapter summarizes what political scientists and economists have learned about the politics of reform adoption. It then identifies both the generic threats to reform sustainability and the structures and processes that can render the reforms durable over the long haul."
> — Patashnik (2008)

**Case-cluster entry:**

> "Chapters 3 thru 8, the empirical heart of the book, examine seven major domestic policy reforms in American national government: tax reform, agriculture reform, two attempts to recast the American public/private welfare state (ERISA and Medicare Catastrophic Insurance Coverage Act), procurement reform, airline deregulation, and the cap and trade program to curb acid rain emissions."
> — Patashnik (2008)

**Individual case entry with claim:**

> "Chapter 6 shows how residents of Motorville, Lutherton, and Gravesend continued to deploy existing diagnostic frames to make sense of the federal government's role in their lives amid the economic fallout of the Covid-19 pandemic, because the crisis did not undermine local organizational contexts."
> — Ternullo (2024)

**Conclusion entry:**

> "Chapter 9 generalizes about the politics of reform sustainability. It summarizes common themes from the cases, teases out broader lessons for the study of public policymaking, and offers some practical suggestions for promoting reform sustainability."
> — Patashnik (2008)

### Syntax Variation

Do not begin every entry with "Chapter N." Vary the openings:

- "Chapter 3 traces..." (Patashnik 2008)
- "In Chapter 5, I examine..."
- "Chapter 6 shows how..." (Ternullo 2024)
- "The analysis shifts in Chapter 6 to..."

### Sequence Logic

If chapters build on each other, signal it: "Having established [X] in Chapter 3, Chapter 4 asks whether [Y]..."

If chapters offer parallel cases, signal the parallel: "Whereas Chapter 3 examines a case of [outcome A], Chapter 4 considers a case where [outcome B] prevailed."

### Common Mistakes

- **Topics without claims.** "Chapter 2 discusses the historical background" tells the reader nothing. "Chapter 2 traces the institutional origins of the policy regime, showing that the organizational choices made in the founding period constrained all subsequent reform efforts" tells them what to expect.
- **Excessive length.** Each entry should be 3–5 sentences, not 8–10.
- **Monotonous syntax.** "Chapter 2 examines... Chapter 3 examines... Chapter 4 examines..." — vary the verb and the sentence structure.
- **Omitting the conclusion.** The conclusion chapter deserves a roadmap entry too.

## Section 7: Closing Stakes

**Target length:** 300–500 words | **Paragraphs:** 1–2

### What This Section Accomplishes

The closing returns to the broadest significance of the book. It answers "so what?" at maximum scale and leaves the reader with a sense of the argument's importance.

### How Published Authors Do It

**The metaphor inversion (Grumbach):**

> "And contrary to the hopes of Louis Brandeis, state governments may not be 'laboratories of democracy' but laboratories against democracy — innovating new ways to restrict the franchise, gerrymander districts, exploit campaign finance loopholes, and circumvent civil rights in the criminal justice system."

Takes a founding metaphor and reverses it. Memorable, quotable, carries the book's thesis in a single sentence.

**The "central purpose" close (Patashnik 2008):**

> "In sum, general-interest reforms have served as the basis for broad generalizations about the fundamental character of policymaking in the American polity. What the recent literature on American national government has largely neglected to investigate, however, is what happens after the reforms are signed into law. That is the central purpose of this book."

"That is the central purpose of this book" is the simplest possible closing — and it works because the gap has been so precisely established.

**The intellectual tradition close (Ternullo):**

> "In sum, this book's most ambitious goal is to revive and extend a contextual account of how partisan attachments form and endure."

Positions the book within a long scholarly lineage while naming its ambition.

**The two-pathways close (SoRelle):**

> "I do, however, identify two potential pathways to mitigate these potentially disastrous consequences. First, I consider several alternative approaches advocacy groups might use. . . . Second, I argue that the creation of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) has the potential to restructure the regulatory arena."

Closes on an actionable note — not just significance, but what can be done.

**The democratic stakes close (Grumbach, alternative passage):**

> "Indeed, contrary to the hopes of James Madison, a large federal republic may not help contain factions but empower them."

A single sentence that inverts a Federalist Papers argument. This is the aphoristic close at its most compressed.

### Common Mistakes

- **Ending on the roadmap.** The last section must be about significance, not about chapter structure.
- **Introducing new information.** The closing synthesizes and elevates; it does not introduce new evidence.
- **Excessive hedging.** The closing is where the author is most assertive. SoRelle's "I do, however, identify two potential pathways" is conditional but forward-looking — not tentative.
- **Restating the thesis verbatim.** Echo the argument's implications, not its exact phrasing.

## Section Sequencing and Proportions

The seven sections above follow the most common sequence. Variations exist:

| Cluster | Typical Sequence | Longest Section | Shortest Section |
|---|---|---|---|
| Puzzle-Solver | Hook → Literature → Thesis → Evidence → Roadmap → Closing | Literature (20–25%) | Thesis (5–8%) |
| Narrative Puzzler | Hook → Literature → Thesis → Evidence → Roadmap → Closing | Hook (20–25%) | Evidence (3–5%) |
| Systematic Builder | Hook → Literature → Thesis → Concept → Evidence → Roadmap → Closing | Concept (15–20%) | Hook (8–10%) |

**Benchmarks from the corpus:**

Total introduction length ranges from 6,000 to 16,000 words, with a median near **8,000 words**. The interquartile range is approximately **7,000–10,000 words**. Shorter introductions (under 6,000 words) are rare and found mainly in Narrative Puzzler introductions (Fisher at ~1,850 words is the extreme outlier). Longer introductions (over 12,000 words) are characteristic of Grand Synthesizer and Systematic Builder clusters.

## Quick-Reference Drafting Checklist

- [ ] **Opening hook:** First sentence is about the world. Puzzle or stakes established within 500 words.
- [ ] **Existing explanations:** Organized by approach, not by author. Every paragraph connects back to the puzzle. Section is 15–25% of total length.
- [ ] **Thesis:** Quotable in 1–2 sentences. Contains a "because" clause. Uses "I argue that..." signal phrase.
- [ ] **Concept definition (if needed):** Key term defined AND distinguished from at least one adjacent concept.
- [ ] **Evidence and approach:** Data type, scope, and unique contribution in 1–2 paragraphs. Points to methods chapter. Appears after the thesis.
- [ ] **Chapter roadmap:** Each entry contains a claim. Entries reveal sequence logic. Syntax varies.
- [ ] **Closing stakes:** Final paragraph is about the world and broader significance. No new information. Assertive tone.

### Cross-Section Checks

- [ ] The puzzle in Section 1 is the same puzzle the thesis in Section 3 answers.
- [ ] The gap named in Section 2 is exactly the shape of the argument in Section 3.
- [ ] The evidence described in Section 5 is suited to testing the mechanism in Section 3.
- [ ] The roadmap in Section 6 shows how each chapter advances the argument in Section 3.
- [ ] The closing in Section 7 returns to the stakes established in Section 1.
- [ ] The introduction falls between 6,000 and 10,000 words (with most falling near 8,000).

## Revision Protocol

After drafting, revise each section in this order:

1. **Thesis first.** Rewrite Section 3 until the argument is quotable in two sentences. Everything else depends on this.
2. **Hook second.** Revise Section 1 to ensure the puzzle it poses is the puzzle the thesis answers.
3. **Literature third.** Revise Section 2 to ensure every foil paragraph ends by connecting back to the puzzle.
4. **Gap check.** Read the last sentence of Section 2 and the first sentence of Section 3 in sequence. The gap should flow directly into the thesis with no logical jump.
5. **Evidence and roadmap.** Revise Sections 5 and 6 for proportion. Together they should not exceed 35% of the introduction.
6. **Closing last.** Revise Section 7 to ensure it echoes the opening without repeating it.

At each stage, read the section aloud. If any sentence requires rereading to parse, rewrite it.
