# Anti-Pattern Reference for Chapter Evaluation

A consolidated index of anti-patterns from all eight writing skills. Use this during the diagnostic to quickly check whether a chapter draft exhibits known problems.

---

## Universal Anti-Patterns (All Chapter Types)

### 1. The chapter that forgets it is part of a book
If the chapter could stand alone as an independent essay with no reference to the comparative framework, it has failed its function as a book chapter. Every chapter must connect to the theory and to the other chapters in the set.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, anti-pattern 1

### 2. The verdict that does not map onto the framework
If the verdict uses language disconnected from the book's theoretical variables, the reader cannot integrate the finding into the book's argument. The verdict must use the framework's own vocabulary.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, anti-pattern 4

### 3. Evidence without interpretation
Every evidence paragraph must contain an interpretive sentence connecting the data to the argument. Data dumps -- paragraphs of facts without analysis -- slow the reader and obscure the argument.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, evidence body

### 4. Orphaned quotes
Quotes that stand alone as paragraphs without analytical framing before and interpretation after. Every quote must be framed and followed up.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, paragraph-functions, QUOTE_INTEGRATE

---

## Opening Anti-Patterns

### 5. Opening with literature
No chapter type in the corpus opens with a literature review. The world comes first -- a case, a puzzle, a scene, a problem.
**Source:** book-introduction, book-parallel-case, book-cross-issue

### 6. Opening with methodology
No chapter type opens with methodology. Methods information belongs in a dedicated subsection or embedded in the evidence body.
**Source:** book-introduction, book-parallel-case

### 7. Burying the case
Three or more paragraphs of historical background before the reader knows which case, city, policy, or country is being discussed. Name the case in sentence one or two.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, anti-pattern 5

### 8. Burying the claim
The reader should be able to state the chapter's argument by the end of paragraph 2-3 (empirical chapters) or the first section (introductions). If the claim appears only on page 8, the opening has failed.
**Source:** All chapter-type skills, universal move 1

## Bridge Anti-Patterns

### 9. The bridge that is actually a conclusion
A bridge that re-argues the chapter's findings instead of pointing forward. Bridges should create demand for the next chapter, not restate the current one.
**Source:** book-cross-issue, anti-pattern 6

### 10. New evidence in the bridge
The bridge synthesizes and points forward; it does not introduce new findings, new quotes, or new data.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, Section 5

### 11. Formulaic bridges
"The next chapter will examine..." repeated identically across every chapter. Vary bridge structure: challenge to universality, open question, theoretical preview.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, Section 5

### 12. Missing bridge for first or middle cases
Last cases can close on the verdict, but first and middle cases that end abruptly leave the reader without guidance about the comparative argument's trajectory.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, bridge conventions table

## Evidence Body Anti-Patterns

### 13. Organizing purely by chronology
Chronology is useful within subsections, but a chapter organized as a pure timeline (1960-1970-1980-1990) has no analytical structure. Use thematic subsections with chronological threads inside them.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, Section 3

### 14. Subsections without analytical purpose
A subsection titled "The 1990s" tells the reader nothing about the argument. "The Failure of Legislative Repeal" tells the reader what the section argues. Subsection headings should signal arguments.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, Section 3

### 15. Uneven subsection lengths
If one subsection is 3,000 words and another is 300, the proportions signal underdevelopment. Aim for rough parity (800-2,000 words per subsection).
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, Section 3

### 16. Overly long background sections
If the historical background exceeds 25% of the chapter, it has become the chapter. Background serves the analysis; it is not the analysis.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, anti-pattern 7

## Verdict Anti-Patterns

### 17. Distributed verdict
The finding is scattered across five paragraphs with no single summary. The verdict must be concentrated enough to quote in 1-2 sentences.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, Section 4

### 18. Descriptive verdict
"This case shows that tax reform was eroded" is a description. "The reform failed to durably reconfigure the political dynamic" is an analytical verdict that names *why*, using the framework's concepts.
**Source:** book-parallel-case, section-recipes, Section 4

---

## Cross-Issue Specific Anti-Patterns

### 19. Four mini-case chapters disguised as one
If each case subsection reads as a self-contained mini-essay with no cross-case reference until the conclusion, the chapter has failed its cross-issue function.
**Source:** book-cross-issue, anti-pattern 1

### 20. Generalizing without evidence
Stating patterns ("across all four cases, X operated through Y") without providing specific evidence from each case. Name specific organizations, leaders, and data from each case in every generalization.
**Source:** book-cross-issue, anti-pattern 2

### 21. Summary instead of synthesis
A synthesis section that says "In Case A, X. In Case B, Y. In Case C, Z" is a summary. Synthesis identifies the pattern connecting the cases.
**Source:** book-cross-issue, anti-pattern 3

### 22. Evidence imbalance across cases
One case gets 3,000 words and another gets 500. Cross-issue chapters require roughly equal treatment across cases (except Template D, which intentionally varies).
**Source:** book-cross-issue, anti-pattern 4

---

## Conclusion Specific Anti-Patterns

### 23. The conclusion that is only a summary
Every conclusion in the 22-book corpus introduces new material. A conclusion that merely summarizes the empirical chapters without new evidence, new scope, or new normative claims is incomplete.
**Source:** book-conclusion, universal move 3 (new material)

### 24. Missing callback to the introduction
Every conclusion in the corpus calls back to the introduction -- revisiting the opening image, puzzle, or framing. Missing this creates structural incompleteness.
**Source:** book-conclusion, universal move 2 (callback)

---

## Introduction Specific Anti-Patterns

### 25. The introduction that does not sell
If the first 500 words do not establish that something important and non-obvious is happening, the introduction fails its primary function. The opening hook must make the reader care.
**Source:** book-introduction, universal move 1

### 26. Missing roadmap
Every introduction in the corpus includes a structural roadmap previewing the book's chapters. This is non-negotiable.
**Source:** book-introduction, universal move 5
