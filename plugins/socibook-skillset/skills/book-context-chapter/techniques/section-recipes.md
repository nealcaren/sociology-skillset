# Section-by-Section Drafting Recipes for Context Chapters

Detailed instructions for drafting each section of a context chapter in political science and American politics monographs. Context chapters do the work that empirical chapters cannot do alone: they establish the historical baseline, introduce the book's key variable in motion, and prepare the reader for the causal claims that follow.

Each recipe specifies what the section accomplishes, its internal paragraph structure, word count targets, the paragraph functions deployed (cross-referenced to `paragraph-functions.md`), and a worked example from the five-book corpus.

---

## Section 1: Opening Frame

**Target length:** 400-800 words | **Paragraphs:** 2-4

### What This Section Accomplishes

The opening frame establishes what the context chapter will do and why it matters. Unlike introduction chapters, which open with scenes and puzzles, context chapters open by locating the reader in a historical period or institutional arrangement that must be understood before the empirical chapters can proceed. The opening frame answers two questions: What was the world like before the change this book studies? And why does understanding that prior world matter for the argument?

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

BASELINE_ESTABLISH, CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, and occasionally EVIDENCE_PRESENT.

### Opening Types in the Corpus

#### Type 1: Epigraph-Plus-Rebuttal Opening

Grumbach (2023) opens Chapter 2 with two epigraphs -- one from a public commentator, one from a Supreme Court opinion -- that state the received wisdom about federalism. The first substantive paragraph then immediately destabilizes that wisdom:

> "Thinkers in the age of Trump are increasingly critical of the U.S. Constitution -- for good reason. The institutional rules of the game, from Senate apportionment to the Electoral College to presidential privilege, are clashing with the realities of partisanship and geography."

This opening works because the epigraphs do the labor of stating the conventional wisdom, freeing the author to begin with critique. The reader enters the chapter already sensing that a dominant framework will be challenged.

#### Type 2: Landmark-Legislation Opening

SoRelle (2020) opens Chapter 2 with the passage of the Truth in Lending Act, establishing a specific policy moment as the baseline:

> "After nearly a decade of failed attempts, on May 22, 1968, Congress finally enacted the Consumer Credit Protection Act (P.L. 90-321). President Lyndon Johnson signed it into law one week later. More commonly referred to by the name of its first provision, the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), this law marked the first major federal regulation of the growing consumer credit industry in the United States."

This opening works because it anchors the chapter in a concrete policy moment that the subsequent narrative will both explain (how did we get here?) and extend (what happened after?). The date and public law number signal that this is an institutional history, not a thematic essay.

#### Type 3: Archival-Dialogue Opening

Pettinicchio (2019) opens Chapter 2 with a verbatim exchange from a 1950 Senate hearing:

> "SENATOR AIKEN: What is your idea of rehabilitation, Mr. Secretary? SECRETARY TOBIN: Well, my idea of rehabilitation is, to begin with, to do everything that is possible in a medical and surgical way to restore persons to good, sound health, and in the case of physical impairment, to restore them to a physical condition where they can perform some gainful employment."

This opening works because the archival dialogue lets the reader hear the prior era's assumptions in the actors' own words. The mid-century understanding of disability as a problem of individual adaptation is revealed through dialogue rather than declared by the author.

#### Type 4: Contemporary-Problem Opening

Bolton and Thrower (2022) open Chapter 3 with a 2019 letter from former legislators urging greater congressional capacity, then pivot to the historical question:

> "In the above-quoted letter, ten former legislators and other prominent policy reformers urge the Appropriations Committees in both chambers to augment congressional capacity, citing its centrality for legislative power. Indeed, contemporary political observers frequently attribute perceptions of encroaching executive power to deficits in congressional resources."

This opening works because it begins with a present-day problem that motivates the historical investigation. The reader understands immediately why the history matters: because the same debate is happening now.

#### Type 5: Theoretical-Puzzle Opening

Krimmel (2024) opens the substantive portion of Chapter 3 by identifying a theoretical gap:

> "The U.S. case underscores both the value and the limitations of existing theory on modes of appealing to voters. We can begin with modernization. Economic growth, population growth, and urbanization trends are all generally consistent with the notion of programmaticism increasing over time, as it has, and sitting at a historic high today, which it does."

This opening works because it begins with an existing theory's partial success and immediately signals that more is needed. The reader enters the chapter expecting theoretical development, not just historical narrative.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Preferred Opening Type | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Institutional Mythos Critique (Grumbach) | Epigraph-Plus-Rebuttal | Opens by voicing the target of critique |
| Variable-in-History (Bolton/Thrower) | Contemporary-Problem | Opens with a present-day manifestation of the historical variable |
| Policy-Regime Genesis (SoRelle) | Landmark-Legislation | Opens with the policy that the chapter will explain and historicize |
| Policy-Monopoly Archaeology (Pettinicchio) | Archival-Dialogue | Opens with primary sources that let the prior era speak |
| Theoretical-Historical Hybrid (Krimmel) | Theoretical-Puzzle | Opens with a theoretical gap that the chapter will fill |

## Section 2: Historical Narrative

**Target length:** 2,500-5,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 10-20

### What This Section Accomplishes

The historical narrative is the longest section of most context chapters. It tells the story of how an institutional arrangement, policy regime, or political variable developed over time. This section provides the evidentiary foundation for the book's causal claims by showing the reader the sequence of events, the actors involved, and the mechanisms at work.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

TEMPORAL_TRANSITION, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, and BASELINE_ESTABLISH (for nested baselines within the narrative).

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Worked Example: Bolton and Thrower, "An Era of Federal Government Expansion"

Bolton and Thrower structure their historical narrative as a chronological march through eras of federal expansion, with each era marked by a TEMPORAL_TRANSITION paragraph and populated by INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE and EVIDENCE_PRESENT paragraphs.

The section opens with a BASELINE_ESTABLISH paragraph about the limited early federal government:

> "The nascent federal government was fairly limited in size and scope and was largely subsumed by state and local governments responsible for regulating most policy areas during much of the nineteenth century."

Then transitions to the Progressive Era:

> "Following the Civil War, a broader administrative state began to emerge. New federal programs administered benefits to Civil War veterans and their families. Economic and social changes in the ensuing decades spurred further demands for federal activity."

Each era gets an EVIDENCE_PRESENT paragraph that quantifies the change:

> "Between 1891 and 1915, the number of paid civilian federal executive branch employees grew from 157,442 to 387,294."

The section culminates with a summary of the cumulative transformation:

> "In sum, the size and scope of federal government expanded significantly over the course of the twentieth century, with particularly large upticks occurring during the Progressive Era, the Great Depression, and World War II."

**What makes this work:** Bolton and Thrower never lose sight of their variable (congressional capacity) even while narrating executive branch expansion. Every paragraph about executive growth is implicitly a paragraph about congressional challenge. The narrative is shaped by the argument, not by chronological obligation.

### Internal Structure for Historical Narratives

1. **Establish the starting point** (BASELINE_ESTABLISH) -- 1-2 paragraphs.
2. **For each era or phase:**
   - Mark the transition (TEMPORAL_TRANSITION) -- 1 paragraph.
   - Describe key institutional developments (INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE) -- 2-4 paragraphs.
   - Present supporting evidence (EVIDENCE_PRESENT) -- 1-2 paragraphs.
3. **Summarize the cumulative trajectory** -- 1 paragraph that connects the narrative to the book's argument.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Narrative Shape | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Institutional Mythos Critique | Intellectual history: traces schools of thought across centuries | Each "era" is a school of thought (Decentralists, Brandeisians, New Federalists) |
| Variable-in-History | Chronological institutional development | Clear periodization with quantitative markers at each stage |
| Policy-Regime Genesis | Path-dependent chain: each policy creates conditions for the next | The narrative is explicitly causal, with each stage constraining future options |
| Policy-Monopoly Archaeology | Detailed excavation of a single period's institutional structure | Depth over breadth; the narrative lingers in one era to show its internal logic |
| Theoretical-Historical Hybrid | Interleaved theory and history | Historical examples are deployed as evidence for theoretical claims |

## Section 3: Literature Integration

**Target length:** 1,000-2,500 words | **Paragraphs:** 4-10

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section engages existing scholarship at scale. Unlike the introduction's literature review (which identifies a gap the book fills), the context chapter's literature integration synthesizes scholarship that illuminates the historical context. It positions the author's narrative within a conversation about the period, the institution, or the variable under study.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE, CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, and REFRAME.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Worked Example: Grumbach, "The Minimalist View of States"

Grumbach structures his literature integration as a deliberate confrontation with the scholarly consensus that state politics are marginal. He opens by naming the view:

> "Three decades after Daniel Elazar (1990) predicted resurgent states in an emerging 'neodualist' era of federalism, observers point to intensifying battles over public policy at the state level. However, there has been little empirical investigation of systemic policy changes in the states over time."

He then escalates by quoting scholars whose conclusions he will challenge:

> "While 'Democrats and Republicans may disagree consistently and even violently,' Devin Caughey, Christopher Warshaw, and Yiqing Xu (2017, 1356) conclude that 'the actual policy consequences of these disagreements are far less dramatic.'"

The section closes with a pivot to his own argument:

> "So, are state-level politicians overstating their centrality to policymaking in the United States? Or is political science due for an update? In this chapter, I argue the latter."

**What makes this work:** Grumbach does not simply review the literature. He stages a confrontation between political practitioners (who claim the states matter) and political scientists (who say they don't), then sides with the practitioners on empirical grounds. The literature is organized by argument, not by chronology or author.

### Internal Structure for Literature Integration

1. **Name the dominant scholarly view** (CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM) -- 1-2 paragraphs.
2. **Engage specific scholars and findings** (SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE) -- 2-5 paragraphs.
3. **Identify the gap, limit, or complication** (REFRAME) -- 1-2 paragraphs.
4. **Position the author's contribution** -- 1 paragraph.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Literature Integration Style | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Institutional Mythos Critique | Maps three or more schools of thought, then challenges all of them | Literature IS the subject matter; the chapter critiques intellectual traditions |
| Variable-in-History | Contrasts two competing explanations for the variable's development | The literature review sets up a test between partisan and institutional accounts |
| Policy-Regime Genesis | Dismisses multiple alternative explanations before advancing the author's | Each candidate explanation (economics, capture) is evaluated and found wanting |
| Policy-Monopoly Archaeology | Draws on historical and sociological scholarship about the period | The literature is used to reconstruct the institutional environment, not to debate |
| Theoretical-Historical Hybrid | Synthesizes cross-national and comparative literature | Literature from other countries is used to generate hypotheses about the U.S. case |

## Section 4: Variable Establishment

**Target length:** 800-2,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 3-8

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section introduces and operationalizes the book's key variable. It shows how the variable developed historically, how it can be measured, and why it is analytically productive. This section is the bridge between the historical narrative (which shows what happened) and the empirical chapters (which test why it happened).

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

VARIABLE_ESTABLISH, SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE, and EVIDENCE_PRESENT.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Worked Example: Grumbach, "Measuring Policy Outcomes"

Grumbach establishes his key variable -- state policy variation and polarization -- by describing his original dataset and measurement strategy:

> "I use data on 135 major state policies to investigate two particular policy dynamics in the fifty states: increased policy variation (the substantive differences between states) and policy polarization (the relationship between party control and policy outcomes)."

He then situates his data within the existing landscape of measurement:

> "I collected data on 35 policies, to which I added data from Jordan and Grossmann 2016, Caughey and Warshaw 2016, and Boehmke and Skinner 2012 to create a data set of 135 policies."

He addresses measurement assumptions transparently:

> "This kind of measure relies on three assumptions: first, the ideological 'direction' of policy (whether it is liberal, conservative, or neither); second, that policies are of equal substantive importance; and third, that the direction and importance remain constant over time. These assumptions are unlikely to be satisfied in practice."

**What makes this work:** Grumbach does not just describe his data. He shows the reader the choices involved in measurement, the tradeoffs between approaches, and the reasons for his particular decisions. The reader finishes the section understanding not just what the variable measures but how confident they should be in the measurement.

### Internal Structure for Variable Establishment

1. **Name the variable and its components** (VARIABLE_ESTABLISH) -- 1-2 paragraphs.
2. **Describe measurement or operationalization** (VARIABLE_ESTABLISH + EVIDENCE_PRESENT) -- 1-3 paragraphs.
3. **Address assumptions, limitations, and alternatives** (SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE) -- 1-2 paragraphs.
4. **Preview the variable's empirical application** -- 1 paragraph.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Variable Establishment Style | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Institutional Mythos Critique | The "variable" is the intellectual framework itself; operationalization is secondary | Establishes the target of critique rather than a measurable variable |
| Variable-in-History | The variable is traced historically then measured; detailed operationalization | Multiple measurement approaches compared; assumptions disclosed |
| Policy-Regime Genesis | The variable is a policy regime; "measurement" is tracing its features over time | Path-dependent logic replaces conventional operationalization |
| Policy-Monopoly Archaeology | The variable is the policy monopoly; mapped through archival and institutional evidence | Operationalization comes through hearing counts, organizational data, and CC scores |
| Theoretical-Historical Hybrid | The variable is defined theoretically through axioms before empirical application | Axioms stated, then hypotheses derived, then historical evidence marshaled |

## Section 5: Evidence Presentation

**Target length:** 1,500-3,000 words | **Paragraphs:** 5-12

### What This Section Accomplishes

This section presents the chapter's core evidentiary contribution. In context chapters, evidence comes in three main forms: archival material (hearing transcripts, presidential archives, organizational records), quantitative data (employment figures, spending trends, survey responses), and documentary evidence (newspaper accounts, legislative records, organizational documents). The section shows the reader the evidence base for the historical claims made in the narrative.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

EVIDENCE_PRESENT, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, and IRONIC_OBSERVE.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Worked Example: Pettinicchio, "Agenda Stability and the Client-Service Monopoly over Disability"

Pettinicchio builds his evidence presentation around three types of data: committee composition, organizational testimony, and archival hearing transcripts. He structures the section as a series of claims, each supported by different types of evidence:

First, institutional data:

> "The House Committee on Education and Labor hosted more than half of all congressional disability-related hearings between the late 1940s and early 1960s. As the committee became more liberal, the House itself shifted."

Second, quantitative evidence in tabular form:

> "Average voting scores with and against the Conservative Coalition, 1959: House Committee on Education and Labor -- With: 29.3, Against: 64.3; Subcommittees/Special Committees on Disability -- With: 36.3, Against: 53.7."

Third, archival testimony:

> "Rep. Carl Elliot (D-KY) claimed that Barden 'could effectively choke any legislation that had a liberal smell. . . . If an aid-to-education bill seemed to be gathering some momentum, he would call in a dozen Chamber of Commerce witnesses to kill it with a filibuster.'"

**What makes this work:** Pettinicchio triangulates. No single piece of evidence carries the argument. The committee data show the institutional structure, the CC scores show the ideological constraints, and the archival testimony shows what those constraints meant in practice. Each type of evidence reinforces the others.

### Internal Structure for Evidence Presentation

1. **State the claim** -- 1 paragraph identifying what the evidence will show.
2. **Present primary evidence** (EVIDENCE_PRESENT) -- 2-4 paragraphs, with different evidence types.
3. **Interpret the evidence** -- 1-2 paragraphs connecting findings to the argument.
4. **Note ironies or paradoxes** (IRONIC_OBSERVE) -- 0-1 paragraphs, when the evidence reveals unexpected patterns.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Evidence Type | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Institutional Mythos Critique | Published scholarship and think-tank reports as primary evidence | Evidence is textual: the chapter analyzes what other people wrote |
| Variable-in-History | Legislative histories, employment statistics, budgetary data | Quantitative evidence dominates, organized chronologically |
| Policy-Regime Genesis | Presidential archives, congressional hearings, newspaper accounts | Mixed archival and documentary evidence with quantitative supplements |
| Policy-Monopoly Archaeology | Hearing transcripts, organizational data, committee records | Archival evidence dominates, with quantitative evidence on committee composition |
| Theoretical-Historical Hybrid | Cross-national data, party platform analysis, comparative evidence | Evidence spans multiple countries and time periods |

## Section 6: Forward Transition

**Target length:** 300-600 words | **Paragraphs:** 2-3

### What This Section Accomplishes

The forward transition closes the context chapter by explicitly handing off to the empirical chapters that follow. It summarizes what the context chapter has established and names the specific questions, tests, or narratives that the next chapter will pursue.

### Paragraph Functions Deployed

FORWARD_BRIDGE and, occasionally, IRONIC_OBSERVE as a closing resonance.

### How Published Authors Do It

#### Worked Example: Grumbach, End of Chapter 2

Grumbach closes his context chapter with a two-paragraph forward transition. The first paragraph summarizes the chapter's finding:

> "Yet few would argue that the American political economy is in a much better place than a generation ago. Rather than ushering in democratic responsiveness, social harmony, and economic prosperity, the shift in policymaking from the national to the state level since the 1970s has coincided with the weakening of democratic institutions, the precipitous rise of economic inequality, and growing mass polarization and discontent."

The second paragraph names what comes next:

> "The next chapter investigates the first consequence of party nationalization in American federalism: state policy resurgence. I take stock of major state policy changes over the past generation as state governments moved from the periphery back to the center of American policymaking."

**What makes this work:** The forward transition is specific. It names not just "the next chapter" but its exact subject ("state policy resurgence") and its exact method ("I take stock of major state policy changes"). The reader finishes the context chapter knowing both what has been established and what is coming.

### Internal Structure for Forward Transitions

1. **Summarize the context chapter's contribution** -- 1 paragraph.
2. **Name the next chapter's question or task** (FORWARD_BRIDGE) -- 1-2 paragraphs.
3. **Optionally, close with an irony or resonant observation** (IRONIC_OBSERVE) -- 0-1 paragraphs.

### Cluster-Specific Variations

| Cluster | Forward Transition Style | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Institutional Mythos Critique | From theoretical critique to empirical investigation | "Now that we have seen why the theory is wrong, let us see what the evidence shows" |
| Variable-in-History | From historical development to quantitative analysis | "Having traced the variable's history, we now measure its effects" |
| Policy-Regime Genesis | From policy origins to political consequences | "Having shown how the regime was created, we now examine how it shaped politics" |
| Policy-Monopoly Archaeology | From institutional stasis to disruption | "Having excavated the monopoly, we now watch it break" |
| Theoretical-Historical Hybrid | From theory development to empirical test | "Having derived the hypotheses, we now test them" |

## Complete Chapter Recipes by Cluster

The following recipes show the full section sequence with word count allocations for each cluster type.

### Recipe 1: Institutional Mythos Critique

**Model:** Grumbach (2023), "The Mythos of American Federalism"
**Total target length:** 7,000-9,000 words

| Section | Purpose | Word Count | Paragraph Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Opening Frame | Epigraphs + destabilize the received wisdom | 500-700 | CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, REFRAME |
| School 1: The Decentralists | Trace the first intellectual tradition and challenge it | 1,500-2,000 | BASELINE_ESTABLISH, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE, REFRAME |
| School 2: The Brandeisians | Trace the second intellectual tradition and challenge it | 1,200-1,500 | CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE, REFRAME |
| School 3: The New Federalists | Trace the third intellectual tradition and challenge it | 1,500-2,000 | CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, REFRAME |
| Synthesis: Federalism Today | Show the gap between promise and reality | 800-1,200 | IRONIC_OBSERVE, FORWARD_BRIDGE |
| Forward Transition | Name the empirical investigation that follows | 300-500 | FORWARD_BRIDGE |

**Key structural principle:** The chapter is organized by intellectual tradition, not by chronological period. Each tradition gets the same three-part treatment: state it fairly, engage its scholarship, then show where it fails. The synthesis section delivers the chapter's punchline: the traditions got what they wanted, and the results are troubling.

### Recipe 2: Variable-in-History

**Model:** Bolton and Thrower (2022), "'Outmanned and Outgunned'"
**Total target length:** 8,000-11,000 words

| Section | Purpose | Word Count | Paragraph Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Opening Frame | Contemporary relevance + the historical puzzle | 500-800 | CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, VARIABLE_ESTABLISH |
| Competing Explanations | Set up the two accounts to be adjudicated | 800-1,200 | SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE |
| Federal Government Expansion | Trace the growth that created the need for capacity | 2,000-2,500 | BASELINE_ESTABLISH, TEMPORAL_TRANSITION, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT |
| Congressional Policymaking Capacity | Establish the formal and informal powers | 1,500-2,000 | VARIABLE_ESTABLISH, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE |
| Evolution of Resource Capacity | Trace specific institutional reforms | 2,500-3,500 | INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, TEMPORAL_TRANSITION |
| Assessment and Forward Bridge | Evaluate the competing explanations | 500-800 | FORWARD_BRIDGE, IRONIC_OBSERVE |

**Key structural principle:** The chapter tells two parallel stories -- executive expansion and congressional response -- that converge in the assessment section. The variable (congressional capacity) is decomposed into two components (policymaking capacity and resource capacity), and each gets its own narrative treatment. The chapter adjudicates between competing explanations by weighing the historical evidence.

### Recipe 3: Policy-Regime Genesis

**Model:** SoRelle (2020), "Full Disclosure"
**Total target length:** 9,000-12,000 words

| Section | Purpose | Word Count | Paragraph Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Opening Frame | Landmark legislation + evidence of its failure | 800-1,200 | BASELINE_ESTABLISH, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, IRONIC_OBSERVE |
| The Trouble with Disclosure | Dismiss alternative explanations | 1,500-2,000 | CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, REFRAME, EVIDENCE_PRESENT |
| Policy Consequences Framework | Introduce the path-dependence framework | 600-1,000 | VARIABLE_ESTABLISH |
| Pre-Federal Period | Consumer credit before federal involvement | 800-1,200 | BASELINE_ESTABLISH, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE |
| Financing Industrialization | New credit forms in the industrial era | 1,000-1,500 | TEMPORAL_TRANSITION, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE |
| Modernizing Consumer Credit | New Deal and the creation of federal credit infrastructure | 2,000-2,500 | TEMPORAL_TRANSITION, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT |
| Post-War Developments | Credit expansion and regulatory entrenchment | 1,500-2,000 | TEMPORAL_TRANSITION, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, IRONIC_OBSERVE |
| Forward Transition | From policy origins to political consequences | 300-500 | FORWARD_BRIDGE |

**Key structural principle:** The chapter begins at the end of the story (TILA's passage in 1968), then rewinds to the beginning (colonial lending) and traces the path-dependent sequence forward. This structure creates dramatic irony: the reader knows the endpoint before they understand the path that led there. Each phase of the narrative shows how policy decisions constrained future options, building the case for path dependence through accumulated institutional detail.

### Recipe 4: Policy-Monopoly Archaeology

**Model:** Pettinicchio (2019), "It's Ability, Not Disability, That Counts"
**Total target length:** 8,000-10,000 words

| Section | Purpose | Word Count | Paragraph Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Opening Frame | Archival dialogue + the rehabilitation paradigm | 400-600 | BASELINE_ESTABLISH, EVIDENCE_PRESENT |
| Origins of Rehabilitation Policy | The Smith-Fess Act and early developments | 1,000-1,500 | INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, TEMPORAL_TRANSITION |
| Business as Usual | Institutional stasis of the 1950s | 1,200-1,500 | BASELINE_ESTABLISH, INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE |
| Institutional Stasis and Incremental Change | Committee structure and political constraints | 1,500-2,000 | INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT |
| Agenda Stability and the Policy Monopoly | How the iron triangle maintained control | 1,500-2,000 | INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE |
| The Rehabilitationists | The organizational ecosystem and its values | 1,200-1,500 | INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT, IRONIC_OBSERVE |
| Forward Transition | Seeds of disruption in the late 1950s | 300-500 | FORWARD_BRIDGE |

**Key structural principle:** The chapter is deliberately slow. It lingers in a single era (the 1940s-1950s) to show the depth and self-reinforcing logic of the policy monopoly. Every actor -- legislators, bureaucrats, nonprofit organizations -- is shown to have a stake in the existing arrangement. The chapter's payoff is that the reader understands, through accumulated institutional detail, why the monopoly persisted for so long -- and therefore why its eventual disruption (covered in subsequent chapters) was so significant. The closing FORWARD_BRIDGE plants the seeds of change (new committee members, changing demographics) without narrating the disruption itself.

### Recipe 5: Theoretical-Historical Hybrid

**Model:** Krimmel (2024), "The Dance of Clientelism and Programmaticism"
**Total target length:** 8,000-10,000 words

| Section | Purpose | Word Count | Paragraph Functions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Existing Theory Applied to the U.S. | Show what existing theories explain and what they miss | 1,500-2,000 | SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE, CONVENTIONAL_WISDOM, REFRAME |
| Supply-Side Theory | Develop the book's theoretical contribution | 2,000-2,500 | VARIABLE_ESTABLISH, SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE |
| Party Organizations | Theorize the complex role of party institutions | 1,500-2,000 | INSTITUTIONAL_DESCRIBE, REFRAME, VARIABLE_ESTABLISH |
| Racial Orders | Theorize the role of racial institutions | 1,000-1,500 | SCHOLARLY_ENGAGE, EVIDENCE_PRESENT |
| Hypotheses | State the testable predictions | 500-800 | VARIABLE_ESTABLISH, FORWARD_BRIDGE |
| Forward Transition | From theory to empirical test | 300-500 | FORWARD_BRIDGE |

**Key structural principle:** The chapter alternates between theoretical development and historical illustration. Abstract claims are immediately grounded in examples from American political history. The chapter's structure mirrors the theory itself: it begins with the existing literature's contributions, identifies what is missing (the supply side), develops that missing piece through two institutional arguments (party organizations and racial orders), and closes with testable hypotheses. The historical evidence is deployed in service of theory, not as a freestanding narrative.

## Quick-Reference Drafting Checklist

- [ ] **Opening frame:** The reader knows within 500 words what historical period or institutional arrangement the chapter will examine and why it matters for the book's argument.
- [ ] **Historical narrative:** Organized by the argument, not by chronological obligation. Every era, phase, or institution earns its presence by connecting to the book's key variable or causal mechanism.
- [ ] **Literature integration:** Organized by approach or argument, not by author. Every scholarly engagement connects back to the chapter's purpose.
- [ ] **Variable establishment:** The book's key variable is defined, decomposed, and shown in operation. The reader can distinguish high from low values.
- [ ] **Evidence presentation:** Multiple evidence types are triangulated. No evidence paragraph lacks interpretive context.
- [ ] **Forward transition:** The specific question, test, or narrative of the next chapter is named. The connection to the context chapter is explicit.

### Cross-Section Checks

- [ ] The baseline established in the opening frame is the baseline that the historical narrative then traces.
- [ ] The conventional wisdom stated early in the chapter is the same wisdom that the REFRAME paragraphs complicate.
- [ ] The variable established in the variable section is the same variable that the evidence sections document.
- [ ] The forward transition names an empirical question that could not be posed without the context chapter's groundwork.
- [ ] The chapter falls between 7,000 and 12,000 words, with the historical narrative comprising 30-50% of the total.

---

## Revision Protocol

After drafting, revise each section in this order:

1. **Forward transition first.** Write the final two paragraphs before revising anything else. These paragraphs define what the chapter needed to accomplish. If you cannot write a specific FORWARD_BRIDGE, the chapter has not yet established the necessary groundwork.
2. **Opening frame second.** Revise the opening to ensure it sets up exactly the question that the forward transition answers.
3. **Historical narrative third.** Cut any era, institution, or event that does not connect to the variable or mechanism established in the variable section. The most common problem in context chapter drafts is narrating too much -- covering history that is interesting but not necessary.
4. **Literature integration fourth.** Ensure every scholarly engagement paragraph ends by connecting to the book's argument. Move any engagement that merely surveys without evaluating to a footnote.
5. **Evidence presentation fifth.** Check that every EVIDENCE_PRESENT paragraph is sandwiched between interpretive context. Add interpretive glosses where evidence is presented without explanation.
6. **Variable establishment last.** Revise for clarity. After reading this section, could a graduate student in another subfield understand what the variable is and how it works?

At each stage, read the section aloud. If any sentence requires rereading to parse, rewrite it. Context chapters are especially prone to sentences that try to do too much -- narrating, citing, and interpreting in a single clause. Break these into two or three sentences.
