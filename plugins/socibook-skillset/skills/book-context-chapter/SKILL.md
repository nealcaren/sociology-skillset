# Book Context Chapter

You help researchers write **historical background and context chapters for academic monographs**. Given a book project memo, manuscript outline, or equivalent description of the project, you guide users through selecting a context-chapter type, structuring the chapter, and drafting publication-ready prose.

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when the researcher has:
- A defined book argument (the core thesis is known)
- A sense of the historical or institutional background the reader needs before encountering the book's main evidence
- An introduction chapter that is drafted or at least outlined
- Need to write or substantially revise a chapter that establishes the historical, institutional, or empirical preconditions for the book's argument

This skill assumes the researcher knows what the book argues. The task is crafting the chapter that gives readers the background they need to follow the argument -- while making an argument of its own.

## Overview: What a Context Chapter Does

A context chapter is not a textbook history. It performs a distinct set of functions:

- **Establishes the conditions** the reader must understand before encountering the book's main evidence
- **Makes an argument** -- 87.5% of context chapters in the corpus advance a claim, not merely narrate events
- **Provides interpretive scaffolding** so readers can evaluate the empirical chapters that follow
- **Synthesizes secondary literature** at scale, demonstrating command of the historiography
- **Sets up the key variable** -- whether independent or dependent -- that the book will analyze
- **Transitions forward** to the book's core evidentiary chapters

A context chapter's temporal scope typically exceeds the book's core empirical period. It synthesizes existing scholarship rather than presenting original findings. It establishes rather than tests the book's main claim. But the boundary between context and empirical is often blurry: some of the best context chapters also present original data.

Target length: **~12,500 words** (corpus mean). The range is wide, from ~7,000 to ~19,000.

## The Six Universal Moves

Every context chapter, regardless of cluster, must execute these six moves. They appear in 80% or more of the 8 context chapters analyzed. Their order and emphasis vary by cluster, but omitting any one produces a structurally incomplete chapter.

| # | Move | Frequency | Function | Diagnostic Question |
|---|------|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Establish the baseline or status quo ante** | 100% | Show the reader what the world looked like before the change the book examines | Does the reader understand the starting conditions against which the book's story unfolds? |
| 2 | **Trace a temporal arc** | 100% | Move the reader through time, showing how conditions changed | Does the chapter have a clear beginning, middle, and end that spans a meaningful historical period? |
| 3 | **Engage secondary literature at scale** | 100% | Synthesize existing scholarship, demonstrating command of the historiography | Does the chapter draw on a wide range of secondary sources, positioning the book within an established body of knowledge? |
| 4 | **Establish the key variable -- independent or dependent** | 87.5% | Introduce the variable (institutional, political, cultural) that the book will explain or use to explain | Does the reader leave this chapter understanding the thing the book is either explaining or explaining with? |
| 5 | **Provide an explicit forward transition** | 100% | Bridge from historical background to the book's empirical core | Does the chapter's ending clearly set up what the next chapter will do? |
| 6 | **Challenge, complicate, or reframe a received understanding** | 87.5% | Make an argument, not just narrate | Does the chapter push back against a conventional account, add nuance to a standard story, or reframe how the reader should understand the background? |

Move 6 is especially important: a context chapter that merely narrates without arguing is a textbook excerpt, not a chapter in a monograph. The corpus shows that nearly all context chapters advance a claim -- about what prior scholarship has missed, what the standard periodization gets wrong, or how the background should be reinterpreted.

## The Five Context Chapter Clusters

### Cluster 1: The Conventional-Wisdom Challenger

**Defining logic:** Identify a widely held belief about the historical background and systematically dismantle it. The chapter exists to clear intellectual ground for the book's argument by showing that what everyone thinks they know is wrong or incomplete.

**Signature features:**
- Shorter (~7,500 words)
- High analytical ratio (30/70 narrative to analytical)
- Thematic organization, not chronological
- Evidence drawn primarily from secondary literature
- Strong negative argument -- the chapter's main contribution is establishing what is NOT the case
- Few subsections organized around counter-claims rather than time periods

**Structural sequence:**
1. State the conventional wisdom clearly and charitably (500--800 words)
2. Present evidence against Claim A of the conventional wisdom (1,500--2,000 words)
3. Present evidence against Claim B (1,500--2,000 words)
4. Present evidence against Claim C (1,000--1,500 words)
5. Establish what is true instead -- the corrected baseline (500--1,000 words)
6. Forward transition: now that the ground is cleared, here is what the book will show (300--500 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 7,000--9,000 |
| Subsections | 4--6 |
| Narrative/analytical ratio | ~30/70 |
| Evidence type | Secondary literature |
| Temporal organization | Thematic, not chronological |
| Closing move | Corrected baseline + forward transition |

**When to use:** The book's argument depends on the reader first unlearning something. Existing scholarship or public understanding of the background is wrong in ways that would distort interpretation of the evidence. The book makes a revisionist claim that requires intellectual ground-clearing before the positive argument can begin.

**Exemplar:** Grumbach, *Laboratories against Democracy*, Chapter 2 -- dismantles conventional wisdom about American federalism by organizing the chapter around claims to be refuted rather than a chronological narrative, using secondary literature to build a strong negative case that clears the way for the book's argument about state-level democratic erosion.

---

### Cluster 2: The Institutional Landscape Mapper

**Defining logic:** Document the institutional configuration that constitutes the status quo the book analyzes. Trace how a policy regime, organizational field, or institutional arrangement developed from its origins through consolidation, producing a detailed map of the landscape the book's actors inhabit.

**Signature features:**
- Long (~15,000--19,000 words)
- Moderate narrative/analytical ratio (~55/45)
- Rich visual apparatus (tables, figures, timelines)
- Mixed evidence: hearings, organizational records, legislative histories, quantitative data
- Traces a policy regime from origins through consolidation
- Temporal scope: focused 20--40 year core period
- Many subsections (10--15+), often organized chronologically within thematic blocks

**Structural sequence:**
1. Establish the puzzle or paradox that the institutional landscape presents (500--1,000 words)
2. Origins: how the institutional arrangement began (2,000--3,000 words)
3. Development: key legislative and organizational milestones (3,000--5,000 words)
4. Consolidation: how the regime stabilized (2,000--3,000 words)
5. Consequences: what the institutional landscape produces (2,000--3,000 words)
6. Tensions or contradictions within the regime (1,000--2,000 words)
7. Forward transition: from landscape to the book's empirical cases (500--1,000 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 14,000--19,000 |
| Subsections | 10--15+ |
| Narrative/analytical ratio | ~55/45 |
| Evidence type | Mixed (hearings, org records, legislative histories, some quantitative) |
| Temporal organization | Chronological within thematic blocks |
| Visual apparatus | Tables, figures, timelines |
| Closing move | Forward transition to empirical core |

**When to use:** The book examines actors operating within a complex institutional environment that the reader must understand to follow the analysis. The policy regime or organizational field has a developmental history that shapes the dynamics the book studies. The book's argument is about how institutions constrain, enable, or channel action.

**Exemplars:** Pettinicchio, *Politics of Empowerment*, Chapter 2 -- maps the disability policy landscape from its legislative origins through consolidation, using hearings, organizational records, and legislative histories to document the institutional configuration that disability advocates navigated. SoRelle, *Democracy Declined*, Chapter 2 -- traces the consumer credit policy regime from its origins through its consolidation into a system that channeled political engagement through individualized financial complaint rather than collective mobilization.

---

### Cluster 3: The Historical Hypothesis Tester

**Defining logic:** Use historical evidence to test a causal hypothesis. The chapter does double duty: it provides necessary background AND contributes to the book's empirical strategy. It sits at the boundary between context and evidence, using the historical record as a testing ground for causal claims.

**Signature features:**
- Long (~16,000 words)
- Balanced narrative/analytical ratio (~45--50/50--55)
- Mixed qualitative-quantitative evidence, including regression analysis
- Dual purpose: provides context AND advances the identification strategy
- Data visualizations alongside archival evidence
- Subsections organized around hypotheses or causal mechanisms, not just time periods
- May include original datasets

**Structural sequence:**
1. State the historical hypothesis to be tested (500--1,000 words)
2. Historical narrative establishing the conditions (2,000--3,000 words)
3. Evidence for/against Hypothesis A (2,000--3,000 words)
4. Evidence for/against Hypothesis B (2,000--3,000 words)
5. Quantitative analysis (regressions, descriptive statistics) (2,000--3,000 words)
6. Assessment: what the historical evidence shows (1,000--1,500 words)
7. Forward transition: how this finding shapes the book's remaining analysis (500--1,000 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 14,000--18,000 |
| Subsections | 8--12 |
| Narrative/analytical ratio | ~45--50/50--55 |
| Evidence type | Mixed qual-quant (archival + regressions) |
| Temporal organization | Chronological backbone with analytical digressions |
| Visual apparatus | Data visualizations, regression tables |
| Closing move | Assessment + forward transition |

**When to use:** The book's argument depends on establishing a causal relationship in the historical period that sets up the conditions for the book's main empirical analysis. The historical evidence is rich enough to support hypothesis testing, not just narration. The book uses a research design where historical variation provides the identification strategy.

**Exemplars:** Krimmel, *The Lives and Deaths of American Political Parties*, Chapter 3 -- uses historical evidence including regression analysis to test hypotheses about party formation, sitting at the boundary between context and empirical chapters. Bolton and Thrower, *Checks in the Balance*, Chapter 3 -- tests causal claims about congressional-executive relations using historical evidence combined with quantitative analysis, providing both the institutional background and part of the book's empirical argument.

---

### Cluster 4: The Genealogical Excavation

**Defining logic:** Trace the deep historical roots of a contemporary category, institution, or practice. Excavate the origins of something the reader takes for granted, showing how it was constructed over a long historical arc. The chapter denaturalizes by historicizing.

**Signature features:**
- Moderate length (~8,000--11,000 words)
- Higher narrative ratio (~50--60/40--50)
- Few subsections (3--4 major sections), each covering a broad historical period
- Evidence drawn from secondary literature plus cultural sources (legal texts, media, intellectual history)
- Very broad temporal scope (colonial era to present, or equivalent long arc)
- Uses a structuring metaphor or conceptual thread to unify the long sweep
- Emphasizes contingency -- showing that what exists now was not inevitable

**Structural sequence:**
1. Introduce the category or practice to be excavated; frame with a structuring metaphor (500--1,000 words)
2. Deep origins: earliest historical roots (2,000--3,000 words)
3. Transformation: how the category changed in a key transitional period (2,000--3,000 words)
4. Modern consolidation: how the contemporary form took shape (2,000--3,000 words)
5. Forward transition: from genealogy to the book's empirical present (500--1,000 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 8,000--11,000 |
| Subsections | 3--4 major |
| Narrative/analytical ratio | ~50--60/40--50 |
| Evidence type | Secondary literature + cultural sources |
| Temporal scope | Very broad (colonial to present, or equivalent) |
| Organization | Chronological with conceptual thread |
| Closing move | Bridge from deep history to book's empirical period |

**When to use:** The book examines a category, classification, or practice that readers treat as natural or self-evident. The argument depends on showing that this thing was historically constructed and contingent. The book's contribution includes denaturalizing something the reader takes for granted. The relevant history spans a very long period.

**Exemplars:** Rodriguez-Muniz, *Figures of the Future*, Chapter 1 -- traces the deep historical roots of census racial categories from colonial enumeration through contemporary debates, using a structuring metaphor to unify a sweep from the colonial era to the present. Vogler, *Sorting Sexualities*, Chapter 1 -- excavates the genealogy of state-based sexuality classification from its earliest legal roots through its modern administrative form, denaturalizing categories that readers might take as given.

---

### Cluster 5: The Phenomenon Establisher

**Defining logic:** Document the empirical phenomenon the book will explain. Before asking "why does X happen?" the chapter establishes that X does in fact happen, using original data and visualization to make the phenomenon visible and measurable.

**Signature features:**
- Long (~13,000 words)
- Very high analytical ratio (20/80 narrative to analytical)
- Heavy quantitative evidence, including original datasets
- Multiple data visualizations (figures, tables, maps)
- Blurs the line between context and empirical chapters
- Subsections organized around different dimensions or measures of the phenomenon
- Reads more like a social science article's descriptive section than a historical narrative

**Structural sequence:**
1. State what needs to be established: the phenomenon to be explained (500--1,000 words)
2. Define and operationalize: what counts as an instance of the phenomenon (1,000--1,500 words)
3. Descriptive evidence, Dimension A (2,000--3,000 words with visualizations)
4. Descriptive evidence, Dimension B (2,000--3,000 words with visualizations)
5. Descriptive evidence, Dimension C (2,000--3,000 words with visualizations)
6. Synthesis: what the descriptive portrait reveals (1,000--1,500 words)
7. Forward transition: now that the phenomenon is established, the book will explain it (500--1,000 words)

| Benchmark | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Word count | 11,000--15,000 |
| Subsections | 8--12 |
| Narrative/analytical ratio | ~20/80 |
| Evidence type | Heavy quantitative, original datasets |
| Visual apparatus | Multiple figures, tables, maps |
| Organization | By dimension or measure, not chronology |
| Closing move | Synthesis + forward transition |

**When to use:** The book explains a phenomenon that the reader may not know exists, or may not appreciate the full scope of. The phenomenon is measurable and the book has original data to describe it. The "that" must be established before the "why" can be addressed. The book has a quantitative or mixed-methods design.

**Exemplar:** Grumbach, *Laboratories against Democracy*, Chapter 3 -- establishes the phenomenon of state-level democratic erosion using original datasets and multiple data visualizations, documenting the empirical pattern that the rest of the book will explain. The chapter reads more as a descriptive empirical chapter than a historical narrative, but functions structurally as context by establishing the dependent variable.

## Opening Move Taxonomy

The corpus reveals five distinct opening types. Choose based on your cluster.

| Type | Description | Frequency | Typical Clusters |
|------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Puzzle Opening** | Open with an empirical puzzle, surprising pattern, or counterintuitive observation | 37.5% | Historical Hypothesis Tester, Phenomenon Establisher |
| **Anecdote-to-Scholarly-Correction** | Open with a vivid anecdote, then pivot to show how the standard scholarly account misses what matters | 25% | Institutional Landscape Mapper, Phenomenon Establisher |
| **Conventional-Wisdom Challenge** | Open by naming the thing everyone believes and flagging that it is wrong | 12.5% | Conventional-Wisdom Challenger |
| **Theoretical-Framework Opening** | Open by establishing the conceptual lens through which the history will be read | 12.5% | Genealogical Excavation |
| **Historical-Narrative Opening** | Open in media res with a historical scene or moment | 12.5% | Genealogical Excavation, Institutional Landscape Mapper |

The Puzzle Opening is the most common (37.5%), followed by the Anecdote-to-Scholarly-Correction (25%). Together they account for nearly two-thirds of context chapter openings. The remaining three types appear once each in the corpus.

**Exemplars:**
- **Puzzle Opening:** SoRelle opens by noting a surprising pattern in how Americans engaged with consumer credit policy; Bolton and Thrower open with the puzzle of congressional capacity; Krimmel opens with the puzzle of party formation.
- **Anecdote-to-Scholarly-Correction:** Grumbach Chapter 3 opens with an anecdote that leads to a correction of how scholars have measured democratic health; Pettinicchio opens with a scene that reveals the gap in how disability policy is understood.
- **Conventional-Wisdom Challenge:** Grumbach Chapter 2 opens by naming the conventional wisdom about federalism and flagging that it is wrong.
- **Theoretical-Framework Opening:** Rodriguez-Muniz opens by establishing the conceptual apparatus for reading census history.
- **Historical-Narrative Opening:** Vogler opens in media res with a historical scene of sexuality classification.

## Closing Move Taxonomy

| Type | Description | Frequency | Typical Clusters |
|------|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Explicit Handoff** | Directly set up the next chapter, often naming what it will do and what evidence it will use | 62.5% | All clusters |
| **Ironic/Paradoxical Summation** | Conclude with an irony or paradox that encapsulates the chapter's argument and creates momentum | 25% | Conventional-Wisdom Challenger, Phenomenon Establisher |
| **Synthesis-with-Qualification** | Summarize the chapter's findings while acknowledging complexity, then transition | 12.5% | Historical Hypothesis Tester |

The Explicit Handoff dominates (62.5%), reflecting the context chapter's structural position: it exists to set up what comes next. The Ironic/Paradoxical Summation is the second most common (25%), typically found when the chapter has been building toward a counterintuitive conclusion. The Synthesis-with-Qualification appears in chapters at the context-empirical boundary.

**Exemplars:**
- **Explicit Handoff:** Bolton and Thrower end by directly introducing what the next chapter will test; SoRelle bridges from the policy regime's history to the book's case studies; Vogler transitions from genealogy to the book's empirical period; Pettinicchio hands off from the institutional landscape to the actors navigating it; Rodriguez-Muniz bridges from deep history to the contemporary period.
- **Ironic/Paradoxical Summation:** Grumbach Chapter 2 concludes with the irony that federalism, celebrated as democratic, enabled democratic erosion; Grumbach Chapter 3 concludes with the paradox that the phenomenon is both more severe and more systematic than prior accounts suggested.
- **Synthesis-with-Qualification:** Krimmel synthesizes the historical evidence while qualifying the strength of the causal claims, then transitions to the book's more focused empirical analysis.

## Cluster Decision Tree

Use this tree to select the right cluster. Work through each question in order.

### Question 1: Does the book need the reader to understand a historical trajectory spanning the whole book?

- **No** -- You probably do not need a standalone context chapter. Consider integrating background into empirical chapters or the introduction.
- **Yes** -- Continue to Question 2.

### Question 2: What is the primary function of this chapter?

- **Challenge an intellectual tradition or dismantle a widely held belief** --> **Conventional-Wisdom Challenger**
- **Document an institutional equilibrium or policy regime** --> **Institutional Landscape Mapper**
- **Test a historical hypothesis using the historical record** --> **Historical Hypothesis Tester**
- **Recover deep cultural, discursive, or categorical roots** --> **Genealogical Excavation**
- **Establish the empirical phenomenon to be explained** --> **Phenomenon Establisher**

### Question 3: Confirming your selection

If the answer to Question 2 is clear, proceed with that cluster. If the chapter serves two functions, consider:

- Does it present original quantitative data? If yes, it leans toward **Phenomenon Establisher** or **Historical Hypothesis Tester**.
- Does it span more than a century? If yes, it leans toward **Genealogical Excavation**.
- Does it focus on a 20--40 year institutional development? If yes, it leans toward **Institutional Landscape Mapper**.
- Is its primary contribution negative (showing what is wrong) rather than positive (showing what happened)? If yes, it leans toward **Conventional-Wisdom Challenger**.

### Question 4: Does the book need one context chapter or two?

In the corpus, 5/8 books use a single context chapter; 1/8 (Grumbach) uses two. A second context chapter is warranted when:

- The book needs both intellectual ground-clearing AND empirical landscape documentation (the functions are too different to combine)
- One chapter handles the received wisdom, the other documents the phenomenon

If one chapter can serve the function, prefer one. A double context sequence risks delaying the empirical evidence unacceptably.

### Quick Heuristic

| If the chapter's main verb is... | Then choose... |
|----------------------------------|---------------|
| "Dismantles" or "corrects" | Conventional-Wisdom Challenger |
| "Maps" or "traces an institution" | Institutional Landscape Mapper |
| "Tests" or "evaluates a hypothesis" | Historical Hypothesis Tester |
| "Excavates" or "genealogizes" | Genealogical Excavation |
| "Documents" or "measures the phenomenon" | Phenomenon Establisher |

## Architecture Decisions: Does the Book Need a Context Chapter?

Not every book needs a standalone context chapter. The corpus reveals three configurations:

| Configuration | Frequency | When It Works |
|---------------|-----------|---------------|
| **Single context chapter** | 5/8 books | The default. The book needs one chapter of historical, institutional, or empirical background before the main evidence. |
| **Double context chapters** | 1/8 books | When both intellectual ground-clearing AND empirical landscape documentation are needed, and the two functions are too distinct to combine. |
| **No context chapter** | 3/8 books | When the book is short and focused; when case-study chapters carry their own context; or when a theory chapter absorbs the literature engagement. |

**Markers that a standalone context chapter is needed:**
- The book's temporal scope exceeds what any single empirical chapter covers
- The reader needs to understand an institutional environment, policy regime, or historical trajectory before encountering the evidence
- The book challenges a received account that must be dismantled before the positive argument begins
- The dependent or independent variable requires historical establishment

**Markers that a context chapter is NOT needed:**
- The book is short and focused enough that empirical chapters can carry their own background
- Case studies naturally incorporate their own historical context
- A theory chapter already handles the relevant literature engagement
- The introduction adequately establishes the background

## Context-Empirical Boundary

Context chapters and empirical chapters sometimes blur together. Use these markers to determine what you are actually writing:

**Markers of a true context chapter:**
- Temporal scope exceeds the book's core empirical period
- Synthesizes secondary literature rather than presenting original findings
- Establishes rather than tests the book's main claim
- Provides scaffolding for interpretation of later chapters

**Markers of an empirical chapter dressed as context:**
- Contains original data analysis
- Introduces methodological innovation
- Produces findings that are cited in later chapters as evidence for the argument
- Would leave a gap in the book's evidentiary chain if removed

This is not a bright line. The Historical Hypothesis Tester and Phenomenon Establisher clusters both straddle the boundary. If a chapter sits at the boundary, acknowledge this openly: note that it does double duty, providing both context and evidence. Do not mislabel an empirical chapter as "background" -- doing so undersells its contribution and confuses readers about the book's evidential architecture.

## Anti-Patterns

Avoid these failure modes. They are derived from what the corpus consistently does NOT do.

### 1. The textbook history

A context chapter that narrates "the history of X" without making an argument has failed its genre function. In the corpus, 87.5% of context chapters advance a claim -- about what prior scholarship has missed, what the conventional wisdom gets wrong, or how the historical record should be reinterpreted. A chapter that is merely informative belongs in an encyclopedia, not a monograph.

**Fix:** Identify the argumentative thread. What does this chapter claim that is not already obvious? If the answer is "nothing," the chapter needs restructuring around a claim.

### 2. The introduction echo

A context chapter that repeats what the introduction already said -- same literature, same framing, same level of detail -- wastes the reader's time and a chapter's worth of real estate. The introduction previews; the context chapter develops at length.

**Fix:** If the introduction already engaged a literature, the context chapter should go deeper, present new evidence, or organize the material around a different question. The two chapters should be complementary, not redundant.

### 3. The missing forward transition

A context chapter that simply stops -- ending with a summary of what it covered rather than setting up what comes next -- breaks the book's momentum. In the corpus, 100% of context chapters include an explicit forward transition. The context chapter's structural purpose is to prepare the reader for the empirical chapters; it must hand the reader off.

**Fix:** The final section (300--1,000 words) should explicitly name what the next chapter does, what evidence it uses, and how the background just provided will matter for interpreting it.

### 4. The overlong delay

A context chapter so long that it delays the book's empirical evidence unacceptably. If the reader reaches page 100 and has not yet encountered the book's original contribution, the context chapter has overstayed its welcome. The longest chapters in the corpus (~19,000 words) earn their length through rich evidence and visual apparatus, not through padded narration.

**Fix:** If the chapter exceeds 16,000 words, audit every section for necessity. Can any material be integrated into empirical chapters instead? Can the chapter be tightened by cutting background that the reader can reasonably be expected to know?

### 5. The mislabeled empirical chapter

A chapter that presents original data analysis, runs regressions, introduces a new dataset, or produces findings cited later in the book is not merely "context" -- it is an empirical chapter that also provides background. Mislabeling it undersells the contribution and confuses the book's architecture.

**Fix:** If the chapter contains original analysis, acknowledge its dual function. Position it honestly in the chapter roadmap: "Chapter 2 provides the historical background for the book's argument and, in doing so, offers the first test of the causal mechanism."

### 6. The neutral backdrop

A context chapter that treats history as a neutral backdrop -- presenting "just the facts" without interpretation or argument -- misunderstands the genre. In the corpus, 87.5% of context chapters take a position. They argue that the standard periodization is wrong, that scholars have overlooked a key institutional development, that the phenomenon is more severe than recognized, or that a category everyone treats as natural was historically constructed.

**Fix:** Ask: what does this chapter argue? If the answer is only "here is what happened," add an argumentative layer. Context chapters in monographs are not neutral; they are strategic deployments of background that serve the book's argument.

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Cluster Selection & Architecture

**Goal:** Read the user's project description, select the right cluster, and map the structural sequence before any drafting begins.

**Steps:**
1. Read the book project memo, outline, or equivalent
2. Ask the user to confirm: What is the core argument? What background does the reader need? What is the relationship between the historical material and the book's evidence?
3. Determine whether the book needs a context chapter at all (see Architecture Decisions above)
4. Walk through the decision tree with the user
5. Select a primary cluster
6. Identify any elements to borrow from adjacent clusters
7. Map the structural sequence with approximate word counts per section
8. Determine:
   - Opening type (puzzle, anecdote-to-correction, conventional-wisdom challenge, theoretical framework, historical narrative)
   - Narrative/analytical ratio target
   - Temporal scope (focused 20--40 year core vs. broad colonial-to-present sweep)
   - Evidence types (secondary lit only, mixed, quantitative)
   - Closing move (explicit handoff, ironic summation, synthesis-with-qualification)
   - Target word count (use cluster benchmarks)

**Pause:** Confirm cluster selection and structural plan before drafting.

---

### Phase 2: Drafting

**Goal:** Draft each section of the context chapter following the selected cluster's sequence, applying word count targets.

**Steps:**
1. Draft the opening. The first 500--1,000 words must accomplish one of the five opening types identified for your cluster. The opening should signal that the chapter has an argument, not just a topic.
2. Draft each subsequent section in the cluster's prescribed order, following the word count targets from the structural plan.
3. Maintain the target narrative/analytical ratio throughout. If the cluster prescribes 50/50, alternate between narrative passages (telling what happened) and analytical passages (interpreting what it means).
4. Integrate citations heavily throughout. Context chapters are the most densely cited chapters in most monographs. Every major claim should be sourced; every historiographic debate should be attributed.
5. Draft the forward transition. The final section (300--1,000 words) must explicitly set up the next chapter.
6. Verify all six universal moves are present:
   - [ ] Baseline or status quo ante established
   - [ ] Temporal arc traced
   - [ ] Secondary literature engaged at scale
   - [ ] Key variable (independent or dependent) established
   - [ ] Explicit forward transition provided
   - [ ] Received understanding challenged, complicated, or reframed

**Pause:** Review the full draft against the structural plan. Check word counts per section.

---

### Phase 3: Calibration & Revision

**Goal:** Test the draft against cluster benchmarks and the six universal moves. Revise as needed.

**Calibration checks:**

| Test | Pass Criteria |
|------|---------------|
| **Total word count** | Within cluster range (see cluster benchmark tables) |
| **Subsection count** | Within cluster range |
| **Narrative/analytical ratio** | Within cluster target (estimate by classifying paragraphs as primarily narrative or primarily analytical) |
| **Citation density** | Heavy throughout; no multi-paragraph stretches without citations |
| **Opening type** | Matches cluster prescription |
| **Closing type** | Matches cluster prescription |
| **Temporal scope** | Appropriate to cluster (focused vs. broad sweep) |

**Universal move tests:**

| Test | Question | Fix if Failing |
|------|----------|---------------|
| **Baseline test** | Does the reader understand the starting conditions? | Add an early section establishing what the world looked like before the change the book examines |
| **Arc test** | Does the chapter move through time with a clear trajectory? | Restructure around a chronological backbone; add temporal signposts |
| **Literature test** | Does the chapter draw on a wide range of secondary sources? | Add historiographic engagement; attribute debates and interpretations |
| **Variable test** | Does the reader leave understanding the key variable? | Add a section explicitly defining and establishing the variable the book will explain or explain with |
| **Transition test** | Does the chapter's ending set up what comes next? | Add a closing section (300--1,000 words) that names the next chapter's task and evidence |
| **Argument test** | Does the chapter make a claim, not just narrate? | Identify the argumentative thread and make it explicit; add interpretive passages that tell the reader what the history means, not just what happened |

**Revision priorities** (in order):
1. Argument -- if the chapter merely narrates without claiming, it is a textbook excerpt
2. Forward transition -- if the chapter does not set up the empirical core, it is structurally disconnected
3. Baseline establishment -- if the reader does not understand the starting conditions, the book's evidence will be uninterpretable
4. Literature engagement -- if the chapter does not synthesize existing scholarship, it lacks authority
5. Narrative/analytical balance -- if the ratio is off, recalibrate by adding or cutting narrative or analytical passages
6. Word count -- trim or expand to match cluster benchmarks
